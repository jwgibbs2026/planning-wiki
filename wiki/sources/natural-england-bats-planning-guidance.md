# Natural England — Bats Planning Guidance

**Source type**: Standing advice (material planning consideration)
**Publisher**: Natural England
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/bats-surveys-and-mitigation-for-development-projects
**Ingested**: 2026-06-10

---

## Summary

This is Natural England's standing advice for local planning authorities (LPAs) assessing development proposals that could affect bats in England. As standing advice, it carries significant weight as a **material planning consideration** — LPAs are expected to apply it when determining applications.

All bat species are [[European Protected Species]] under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017, giving them the highest tier of legal protection available in England. The guidance sets out when surveys are required, how impacts should be assessed, what mitigation is expected, and how the licensing system interacts with the planning process.

---

## Key Points

### Legal Status
- All bats are European Protected Species (EPS) — the strongest legal protection in England
- Prohibited activities include deliberately killing, injuring, disturbing, or capturing bats, and damaging or destroying roosts
- Additional protections under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 cover disturbance in occupied shelters and obstruction of roost access
- Several bat species are listed as [[Species of Principal Importance]] under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006

### Survey Triggers
- Surveys are required whenever development **could** affect bat roosts, foraging habitat, or commuting habitat
- **Absence of records does not indicate absence of bats** — the precautionary principle applies
- Key triggers include: buildings with roosting potential, ancient or veteran trees, water bodies, woodland, and proposed lighting near bat habitat
- Wind turbines within 50 metres of bat habitat require survey

### Mitigation Hierarchy
The guidance applies a sequential avoidance-first approach:
1. **Avoidance** — redesign to preserve roosts; adjust timing to avoid maternity/hibernation seasons
2. **Mitigation** — new roosting features within structures; dark commuting corridors; insect-rich habitat enhancement; lighting redesign
3. **Compensation** — like-for-like roost replacement where destruction is unavoidable; no net loss of roosts

### Licensing
- Developers may need an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] from Natural England
- LPAs must be satisfied that a licence **will** be granted before approving development — they cannot simply leave this to chance
- This creates a procedural obligation: LPAs must assess licence likelihood as part of their decision-making

### Biodiversity Enhancement
- Beyond compliance, developers should be encouraged to create or enhance on-site habitats
- Enhancements should align with relevant nature conservation plans, including the Local Nature Recovery Strategy and Biodiversity Action Plan

## Relevance to Gloucestershire

Bats are the most commonly encountered European Protected Species in Gloucestershire planning applications. The county's mix of ancient woodland, veteran trees, water meadows, and historic buildings creates widespread bat habitat. Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds bat distribution data that should be consulted when assessing survey triggers.

Communities and parish councils can use this guidance to challenge planning applications where bat surveys have not been requested — the precautionary principle means developers cannot avoid survey obligations simply by citing a lack of existing records.
