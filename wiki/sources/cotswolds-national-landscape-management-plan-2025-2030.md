# Cotswolds National Landscape Management Plan 2025–2030

**Type:** Statutory management plan
**Published:** February 2025 (adopted by CNL Board)
**Legal basis:** Section 89, Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000 (as amended by NERC Act 2006)
**Produced by:** [[Cotswolds National Landscape Board]]

---

## What this document is

The Cotswolds National Landscape Management Plan 2025–2030 is the sixth statutory five-year management plan for the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] (CNL) — the largest Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty (AONB) in England. It is a **material consideration in planning decisions** affecting the CNL and its setting. Under the Levelling Up and Regeneration Act 2023 (s.245), relevant authorities now have a strengthened "seek to further" duty in relation to the plan's purposes.

The plan covers 787 square miles (2,038 km²) across 15 local authority areas, with [[Gloucestershire County Council]] covering 63.7% of the CNL area and Cotswolds District Council covering 43.8%.

---

## Structure

The plan is organised around:
- **Vision** — the long-term aspiration for the CNL
- **Three key issues** — climate emergency, nature's decline, health and societal change
- **14 outcomes** — the desired results across all themes
- **19 policies** — grouped under three headings:
  - Cross-cutting themes (CC1–CC4)
  - Conserving and enhancing (CE1–CE16)
  - Understanding and enjoyment (UE1–UE3)
- **10 appendices** — including designation history, natural beauty criteria, Board structure, "seek to further" duty, stakeholder delivery, PLTOF monitoring indicators, net zero pathway, priority habitats and species, BNG rationale, and major development guidance

**Policy verb convention:** "must" = statutory requirement; "will" = Board responsibility; "should" = other stakeholder responsibility.

## Key policies summary

### Cross-cutting themes

**CC1 — Climate change mitigation:** Carbon sequestration through land management; tree, woodland, and hedgerow creation; cover cropping; wildflower grassland restoration; reducing synthetic fertilisers; retrofitting buildings; low-carbon energy generation consistent with AONB designation; active travel and EV charging; community food networks. PLTOF Target 6 (net zero by 2050) and Target 8 (tree canopy +4.59% / 9,367 Ha by 2050) apply.

**CC2 — Climate change adaptation:** All new development and infrastructure must treat adaptation as a driver; land management and ELM schemes must include adaptation measures. CNL Climate Adaptation Plan expected by end of summer 2025.

**CC3 — CRoW Act s.85 compliance:** Relevant authorities must seek to further the conservation and enhancement of the natural beauty of the CNL. The [[Seek to Further Duty]] has been in force since 26 December 2023. Only 10% of planning decisions in 2024 explicitly referred to it; only 6% adequately addressed it.

**CC4 — Working in partnership:** Coordinated action across 15 local authorities on planning, ELM, nature recovery, and tourism.

### Conserving and enhancing

**CE1 — Landscape:** Proposals must be compatible with and seek to further landscape character. Presumption against loss of key characteristics. Rural skills (dry stone walling, stonemasonry, hedge laying) to be supported.

**CE2 — Geology and geomorphology:** 36 geological SSSIs and 85+ Regionally Important Geological and Geomorphological Sites (RIGS) in the CNL. Oolitic limestone band; tufa streams and springs.

**CE3 — Natural and cultural capital:** Ecosystem services framework; natural capital accounting.

**CE4 — Local distinctiveness:** Limestone building material; design guides; limestone quarrying at appropriate scale with conditions; quarry restoration to contribute to nature recovery.

**CE5 — Tranquillity:** Noise and visual disturbance minimised. A 10% traffic increase is a rule-of-thumb trigger for significance. Drones are an emerging threat.

**CE6 — Dark skies:** Dark skies are a special quality of the CNL. The area of darkest skies has diminished by more than 40% since 1993. Four forms of light pollution: sky glow, glare, light trespass, and presence. Formal dark sky designation is being considered. Cotswolds District Council ranks 13th for dark sky quality in England.

**CE7 — Historic environment and cultural heritage:** Heritage and Cultural Heritage Impact Assessments required. PLTOF Target 10: reduce heritage assets at risk from 62 (2024 baseline) to 52 by 2030 and 7 by 2050.

**CE8 — Nature recovery and biodiversity:** Requires **20% Biodiversity Net Gain** for development in the CNL — double the national mandatory minimum of 10%. Mitigation hierarchy applies (avoid, mitigate, compensate). Irreplaceable habitats defined as: ancient/veteran trees, ancient woodland (continuously wooded since 1600, including PAWS), ancient unimproved grassland (since 1945), and ancient hedgerows (pre-Enclosure Acts). Six LNRSs are coincident with the CNL. Current wildlife-rich habitat: 48,000 Ha (23%); target: 82,000 Ha (40%) by 2050.

**CE9 — Water:** SuDS required; RIBA 2030 Climate Challenge water efficiency standards; Grampian conditions for sewerage capacity; phosphorus treatment targets (0.25 mg/l at sewage treatment works; good status 0.05–0.075 mg/l); 10m undeveloped buffer zone adjacent to watercourses; natural flood management. None of the CNL's rivers achieve good chemical status; only 11.7% achieve good ecological status.

**CE10 — Farming and land management:** 87% of CNL land is agricultural (49% arable, 43% grassland). Farming worth ~£800m/year; tourism ~£1bn/year. 'Right Tree, Right Place, Right Reason' principle. 38% of CNL woodland (10,158 ha) is unmanaged; 35% of ancient woodland (9,218 ha) is unmanaged. ELM schemes are the most important single mechanism for nature recovery in the wider countryside.

**CE11 — Problem species, pests and diseases:** Grey squirrel and deer population control at landscape scale; ash dieback management; invasive non-native species (American Signal Crayfish, Himalayan Balsam, New Zealand Pygmy Weed, Holm oak, Cotoneaster). Pine marten recolonisation/reintroduction considered as grey squirrel control tool.

**CE12 — Soils:** Halt and reverse soil degradation. Five principles: minimise disturbance, maintain living roots, increase biodiversity, mitigate compaction, integrate livestock. Arable soils have lost 40–60% of organic carbon. Soil degradation costs £1.2bn/year (2010 estimate).

**CE13 — Development and infrastructure principles:** Landscape-led approach; 7-point design criteria; cumulative impact assessment. CNL has the highest level of protection in the planning system, equivalent to National Parks.

**CE14 — Major development:** NPPF paragraphs 190–191 and footnote 67 apply. Not a simple planning balance — must refuse unless exceptional circumstances and public interest criteria are met. Case law: *R (Mevagissey Parish Council) v Cornwall Council* [2013]; *R (Advearse) v Dorset Council v Hallam Land Management Ltd* [2020].

**CE15 — Development priorities and evidence of need:** Housing should focus on affordable housing (especially social rented/perpetuity). At least 50% affordable in market developments; 100% affordable on Rural Exception Sites (minimum 75% in exceptional circumstances). Standard method housing need may not be fully met in the CNL. House price affordability ratio in CNL is 11x (England: 8x).

**CE16 — Waste management and circular economy:** Waste hierarchy applies. No new landfill or strategic waste facilities normally permitted.

### Understanding and enjoyment

**UE1 — Health and wellbeing:** Green/blue spaces, walking/cycling/riding routes, disabled access, environmental education, green prescribing.

**UE2 — Access and recreation:** 3,000 miles of public rights of way. Sandford Principle applies (conservation over enjoyment when purposes conflict). SANG provision for designated site protection.

**UE3 — Sustainable tourism:** Tourism compatible with conservation. 'Caring for the Cotswolds' visitor giving scheme. Visitors' emissions equivalent to ~half of residents' emissions (48% flying, 41% road fuel).

## Priority habitats and species (Appendix 8)

Adapted from S41 NERC Act 2006 with CNL-specific additions:

**Habitats:** Lowland mixed deciduous woodland; lowland beech and yew woodland; wood pasture/parkland/veteran trees mosaic; wildflower-rich grasslands (calcareous, meadows, neutral); flushes, streams and rivers; arable field margins; hedgerows; natural box scrub; batscapes.

**Species:** Farmland birds (skylark, lapwing, corn bunting); pasqueflower; pearl-bordered fritillary; arable plants; juniper; Cotswold pennycress; bats; dormouse; water vole; brown hare; limestone grassland butterflies; marsh fritillary; violet click beetle; white-clawed crayfish; native brown trout; Bath asparagus; common box; rockrose pot beetle; rugged oil beetle; ancient woodland ground flora (helleborines, angular Solomon's seal); ancient grassland flora (musk orchid, fly orchid).

## Governance and delivery

The [[Cotswolds National Landscape Board]] (legal name: Cotswolds Conservation Board) is the statutory body responsible for the plan. It has 37 members (15 nominated by principal local authorities, 8 by parish councils, 14 appointed by government). It does not own or directly manage any land in the CNL. It is supported by the Cotswold Voluntary Wardens (497 active volunteers, 52,971 hours in 2023/24).

Delivery is monitored through the [[Protected Landscapes Targets and Outcomes Framework]] and local indicators, reported in the 'State of the Cotswolds Report'. 10 of 15 LPAs (76%) have adopted Local Plans with policy-level reference to the CNL Management Plan.

## Gloucestershire context

The CNL is highly significant for Gloucestershire planning. [[Gloucestershire County Council]] covers 63.7% of the CNL; Cotswolds District Council covers 43.8%; Tewkesbury Borough Council covers 8.5%; Stroud District Council covers 10.9%; Cheltenham Borough Council covers 0.5%. The plan is a material consideration in all planning decisions affecting the CNL and its setting. Parish and town councils within the CNL are asked to use the Management Plan when preparing Neighbourhood Development Plans, Parish Plans, and Village Design Statements.
