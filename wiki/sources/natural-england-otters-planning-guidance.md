# Natural England — Otters Planning Guidance

**Source type**: Natural England standing advice (material planning consideration)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/otters-surveys-and-mitigation-for-development-projects
**Ingested**: 2026-06-10

---

## Summary

This is Natural England's standing advice on otters in the planning system. It sets out the legal protections applying to otters, when surveys are required, how to assess development effects, what avoidance, mitigation, and compensation measures are appropriate, and what obligations fall on local planning authorities before granting permission. As standing advice, it is a material consideration in planning decisions.

The guidance follows the same procedural structure as Natural England's standing advice for bats and great crested newts, applying the European Protected Species framework to a third commonly encountered species in English planning.

---

## Legal Status of Otters

Otters are European Protected Species under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010 (2017 consolidation). Key prohibitions cover deliberately killing, injuring, disturbing, or capturing otters; damaging or destroying breeding sites and resting places; and possessing, transporting, or selling them or their remains.

Additional protection applies under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. Otters are also listed as a [[Species of Principal Importance]] under the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006 (S41 list), making them a material consideration in planning decisions even where EPS licensing is not triggered.

---

## Survey Triggers

Surveys are required when:

- Distribution records indicate potential otter presence — the [[NBN Atlas]] is the recommended first check
- Development affects water bodies, rivers, streams, lakes, or marshland
- Development may affect nearby habitats indirectly through noise, light, or other environmental effects

Survey work covers presence/absence assessments and habitat evaluations. Ecologists must be qualified and experienced; certain activities (e.g. entering holts) require a survey licence from Natural England.

---

## Assessing Effects

Development proposals must address the likelihood of otter impacts and the scale and proximity of development to otter habitat. Potential harm sources include:

- Habitat loss or degradation near water
- Habitat fragmentation or obstruction of movement routes
- Loss of holts and resting places
- Disturbance to feeding and movement
- Water quality degradation affecting prey species

---

## Mitigation Hierarchy

**Avoidance**: avoiding work near water bodies and known otter habitat; creating buffer zones along rivers; scheduling work outside nighttime hours.

**Mitigation**: retaining otter habitats in water and on banks; installing otter-proof fencing during construction.

**Compensation**: constructing artificial holts; building viaducts or underpasses at crossing barriers; installing mammal ledges on bridges and culverts; restoring or improving riparian habitats.

---

## LPA Obligations

Before granting planning permission, local planning authorities must:

- Ensure planning conditions align with otter mitigation licence requirements
- Satisfy themselves that Natural England will likely issue a mitigation licence (the standard [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] three-part test)
- Attach mitigation and compensation strategies as planning conditions

There is no District Level Licensing equivalent for otters — the standard site-by-site EPS licence route applies in all cases.

---

## Post-Development Monitoring

Management plans must maintain habitat suitability, monitor for movement barriers, track human-related pressures, and verify mitigation effectiveness through surveys and remedial action.

---

## Gloucestershire Relevance

Gloucestershire's extensive watercourse network — including the Severn, Avon, Churn, Coln, Leadon, and Windrush — means otter survey triggers will apply to a wide range of development proposals across all six districts. The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds local otter distribution data; the [[NBN Atlas]] provides the national picture. Both should be consulted at pre-application stage.
