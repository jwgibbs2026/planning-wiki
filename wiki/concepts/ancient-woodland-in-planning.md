# Ancient Woodland in Planning

Ancient woodland is one of the most strongly protected habitats in the English planning system. It is classified as an **irreplaceable habitat** — meaning it cannot be recreated or substituted, and compensation alone cannot justify a planning approval that would damage or destroy it.

---

## What is ancient woodland?

Ancient woodland is any area that has been **continuously wooded since at least 1600 AD**. The 1600 AD threshold is used because it pre-dates systematic planting of new woodland in England, so any woodland present at that date is considered to have developed naturally over centuries.

Ancient woodland includes:

- **Ancient semi-natural woodland (ASNW)** — retains native trees, shrubs, ground flora, soils, and invertebrate communities developed over centuries
- **Plantations on ancient woodland sites (PAWS)** — original trees replaced with plantation species, but ancient woodland soils, flora, and invertebrates persist; see [[Plantations on Ancient Woodland Sites]]
- **Wood pastures** identified as ancient
- **Historic parkland** — protected as a heritage asset

The ecological value of ancient woodland lies not just in its trees but in its **soils, mycorrhizal networks, ground flora, and invertebrate communities** — all of which take centuries to develop and cannot be replicated elsewhere.

---

## Why ancient woodland is irreplaceable

The irreplaceability principle is the foundation of all planning decisions affecting ancient woodland. It means:

- **You cannot create equivalent ancient woodland** — planting new trees does not recreate the soil biology, fungal networks, or specialist invertebrate communities
- **Translocation is explicitly unacceptable** — unlike some species mitigation, ancient woodland cannot be moved to another site
- **Compensation can only partially offset losses** — it is a last resort, not a justification for approval
- **The planning test is high** — permission should be refused unless "wholly exceptional reasons" exist AND a suitable compensation strategy is in place

This mirrors [[NPPF]] paragraph 186, which gives ancient woodland the same irreplaceable status as ancient trees and veteran trees. The Natural England and Forestry Commission standing advice operationalises that policy for individual planning decisions.

---

## The planning decision framework

When a planning application affects ancient woodland, the LPA must apply a two-part test:

1. **Are there wholly exceptional reasons for the development?**
2. **Is a suitable compensation strategy in place?**

Both conditions must be met for approval to be possible. Even then, the irreplaceability principle means the bar is very high. In practice, most proposals that would cause significant loss or deterioration of ancient woodland should be refused.

Natural England is a **statutory consultee** where ancient woodland is within or adjacent to a [[SSSI]]. The [[Forestry Commission]] is a **non-statutory consultee** for proposals affecting ancient woodland — their objections carry less formal weight than Natural England's, but are still a material consideration.

## Types of impact

### Direct impacts

Development can damage ancient woodland through:
- Physical destruction of trees, soils, and ground flora
- Root damage and soil compaction from construction
- Pollution increases (air, light, noise, water)
- Water table changes affecting soil moisture and flora
- Habitat fragmentation and increased wind exposure

### Indirect impacts

Development can cause deterioration without direct physical contact:
- Severing ecological connections between woodland patches
- Reducing adjacent semi-natural habitat (edge effects)
- Increasing disturbance, trampling, and trespassing
- Introducing invasive species via adjacent gardens or paths
- Requiring management changes that harm habitat quality

Both types of impact are material planning considerations. Developers cannot argue that a proposal is acceptable simply because it does not physically enter the woodland boundary.

---

## Buffer zone requirements

Buffer zones are the primary tool for protecting ancient woodland from indirect impacts. The Natural England and Forestry Commission standing advice specifies **minimum** distances:

| Feature | Minimum buffer |
|---|---|
| Ancient woodland boundary | **15 metres** from the woodland edge |
| Individual ancient or veteran tree | **15 times the trunk diameter** OR **5 metres from canopy edge** — whichever is larger |

**Important caveats:**
- The 15m woodland buffer is a **minimum** — larger zones are required where additional impacts such as air pollution, noise, or light are present
- Buffer zones should consist of **semi-natural habitats**, not hard surfaces or amenity grass
- Buffer zones should contribute to **ecological networks** — they are a green infrastructure opportunity, not just a sterile exclusion zone

These figures are directly usable in Neighbourhood Development Plan policies and in objection letters to planning applications. See Annex A — NDP Policy Ideas for suggested NDP policy language.

## The mitigation hierarchy

The same three-stage hierarchy used for protected species applies to ancient woodland:

1. **Avoidance** — select alternative sites or redesign the scheme to prevent impacts entirely; this is always the preferred approach
2. **Mitigation** — where unavoidable impacts occur, implement protective measures: screening barriers, noise reduction, buffer zones, footpath rerouting, construction management plans
3. **Compensation** — as a last resort only: woodland creation, habitat restoration, invasive species removal, long-term management plans

Compensation measures that may be required include:
- Creating native woodland or wood pasture nearby
- Improving the condition of existing woodland
- Removing invasive species from adjacent woodland
- Restoring [[Plantations on Ancient Woodland Sites]] to semi-natural woodland
- Creating ecological connections via green bridges or hedgerows
- Developing long-term management plans with monitoring

---

## Evidence requirements

Developers must provide sufficient evidence to support planning decisions. This includes:

- **Tree surveys following BS 5837** — the British Standard for tree surveys in relation to design, demolition, and construction; desk study alone is not sufficient
- **Ecological surveys** following best practice guidance (typically CIEEM standards)
- **Historical map analysis** — to confirm ancient woodland status where the Ancient Woodland Inventory record is uncertain
- **Fieldwork** — where desk study evidence is insufficient

LPAs can require this evidence before validating or determining an application.

## Gloucestershire context

Ancient woodland is highly relevant across Gloucestershire:

- **Forest of Dean** — one of England's largest ancient woodland complexes; significant areas are also [[SSSI]] and Special Area of Conservation
- **Cotswold escarpment** — numerous ancient woodland fragments along the scarp slope and in combes; many are Local Wildlife Sites
- **Severn Vale** — scattered ancient woodland remnants, often in river valleys and on steeper ground
- **Hedgerows and parkland** — ancient and veteran trees occur widely across all six districts in hedgerows, historic parkland, and orchards

The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds data on ancient woodland and veteran trees in the county. The Ancient Woodland Inventory (a Natural England dataset) is the definitive register and should be checked for any site within or adjacent to woodland.

Communities and parish councils can use ancient woodland buffer zone requirements directly in Neighbourhood Development Plan policies to protect local woodland from development pressure.
