# Plantations on Ancient Woodland Sites (PAWS)

A Plantation on an Ancient Woodland Site (PAWS) is an area of ancient woodland where the original native tree canopy was removed and replaced with planted trees — typically conifers or other non-native species — during the twentieth century. Despite the altered canopy, PAWS retain the ancient woodland features that make these sites irreplaceable.

---

## Why PAWS are still protected

The ecological value of ancient woodland lies primarily in its **soils, ground flora, mycorrhizal networks, and invertebrate communities** — not just its trees. These features persist in PAWS even after the original canopy has been replaced:

- **Ancient woodland soils** — centuries of leaf litter, fungal networks, and soil biology survive beneath plantation canopy
- **Ground flora** — ancient woodland indicator plants (bluebell, wood anemone, wild garlic, dog's mercury, and many others) persist in the understorey
- **Invertebrates** — specialist saproxylic and ground-dwelling invertebrates associated with ancient woodland soils and dead wood remain present
- **Structural features** — old coppice stools, veteran trees retained within the plantation, and ancient boundary features may survive

Because these features are irreplaceable, PAWS receive the same planning protection as ancient semi-natural woodland. The fact that the canopy has been altered does not reduce the site's protected status.

---

## PAWS and the planning system

PAWS are included in the Ancient Woodland Inventory (the Natural England dataset identifying ancient woodland in England). Any planning application affecting a PAWS site must be assessed against the same framework as other ancient woodland:

- The **irreplaceability principle** applies in full
- The **wholly exceptional reasons** test applies
- **Buffer zone requirements** apply (minimum 15m from woodland boundary)
- Both **direct and indirect impacts** must be assessed

See [[Ancient Woodland in Planning]] for the full planning decision framework.

---

## PAWS restoration as compensation

One distinctive feature of PAWS is that **restoration to semi-natural woodland** is a recognised compensation measure. Where development causes unavoidable harm to ancient woodland, a developer may be required to fund or undertake PAWS restoration elsewhere as part of a compensation package.

PAWS restoration typically involves:
- Removing plantation species (particularly non-native conifers)
- Allowing natural regeneration of native species from the surviving seed bank and root systems
- Supplementary planting of native species where natural regeneration is insufficient
- Removing invasive species (rhododendron, laurel, etc.)
- Long-term management to maintain semi-natural woodland structure

**Important caveat:** PAWS restoration is compensation for residual harm — it does not make development acceptable in itself. The irreplaceability principle means that compensation cannot alone justify approval. Restoration of a PAWS elsewhere cannot recreate the specific ancient woodland features of the site being damaged.

## Gloucestershire context

PAWS are present across Gloucestershire, particularly in:
- **Forest of Dean** — significant areas of the Forest were planted with conifers in the twentieth century over ancient woodland soils; ongoing restoration work is underway
- **Cotswold escarpment** — some ancient woodland fragments were converted to plantation in the mid-twentieth century
- **Private estates** — conifer plantations on ancient woodland soils occur on private land across the county

The [[Forestry Commission]] manages much of the Forest of Dean and is involved in PAWS restoration programmes there. The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds data on PAWS locations in the county.
