# Invasive Non-Native Species

Invasive Non-Native Species (INNS) are plants, animals, or microorganisms that cause significant negative environmental, social, or economic impacts when introduced beyond their natural range. Several INNS are material planning considerations and must be addressed in planning applications where they are present on or near development sites.

## Legal Framework

- **Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (Schedule 9)**: Makes it a criminal offence to plant or allow to grow certain listed plants in the wild. Includes Japanese knotweed, giant hogweed, Himalayan balsam, rhododendron (in certain contexts), and others
- **Environmental Protection Act 1990**: Classifies INNS-contaminated soil as "controlled waste" — requiring disposal at licensed sites
- **Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000**: Strengthened enforcement powers

## Key Species in Gloucestershire

### Japanese Knotweed (*Fallopia japonica*)
The most commonly encountered INNS in planning. Characteristics:
- Extremely invasive; hollow bamboo-like stems up to 3m tall
- Spreads by rhizome fragments — even tiny pieces can establish new plants
- Rhizomes penetrate foundations, drainage systems, and hard surfaces
- Classified as controlled waste; cannot be disposed of at normal landfill
- Presence must be disclosed in property transactions; mortgage lenders may refuse loans

For planning: any site with knotweed requires a **Japanese Knotweed Management Plan** before or as a condition of permission. Treatment typically involves multi-year herbicide programmes or excavation and disposal off-site.

### Himalayan Balsam (*Impatiens glandulifera*)
- Dominant in Gloucestershire's river corridors (Severn, Wye, Avon tributaries)
- Outcompetes native bankside vegetation; dies back in winter leaving bare banks vulnerable to erosion
- Spreads rapidly by explosive seed dispersal
- Management: hand-pulling before seed set (May–July)

### Giant Hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*)
- Sap causes severe photosensitive burns on skin contact
- Mainly on riverbanks and disturbed ground
- **Public health hazard**: sites with giant hogweed require risk assessment and specialist removal

### Rhododendron (*Rhododendron ponticum*)
- Major issue in the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] and Forest of Dean
- Shades out woodland ground flora; toxic to grazing animals
- Targeted for removal in ancient woodland restoration programmes
- The [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy|Gloucestershire LNRS]] identifies INNS removal as a priority action in woodland habitats

### American Signal Crayfish (*Pacifastacus leniusculus*)
- Outcompetes and transmits crayfish plague to the native white-clawed crayfish
- Burrows into riverbanks causing erosion and collapse
- White-clawed crayfish are a primary reason the River Wye is designated as a SAC
- Any works to watercourses must consider signal crayfish and avoid spreading them to unaffected reaches

### American Mink (*Neovison vison*)
- Predator of water voles, ground-nesting birds, and fish
- Mink control is recognised as a legitimate compensatory measure in [[Water Voles in Planning|water vole]] mitigation programmes

## INNS in Planning Applications

Where INNS are identified on or adjacent to a development site, the LPA will typically require:
- An **INNS survey and risk assessment**
- A **management and control plan** detailing treatment methods, timescales, and monitoring
- Conditions ensuring INNS are treated before or during development, and not spread by construction activities
- Disposal routes for excavated material confirmed as compliant with controlled waste regulations

## Gloucestershire LNRS

The [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy|Gloucestershire LNRS (2026)]] identifies INNS as a key pressure on nature recovery, alongside climate change and deer pressure. The strategy recommends INNS removal as part of habitat creation and restoration projects, and flags the importance of preventing spread during development works.
