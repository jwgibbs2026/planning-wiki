# Agri-environment Schemes

Agri-environment schemes are government-funded programmes that pay farmers and land managers to manage their land in ways that benefit the environment, including wildlife habitat creation, water quality improvement, and carbon sequestration. They are the primary mechanism through which the Local Nature Recovery Strategy can be delivered on agricultural land.

## Why They Matter for Planning

Over 75% of Gloucestershire's land is in agricultural or horticultural use. Agri-environment schemes are how most nature recovery actually happens on the ground — enabling farmers to create habitats, restore hedgerows, manage grasslands, and improve watercourses in a financially viable way. Planning policy increasingly references these schemes as a delivery mechanism for Biodiversity Net Gain off-site habitat creation.

## Current Schemes (post-2025)

The UK left the EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) and replaced it with a new suite of schemes under the **Environmental Land Management (ELM)** framework.

### Sustainable Farming Incentive (SFI)
The main scheme for most farmers. Pays for sustainable farming practices through standardised **actions** (previously called "standards"). Key nature-relevant actions include:
- **Hedgerow management**: no-cut buffer strips, gapping up, coppicing
- **Arable field margins**: grass margins, wildflower margins, beetle banks
- **Watercourse management**: 4m or 6m grass buffer strips along rivers and streams
- **Ponds**: creation and management of in-field ponds
- **Grassland**: management of species-rich grassland, purple moor grass rush pasture
- **Woodland**: edge management, veteran tree protection
- **Integrated Pest Management**: reduced pesticide use

SFI is open to all farmers in England who are claiming the Basic Payment Scheme (BPS) or farm at least 1 hectare. Actions can be combined flexibly.

### Countryside Stewardship (CS)
A more targeted scheme for higher-value environmental outcomes, typically on land with significant existing ecological value. Options include:
- **Higher Tier CS**: complex, ecologically important sites — ancient woodland, lowland meadows, heathland, wetland restoration; requires an application and assessment
- **Mid Tier CS**: targeted options for farms with good ecological potential
- **Capital grants**: one-off payments for hedgerow restoration, pond creation, barn owl boxes, otter ledges, and similar infrastructure

Countryside Stewardship is the main route for:
- Local Wildlife Sites management agreements
- Habitat creation qualifying as off-site Biodiversity Net Gain
- Restoration of degraded habitats to contribute to LNRS targets

### Landscape Recovery
A competitive scheme for large-scale, long-term land use change — typically 500–5,000 hectares. Aimed at significant nature recovery projects, rewilding, wetland creation at scale, and new woodland. Likely to be the mechanism for GWT's Wildbelt (300 ha wetland) and Severn Treescapes ambitions.

## Agri-environment and the LNRS

The Gloucestershire LNRS (2026) is the spatial guide for where agri-environment investment delivers the most benefit. The SFI and CS schemes use LNRS priority areas to:
- Direct funding toward land where habitat creation will connect with existing good habitat
- Prioritise species-specific actions (e.g. curlew habitat in the Severn and Avon Vales, large blue butterfly grassland in the Golden Valley)

## Agri-environment and BNG

Countryside Stewardship agreements creating new habitats can register those habitats on the **Biodiversity Gain Site Register** as a [[Conservation Covenants|conservation covenant]] or S106 agreement, allowing them to sell off-site BNG units to developers. This creates a direct financial link between farming for nature and the planning system.

## Gloucestershire Context

The GWT and the Farming and Wildlife Advisory Group (FWAG) provide advice to farmers on agri-environment scheme options in Gloucestershire. Key habitats targeted:
- Calcareous grassland restoration in the Cotswolds
- Hedgerow restoration across lowland Gloucestershire
- Watercourse buffer strips in the Severn Vale
- Wet grassland and floodplain restoration in the Severn Hams
