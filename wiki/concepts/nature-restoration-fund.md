# Nature Restoration Fund

The Nature Restoration Fund (NRF) is a new mechanism created by the Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025 that allows developers to meet environmental obligations through a strategic pooled fund, rather than site-by-site mitigation.

## The Problem It Solves

Environmental assessment requirements — particularly for protected species licences and nutrient neutrality — can block or significantly delay planning permissions. Site-specific mitigation is often inefficient: small-scale ecological measures on individual sites deliver less than strategic, landscape-scale conservation.

The NRF replaces this with a streamlined payment into a central fund that Natural England uses to deliver better outcomes at scale.

## How It Works

1. Natural England creates an **Environmental Delivery Plan (EDP)** for a specific geographic area or environmental issue
2. The EDP sets out the conservation measures, the development impacts covered, and a charging schedule
3. Developers can choose to pay a levy into the fund instead of producing individual habitat assessments or obtaining species licences
4. Natural England contracts conservation delivery through competitive tendering
5. Once an EDP is in force, developers in scope are no longer required to carry out site-specific [[Habitats Regulations Assessment|HRAs]] or obtain [[EPS Mitigation Licence|EPS licences]] for covered impacts

## Safeguards

- The Secretary of State approves EDPs only if conservation measures "materially outweigh the negative effects of development"
- Annual reports and mid-term EDP monitoring reports
- Secretary of State power to amend or revoke an EDP if the improvement test is not being met
- Backup measures triggered if targets appear insufficient

## First Priority: Nutrient Neutrality

The first EDPs address nutrient neutrality requirements, which currently block around **16,500 homes per year** across England. Affected catchments include the Severn/Avon (relevant to Gloucestershire), Solent, Somerset Levels, The Broads, and others.

Natural England's existing Nutrient Mitigation Scheme will transition into these EDPs.

## Gloucestershire Context

The Severn and Avon river catchment is one of the priority catchments for the first wave of EDPs. This is directly relevant to Gloucestershire, where nutrient neutrality has restricted development near certain catchments. Once the Severn/Avon EDP is operational (expected from 2026), developers in affected Gloucestershire locations may be able to pay a levy rather than purchasing individual nutrient neutrality credits.

## Future Scope

Government intends to expand to other species and habitats. [[District Level Licensing]] for [[Great Crested Newts in Planning|great crested newts]] is a likely next phase.
