# Barn Owls in Planning

Barn owls (*Tyto alba*) are explicitly named in Natural England's wild bird standing advice as a species requiring surveys before planning decisions are made. They are a Species of Principal Importance under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006 and are amber-listed on the Birds of Conservation Concern assessment. Their association with buildings and mature trees makes them particularly relevant to rural development, demolition, and conversion projects.

---

## Legal status

Barn owls are protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981:
- It is an offence to kill, injure, or take a barn owl
- It is an offence to damage or destroy a nest in use or being built
- It is an offence to take or destroy barn owl eggs

Barn owls are **not** European Protected Species and are not covered by the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017. There is no development licence available for barn owl harm — see [[Wild Birds in Planning]] for the critical no-licence rule.

Barn owls are listed as a [[Species of Principal Importance]] (S41 species), making their presence a material planning consideration.

---

## Survey triggers

A barn owl survey is required when development affects:
- **Buildings** — barns, farm buildings, churches, old houses, derelict structures, and any building with roof voids, loft spaces, or cavities
- **Trees** — trees over 100 years old or with cavities large enough to support nesting
- **Known or likely habitat** — open farmland, rough grassland, field margins, and riparian habitats used for foraging

The presence of barn owl records within the local area (from Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records or [[NBN Atlas]]) should also trigger a survey even if no direct evidence is found on site.

---

## Survey approach

Barn owl surveys are typically conducted by qualified ecologists and involve:
- **Building inspection** — checking roof voids, loft spaces, and cavities for pellets, feathers, droppings, and prey remains
- **Tree inspection** — checking cavities in veteran and mature trees
- **Habitat assessment** — evaluating foraging habitat quality in the surrounding area
- **Dusk/dawn watches** — observing flight lines and foraging behaviour where presence is suspected but not confirmed

Surveys should be timed to avoid the breeding season where possible, though building inspections can be conducted year-round if no active nest is present.

## Mitigation hierarchy

> **Important — barn owl nesting season differs from the general rule.** The general March–August nesting season stated in [[Wild Birds in Planning]] does **not** apply to barn owls. Barn owls can nest from **April to October** — chicks may still be in the nest in September and October. Works to buildings or trees with confirmed or potential barn owl use must avoid the full April–October window, or be preceded by an inspection confirming no active nest. Following the general March–August rule for barn owl works is insufficient and could result in an offence.

### Avoidance
- Time demolition, conversion, or roof works outside the barn owl nesting season: **works safe window is November to March only**
- Retain buildings or trees supporting barn owl nests where possible within the site layout

### Displacement (outside breeding season only)
- Block access to nest sites progressively outside the breeding season to encourage birds to relocate before works begin
- Displacement is only lawful when no active nest is present

### Replacement features
- **Integrated nest boxes** — barn owl nest boxes should ideally be integrated into new or replacement structures rather than surface-mounted; internal nest chambers within new barn conversions are a common solution
- **External nest boxes** — where integration is not possible, purpose-built external boxes on retained trees or new structures
- **Habitat retention and creation** — rough grassland, field margins, and hedgerow networks supporting foraging should be retained or created as part of the development

### Pre-establishment requirement
Replacement nest boxes and habitat must be established **before** works begin, not delivered post-development. This reflects the no-licence rule: if a barn owl is present when works start, an offence has already been committed.

---

## Monitoring

Post-development monitoring should verify that:
- Nest boxes are being used
- Foraging habitat is maintained
- Mitigation has been effective

Remedial measures must be specified in advance in case monitoring reveals failure.

## Gloucestershire context

Barn owls are widespread in Gloucestershire, particularly in the Severn Vale, the Cotswold escarpment, and the Forest of Dean fringe. The county's agricultural landscape — with its mix of old farm buildings, rough grassland, and hedgerow networks — provides good barn owl habitat.

Key planning contexts in Gloucestershire include:
- **Agricultural building conversions** — a common permitted development and full planning application type across all six districts; barn owl surveys should be standard practice
- **Demolition of farm buildings** — triggers survey requirement even where the building appears derelict
- **Rural housing developments** — sites adjacent to rough grassland or field margins may affect foraging habitat
- **Neighbourhood Development Plans** — barn owl nest box integration into new buildings is a straightforward NDP policy hook, particularly in rural parishes

The Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust and Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records hold local barn owl data and can advise on survey requirements and mitigation standards.
