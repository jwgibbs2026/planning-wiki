# Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects

Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects (NSIPs) are large-scale infrastructure projects of national importance. They follow a separate consenting regime to standard planning applications and are decided at the national level rather than by Local Planning Authorities.

## What Counts as an NSIP?

Examples include:
- New motorways and major A-road improvements
- New railway lines
- Large electricity generating stations (e.g. wind farms above a certain capacity)
- Major water resource schemes (reservoirs)
- New ports or harbour developments

The relevant Secretary of State makes the final decision, based on a recommendation from the Planning Inspectorate.

## Getting Involved

The examination process is open to the public and statutory bodies. Any person with an interest in the area affected can register as an "interested party" and submit representations to the Planning Inspectorate's examination.

Key stages to watch for:
1. **Pre-application consultation** — the developer must consult affected communities before submitting
2. **Acceptance** — the Inspectorate decides whether to accept the application
3. **Pre-examination** — opportunity to register as an interested party
4. **Examination** — hearings and written representations over several months
5. **Recommendation and decision** — the Inspector reports to the Secretary of State

## Nature and NSIPs

Large infrastructure projects can have significant impacts on Protected Species, [[SSSI|SSSIs]], Local Wildlife Sites, and ecological networks. Statutory consultees such as Natural England have formal roles in the process, but community representations and wildlife records from the Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records can still strengthen protections.
