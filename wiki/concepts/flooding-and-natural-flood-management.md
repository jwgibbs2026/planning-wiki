# Flooding and Natural Flood Management

Flooding is one of the most significant natural hazards in England, and one of the key climate change risks for Gloucestershire. Planning policy requires all new development to manage flood risk, and to use [[SuDS|sustainable drainage]] wherever possible.

## Why Flooding Matters in Gloucestershire

Gloucestershire contains the Severn Vale — one of England's most flood-prone floodplains. The county's rivers (Severn, Wye, Avon, Thames headwaters, Frome) have been extensively modified — straightened, canalised, drained — making flooding faster and more severe.

Key pressures:
- 75% of Gloucestershire land in agricultural/horticultural use: heavily drained
- Rivers too constrained; floodplains no longer functioning naturally
- Climate change making extreme rainfall events more frequent and intense

## Natural Flood Management (NFM)

NFM uses natural processes and features to slow, store, and filter floodwater, reducing flood risk downstream. Key techniques:

| Technique | How It Works | Co-Benefits |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Floodplain restoration | Remove flood banks; allow rivers to reconnect with floodplain | Wetland habitat, wading birds, carbon storage |
| River re-naturalisation | Remove weirs, restore meanders, re-widen channels | Fish passage, riparian habitat, water quality |
| Riparian woodland/wet woodland | Woody debris slows flow; roots stabilise banks | Carbon storage, bat commuting corridors |
| Leaky dams / woody debris | Slow water movement in headwater streams | Invertebrate habitat |
| Moorland/peat restoration | Restore water-holding capacity of upland soils | Carbon sequestration, biodiversity |
| Beavers | Natural dam-builders; create wetland mosaics | Biodiversity, water storage, sediment |

The Gloucestershire LNRS (2026) identifies river re-naturalisation and floodplain restoration as a priority key message for the county.

## Sustainable Urban Drainage (SuDS)

[[SuDS]] manage surface water runoff from buildings and hard surfaces using natural drainage principles. National Standards (2025) require all new developments to use SuDS, following a runoff destination hierarchy and meeting standards for biodiversity (including contributing to Biodiversity Net Gain).

Biodiverse SuDS are specifically advocated in the Gloucestershire LNRS as a mechanism for biodiversity connectivity in urban areas.

## Planning Framework

### National Policy

- [[NPPF]]: Requires development to demonstrate flood risk is acceptable; directs development away from highest flood risk areas (Flood Zones 2 and 3); requires SuDS on major developments
- National SuDS Standards: Statutory requirements for all new development drainage

### Flood Risk Assessments

For development in or near Flood Zones 2 or 3, a Flood Risk Assessment (FRA) is required. The Sequential Test and Exception Test apply:
- **Sequential Test**: Steer development to lower flood risk areas first
- **Exception Test**: Where development in higher flood risk areas is justified, it must be safe, not increase flood risk elsewhere, and deliver wider sustainability benefits

### Lead Local Flood Authorities (LLFAs)

The Lead Local Flood Authority for Gloucestershire is **Gloucestershire County Council**. LLFAs:
- Manage local flood risk (surface water, groundwater, ordinary watercourses)
- Approve SuDS drainage for new developments
- Publish Local Flood Risk Management Strategies

The Environment Agency manages flooding from main rivers and the sea.

## Gloucestershire Context

Key flood risk areas:
- Gloucester city — Severn Vale floodplain; historic flooding 2007 and 2012
- Tewkesbury — confluence of Severn and Avon; significant flood events
- Stroud Valleys — steep catchments with rapid response to rainfall; local SUDS vital

The Gloucestershire LNRS (2026) recommends:
- Re-naturalising rivers and restoring floodplains
- Treating land next to watercourses as "functionally linked" to the river
- Exploring beaver reintroduction for NFM
- Biodiverse SuDS in all new urban developments
- Reducing artificial drainage across the agricultural landscape
