# Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017

> **Note on versions**: The operative instrument is the **Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017** (SI 2017/1012). The 2017 Regulations consolidated and replaced the 2010 version; all current Natural England guidance, EPS licences, and HRA decisions are made under the 2017 instrument. The 2010 version is no longer in force.

The Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017 (often called the "Habitats Regulations") implement the EU Habitats Directive and Birds Directive in domestic UK law. Although the UK has left the EU, these protections have been retained and preserved in domestic legislation.

## What They Protect

### European Protected Species
The Regulations give the strongest level of legal protection to "European Protected Species" (EPS), which include:
- All bat species
- Great crested newts
- Dormice
- Otters
- Water voles (partial)
- Various invertebrates and plants

It is an offence to deliberately capture, injure or kill an EPS, deliberately disturb them (including at roost or nest), or damage or destroy a breeding site or resting place.

### Protected Habitats
The Regulations protect **Special Areas of Conservation (SACs)** and **Special Protection Areas (SPAs)** — the most important wildlife sites in Europe. In England, these largely overlap with [[SSSI|SSSIs]].

## Habitats Regulations Assessment (HRA)

Any plan or project that may significantly affect a SAC or SPA must undergo a **Habitats Regulations Assessment (HRA)** before consent is given. LPAs must carry out HRAs for Local Plans and relevant planning applications.

The Nature Restoration Fund (Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025) introduces Environmental Delivery Plans that can replace the requirement for site-specific HRAs for covered development types — the first significant change to how HRA obligations are met since the Regulations came into force.

## EPS Mitigation Licensing

Where a development cannot avoid affecting an EPS, the developer must obtain a mitigation licence from Natural England. The licence is only granted if all three tests are met:
1. There is no satisfactory alternative
2. The development is in the overriding public interest
3. The favourable conservation status of the species is maintained

This is a powerful protection — if a developer cannot demonstrate these three tests, the licence (and therefore the development) cannot proceed. See [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] for full detail.
