# Major Development in AONBs

Development in Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty (AONBs) and National Landscapes is subject to the **highest level of protection** in the planning system — equivalent to National Parks. For proposals that constitute "major development", a stringent exceptional circumstances test applies under the National Planning Policy Framework (NPPF).

---

## The NPPF test (paragraphs 190–191 and footnote 67)

NPPF paragraphs 190–191 and footnote 67 set out the test for major development in AONBs. This is **not a simple planning balance**. A proposal must be refused unless:

1. There are **exceptional circumstances** justifying the development; AND
2. It is in the **public interest**

To demonstrate public interest, applicants must address:
- **(a) Need** — including national considerations and the impact of permitting or refusing the development
- **(b) Sequential site selection** — whether the development could be located outside the AONB, and the cost and scope of doing so
- **(c) Detrimental effects** — any detrimental effects on the environment, landscape, and recreational opportunities, and how they could be moderated

---

## Defining "major development" in an AONB

There is **no blanket definition** of major development in the AONB context. The NPPF definition (footnote 67) differs from the Town and Country Planning (Development Management Procedure) Order 2015 thresholds. A development that falls below DMP Order thresholds may still constitute major development in the NPPF sense, and vice versa.

The test is whether a development **could have a significant adverse impact on the natural beauty** of the AONB, including its:
- Landscape quality and scenic quality
- Relative wildness and tranquillity
- Natural heritage features
- Cultural heritage

This must be assessed **case by case** based on the nature, scale, setting, and potential impacts of the proposal. The test applies to Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects (NSIPs) as well as locally determined applications.

Critically, the major development test must be addressed at the **plan-making stage** (when allocating sites in Local Plans) as well as at the development management stage. A legal opinion (Landmark Chambers, 2017) confirmed it would "arguably amount to an error of law" to fail to consider NPPF paragraph 190 at the site allocations stage. The West Oxfordshire Local Plan inspector found an allocation unsound in the absence of a housing need figure for the AONB sub-area (Planning Inspectorate, 2018).

---

## Case law

- **R (Mevagissey Parish Council) v Cornwall Council [2013]** — confirmed that planning committees must refuse major development in AONBs unless exceptional circumstances and public interest criteria are met; it is not a simple balance
- **R (Advearse) v Dorset Council v Hallam Land Management Ltd [2020]** — further confirmed the stringency of the test

## Cotswolds context

The [[Cotswolds National Landscape Board]] has published a **Landscape-led Development Position Statement** (2021) with a major development checklist (Appendix 5). The A417 Missing Link Scheme is cited as a case study for landscape-led development principles.

In 2023/24, the CNL Board considered that 3 major development proposals were permitted that it regarded as major development in the context of NPPF paragraph 183.

The [[Seek to Further Duty]] (in force since 26 December 2023) adds a further obligation: relevant authorities must not only apply the exceptional circumstances test but must also take positive steps to further the conservation and enhancement of the CNL's natural beauty.

---

## Housing in the CNL

Housing in the Cotswolds is among the most unaffordable in England (affordability ratio 11x, compared to England's 8x). The CNL Management Plan (Policy CE15) states that:
- Housing should focus on affordable housing, especially social rented in perpetuity
- At least 50% affordable in market developments
- 100% affordable on Rural Exception Sites (minimum 75% in exceptional circumstances)
- The standard method housing need figure is unconstrained and may not be fully met in the CNL
- The CNL is unlikely to be suitable for meeting unmet housing needs from non-designated areas

NPPF paragraph 36 requires Local Plans to meet Objectively Assessed Need (OAN) as a minimum, but paragraph 11 allows exceptions including national landscape policies. The CNL may legitimately have a housing requirement below OAN.
