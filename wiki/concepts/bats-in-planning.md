# Bats in Planning

Bats are the most commonly encountered [[European Protected Species]] in English planning applications. All 18 resident bat species in England have the highest tier of legal protection, meaning any development that could affect them triggers specific legal obligations for developers and local planning authorities (LPAs).

This page explains what those obligations are, when surveys are needed, what mitigation is required, and how the licensing system works — in plain English, for communities, parish councils, and developers alike.

---

## Why Bats Matter in Planning

Bats are long-lived, slow-breeding mammals that depend on stable roost sites — often returning to the same building or tree for decades. Losing a roost is not a minor inconvenience; it can eliminate a local population. This is why the law treats roost destruction as a serious matter even when bats are not physically present at the time of works.

Bats also serve as indicators of wider ecological health. Their presence signals insect-rich habitats, connected green corridors, and low light pollution — all features that planning policy increasingly seeks to protect and enhance.

---

## Legal Protection

All bat species are protected under two pieces of legislation:

**Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010** (as amended; the operative version is the 2017 consolidation):
- Bats are [[European Protected Species]] (EPS)
- It is illegal to deliberately kill, injure, disturb, or capture bats
- It is illegal to damage or destroy a breeding site or resting place — **even when bats are not present**
- Possession, transport, sale, or exchange of bats is prohibited

**Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981**:
- Additional protection against disturbance in occupied shelters
- Obstruction of access to roosting places is prohibited

Several bat species are also listed as [[Species of Principal Importance]] under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006, requiring public bodies to have regard to their conservation.

## When Surveys Are Required

Natural England standing advice is clear: surveys are required whenever development **could** affect bat roosts, foraging habitat, or commuting routes. The trigger is **potential impact**, not confirmed presence.

> **Absence of records does not indicate absence of bats.**

This precautionary principle is legally significant. Developers cannot avoid survey obligations simply by pointing to a lack of existing data. LPAs should request surveys whenever any of the following apply:

### Buildings
- Minimal artificial lighting
- Proximity to woodland or water
- Uneven roof tiles, large timbers, cracks, or crevices
- South-facing walls with hanging tiles
- Any structure with potential roosting features

### Trees
- Ancient, veteran, or within ancient woodland
- Natural cavities (ash, beech, oak are particularly important)
- Damage from rot, weathering, or wildlife
- Loose or creviced bark
- Dense ivy coverage

### Other Triggers
- Water bodies (rivers, ponds, ditches) — key foraging habitat
- Proposed lighting near bat habitat
- Wind turbines within 50 metres of bat commuting, foraging, or roosting habitat

## Roost Types

Not all roosts are equal. Surveys must identify roost type because this determines the level of protection and the mitigation required:

| Roost Type | Function | Conservation Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Maternity roost | Females give birth and raise young | Highest — loss can eliminate local breeding population |
| Hibernation roost | Bats overwinter | High — disturbance in winter can be fatal |
| Transitional roost | Used during spring/autumn migration | Moderate |
| Day roost | Resting during daylight hours | Variable |
| Commuting route | Flight path between roosts and foraging areas | Important — must be maintained |

## Mitigation Hierarchy

The guidance requires a sequential approach — avoidance must be considered before mitigation, and mitigation before compensation.

### 1. Avoidance
- Redesign the development to preserve roosts in situ
- Maintain supporting habitat features (trees, hedgerows, water)
- Adjust timing of works to avoid maternity season (May–August) and hibernation (November–March)
- Modify working methods to prevent harm

### 2. Mitigation
- Create new roosting features within existing structures
- Install bat boxes or integrated bat bricks elsewhere on site
- Redesign lighting to avoid roosts and flight corridors (see [[Dark Corridors]])
- Enhance insect-rich habitats to support foraging

### 3. Compensation
Where roost destruction is genuinely unavoidable:
- No net loss of occupied or unoccupied roosts
- Like-for-like replacement (maternity roost replaced with maternity roost)
- Continued population function must be demonstrated

## The Licensing System

Even where planning permission is granted, developers may need an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] from Natural England before carrying out works that would otherwise be illegal.

**The critical procedural point for LPAs**: before granting planning permission, the LPA must be satisfied that a licence *will* be granted. This means assessing whether the three-part licensing test is likely to be met:
1. The development is for an imperative reason of overriding public interest (or other permitted purpose)
2. There is no satisfactory alternative
3. The favourable conservation status of the species will be maintained

LPAs that grant permission without considering licence likelihood expose themselves to legal risk. Communities can use this as a basis for challenging applications where bat impacts have not been properly assessed.

---

## Biodiversity Enhancement

Beyond legal compliance, Natural England encourages developers to go further:
- Create or enhance on-site habitats for bats and their prey
- Integrate bat features into building design (bat bricks, tiles, loft spaces)
- Align enhancements with the Local Nature Recovery Strategy and Biodiversity Action Plan
- Contribute to Biodiversity Net Gain requirements

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire's landscape — ancient woodland in the Forest of Dean, veteran trees in the Cotswolds, water meadows along the Severn and its tributaries, and a large stock of historic buildings — creates extensive bat habitat across the county. Bats are likely to be a consideration in a significant proportion of planning applications, particularly in rural areas and historic settlements.

Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) holds bat distribution data and should be consulted as part of any survey trigger assessment. Parish councils and community groups can request this data when scrutinising planning applications.

Key species of concern in Gloucestershire include lesser horseshoe bat, greater horseshoe bat, Bechstein's bat, and barbastelle — all species of high conservation concern with restricted distributions.
