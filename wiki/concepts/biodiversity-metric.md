# Biodiversity Metric

The Biodiversity Metric (officially the Statutory Biodiversity Metric) is the calculation tool used to measure Biodiversity Net Gain for planning purposes in England. It was developed by Natural England and Defra and is the mandatory method for calculating BNG on all applicable developments.

## What It Measures

The metric converts habitats into **biodiversity units** — a standardised score that reflects:
- **Area** of the habitat (hectares)
- **Condition** (poor, moderate, good — assessed against standard criteria)
- **Distinctiveness** (how ecologically valuable the habitat type is)
- **Strategic significance** (whether it is in a location identified by the Local Nature Recovery Strategy)
- **Difficulty of creation** (some habitats take decades to establish)
- **Time to target condition** (a discount applied for habitats that take longer to reach good condition)

## How the Calculation Works

A development must show that the **post-development** biodiversity unit score is at least **10% higher** than the **pre-development** score, across three separate accounts:
1. **Habitat units** (terrestrial habitats)
2. **Hedgerow units** (hedgerows measured by length)
3. **Watercourse units** (rivers and streams measured by length)

Each account is calculated separately — you cannot compensate for hedgerow loss with pond creation, for example.

## The Condition Assessment

Habitat condition is assessed on site against standard criteria for each broad habitat type (e.g. species-rich grassland, woodland, wetland). Common condition categories:
- **Poor**: degraded, low species diversity, heavily modified
- **Moderate**: some ecological value but below potential
- **Good**: high ecological value, approaching natural reference condition

Condition is assessed by a qualified ecologist, using Natural England's condition assessment guidance for each habitat type.

## Distinctiveness

Habitats are scored by distinctiveness — how ecologically valuable they are regardless of condition:

| Distinctiveness | Examples |
|----------------|---------|
| Very high | Ancient woodland, blanket bog, lowland raised bog |
| High | Species-rich grassland, reedbed, wood pasture |
| Medium | Hedgerows, scrub, woodland |
| Low | Improved grassland, amenity grassland |
| Very low | Bare ground, hard standing |

A very high distinctiveness habitat in poor condition can still score more than a low distinctiveness habitat in good condition.

## On-Site vs Off-Site vs Statutory Credits

BNG must be delivered in this priority order:
1. **On-site**: habitat creation or enhancement within the development site
2. **Off-site**: habitat creation elsewhere, secured by a Section 106 agreement or conservation covenant; should be in locations identified by the LNRS
3. **Statutory biodiversity credits**: government-sold credits as a last resort; developers pay into a central pot which funds habitat creation nationally

Off-site units are sold by habitat banks — landowners who create and manage habitats under long-term legal agreements.

## Key Rules

- **Irreplaceable habitats** (ancient woodland, ancient trees) cannot be offset in the metric — they score so highly that no amount of compensatory habitat would achieve net gain
- **30-year minimum**: habitats created for BNG must be maintained and legally secured for a minimum of 30 years
- **Like-for-like**: the metric discourages replacing high-distinctiveness habitats with low-distinctiveness ones
- **[[SuDS|SuDS habitats]]** can count toward BNG — a retention pond designed for wildlife can contribute habitat units

## Gloucestershire Context

The Gloucestershire LNRS (2026) identifies spatial priority areas for nature recovery. BNG off-site units created within these areas receive a **strategic significance multiplier**, increasing their unit value. This incentivises developers to direct off-site BNG funding toward LNRS priority locations — particularly GWT Nature Recovery Zones.
