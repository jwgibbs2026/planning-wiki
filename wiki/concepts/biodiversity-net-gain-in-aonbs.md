# Biodiversity Net Gain in AONBs

The [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] (CNL) requires a **20% Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG)** for development — double the national mandatory minimum of 10% under the Environment Act 2021. This is set out in Policy CE8 of the CNL Management Plan 2025–2030 and justified in detail in Appendix 9.

---

## Why 20% rather than 10%?

The CNL Management Plan makes a detailed legal and policy argument for requiring higher BNG in the CNL:

**1. 10% BNG only ensures no net loss**
Defra's own research (consultation Impact Assessment, section 5.1.1) shows that 10% BNG is the **minimum to ensure no net loss** — meaning 10% BNG does not deliver genuine biodiversity gain. It merely compensates for what is lost.

**2. The "seek to further" duty requires genuine enhancement**
The [[Seek to Further Duty]] (in force since 26 December 2023) requires relevant authorities to actively further the conservation and enhancement of natural beauty — which includes biodiversity. Merely avoiding net loss is insufficient to meet this duty.

**3. Viability evidence supports higher BNG**
Research by the Kent Nature Partnership found that increasing BNG from 10% to 20% **does not significantly affect development viability**. This removes the standard objection to higher BNG requirements.

**4. Early adopter evidence shows risks of low BNG**
Research by Zu Ermgassen et al. (University of Kent, 2021) found that mandatory BNG in early adopter jurisdictions resulted in certain biodiversity loss being traded for unverifiable future gains — suggesting that 10% BNG in practice may not even achieve no net loss.

**5. Protected landscape obligations**
Protected landscapes cover 25% of England but are expected to deliver 50% of the national 500,000 ha habitat restoration target (PLTOF Target 1). The Colchester Declaration (2019) commits all AONBs to redress species and habitat declines. These obligations require more than the national minimum.

---

## How the 20% requirement works in practice

The 20% BNG requirement applies to development proposals within the CNL. It uses the same national BNG metric as the mandatory 10% requirement, but sets a higher threshold. The mitigation hierarchy applies:
1. **Avoid** biodiversity loss in the first place
2. **Mitigate** unavoidable impacts on site
3. **Compensate** for residual losses off-site (as a last resort)

LNRSs are already used by the BNG metric for targeting off-site compensation. The six LNRSs coincident with the CNL should be compatible with the CNL Management Plan.

---

## Irreplaceable habitats

Policy CE8 defines certain habitats as **irreplaceable** — they cannot be recreated, and compensation alone cannot justify their loss:
- Ancient and veteran trees
- Ancient woodland (continuously wooded since 1600, including PAWS)
- Ancient unimproved grassland (continuously unimproved since 1945)
- Ancient hedgerows (pre-Enclosure Acts)

These habitats are subject to the strongest protection in the planning system. The BNG metric cannot be used to offset their loss.

---

## Relationship to national BNG policy

The national mandatory 10% BNG requirement under the Environment Act 2021 applies to all qualifying development in England. The CNL's 20% requirement is a **local policy uplift** — it is not a contradiction of national policy but an exercise of the CNL Board's statutory duty to conserve and enhance natural beauty. Whether MHCLG will publish new NPPF guidance explicitly supporting higher BNG in AONBs/National Landscapes remains to be seen.

---

## Gloucestershire context

The 20% BNG requirement applies to development in the CNL parts of Cotswolds District Council, Stroud District Council, Tewkesbury Borough Council, and Cheltenham Borough Council. Developers and their ecologists working in the CNL should be aware that the standard national BNG metric threshold of 10% is insufficient — the CNL requires 20%.
