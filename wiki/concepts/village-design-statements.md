# Village Design Statements

A Village Design Statement (VDS) is a community-produced document that describes the distinctive character of a village or parish and sets out design principles to guide new development. It is less formal than a Neighbourhood Development Plan but can still carry weight in planning decisions when adopted as a material consideration by the Local Planning Authority.

## What a VDS Does

A VDS typically covers:
- The historic and architectural character of the settlement
- Landscape setting and views
- Street patterns, building materials, and scale
- Green spaces, trees, and biodiversity features
- Guidance for new development to fit with local character

Unlike an NDP, a VDS does not allocate land or create planning policies — it provides **design guidance** that decision-makers are expected to have regard to.

## Status in Planning

A VDS has no statutory weight on its own. Its influence depends on:
- Whether the LPA has formally **adopted it as a material consideration** (supplementary planning guidance)
- Whether the Local Plan includes a policy referring to it
- Whether an NDP references it

Many LPAs in Gloucestershire adopt well-prepared VDS documents as supplementary planning documents, giving them more formal weight.

## VDS vs Neighbourhood Development Plan

| Village Design Statement | Neighbourhood Development Plan |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Design guidance only | Can include land allocations, policies, green space designations |
| No statutory weight alone | Statutory weight — part of the development plan |
| Cheaper and quicker to produce | Requires referendum, formal examination |
| No neighbourhood portion of CIL | NDP triggers 25% neighbourhood portion of CIL |
| Good starting point | More powerful but more demanding |

A VDS is often the first step a community takes — it builds understanding of local character and can form the evidence base for a future NDP.

## Nature and Biodiversity in a VDS

A VDS can be a valuable tool for embedding nature into local design expectations:
- Identifying important green spaces, trees, and hedgerows that new development should protect
- Specifying materials and design features that support wildlife (swift bricks, bat boxes, wildflower verges)
- Describing valued landscape features that should be preserved in new development
- Providing a basis for requiring [[SuDS]] features that complement local character

## Gloucestershire Context

Many Gloucestershire parishes have produced VDS documents, particularly in the Cotswolds where character protection is a strong community concern. The [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] management plan encourages local character guidance as part of good stewardship of the landscape.

For parishes thinking about protecting local nature, a VDS can be an accessible entry point — less demanding than an NDP but still a credible evidence base for commenting on planning applications.
