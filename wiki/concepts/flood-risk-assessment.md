# Flood Risk Assessment and Sequential Test

Development in areas at risk of flooding requires a **Flood Risk Assessment (FRA)** and — where it involves a vulnerable land use in a flood zone — must pass the **Sequential Test** and possibly the **Exception Test**. These requirements stem from the [[NPPF]] and are among the most significant planning constraints in Gloucestershire, where large areas of the Severn Vale are at flood risk.

## Flood Zones

The [[Environment Agency]] publishes **Flood Zone Maps** dividing land into:

| Flood Zone | Risk level | Probability of flooding in any year |
|------------|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Zone 1** | Low | Less than 0.1% (1 in 1,000 or lower) |
| **Zone 2** | Medium | 0.1%–1% (1 in 1,000 to 1 in 100) |
| **Zone 3a** | High | Greater than 1% (more than 1 in 100) |
| **Zone 3b (Functional Floodplain)** | Active floodplain | Land that floods frequently or is designed to flood |

Most sensitive to planning restrictions are Zone 3a and 3b.

## When an FRA Is Required

An FRA must accompany planning applications for development:
- In **Flood Zone 2 or 3** (any size)
- In Flood Zone 1 if the site is **more than 1 hectare**
- In Flood Zone 1 where a **Strategic Flood Risk Assessment** (the council's own) identifies site-specific risk
- Where drainage or run-off issues are identified regardless of zone

## The Sequential Test

The Sequential Test requires applicants to demonstrate that **no reasonably available lower-risk alternatives** exist for the proposed development. It applies when a development with "more vulnerable" or "highly vulnerable" land uses is proposed in Flood Zone 2 or 3.

The sequence of preference is:
1. **Zone 1** — always prefer where possible
2. **Zone 2** — only if Zone 1 alternatives are not available
3. **Zone 3a** — only if Zones 1 and 2 alternatives are not available
4. **Zone 3b** — only for water-compatible development and essential infrastructure

**Vulnerability classifications** determine which land uses can go where:

| Vulnerability class | Zone 3b | Zone 3a | Zone 2 |
|--------------------|---------|---------|--------|
| Highly vulnerable (e.g. care homes, basement dwellings) | Never | Never | Seq. + Exception Test |
| More vulnerable (e.g. houses, schools, hospitals) | Never | Seq. + Exception Test | Sequential Test |
| Less vulnerable (e.g. shops, offices, restaurants) | Never | Sequential Test | Permitted |
| Water compatible (e.g. boat houses, open space, SuDS) | Permitted | Permitted | Permitted |

## The Exception Test

Where the Sequential Test is satisfied but the development is still in a high-risk zone, the **Exception Test** may additionally be required. It has two parts — both must be passed:

1. **Benefits**: the development must provide wider sustainability benefits to the community that outweigh flood risk
2. **Safe for lifetime**: the development must be safe for its users for the full lifetime of the development, without increasing flood risk elsewhere

The Exception Test is hardest to pass for residential development in Zone 3a or 3b.

## What an FRA Must Cover

An FRA must assess:
- **Current flood risk**: all sources (fluvial, surface water, groundwater, sewer, reservoir)
- **Future flood risk**: accounting for climate change; the Environment Agency publishes allowances (Higher Central and Upper End scenarios)
- **Impact of development**: whether the development increases flood risk on or off site
- **Mitigation measures**: how risk is managed, including finished floor levels, flood-proofing, safe access and egress, and [[SuDS]] for surface water management
- **Residual risk**: the risk remaining after mitigation

Climate change allowances must use the **Upper End** scenario for planning purposes in most cases; this significantly increases the modelled flood extents.

## Relationship to SuDS and NFM

Flood risk in planning is closely linked to [[SuDS|SuDS requirements]] and Natural Flood Management:
- SuDS manage surface water on site and reduce post-development runoff
- NFM techniques (leaky dams, wetland storage, floodplain reconnection) address catchment-scale flood risk
- An FRA for larger sites should consider how [[SuDS]] contribute to flood risk reduction and what NFM opportunities exist in the vicinity

## The Strategic Flood Risk Assessment

LPAs must produce a **Strategic Flood Risk Assessment (SFRA)** covering their district, informing the Sequential Test at Local Plan level — i.e. site allocations should be located in lower flood risk zones where possible. SFRAs are publicly available documents from each Local Planning Authority.

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire has some of England's most significant flood risk:
- The **Severn Vale** (Vale of Gloucester, Tewkesbury, and Gloucester itself) floods regularly from the River Severn and River Avon — notably the 2007 floods (worst in 60 years) and 2019–2020 events
- **Tewkesbury** sits at the confluence of the Severn and Avon and has large Zone 3 areas
- The **Cotswolds Water Park** (mineral extraction lakes) has complex groundwater flood risk
- Many rural Gloucestershire villages have fords, floodplain field systems, and localised drainage issues that fall within Zone 2 or 3

The [[Gloucestershire County Council|Lead Local Flood Authority]] (GCC) is the statutory consultee for surface water and ordinary watercourse flood risk; the [[Environment Agency]] is consultee for main river flood risk.
