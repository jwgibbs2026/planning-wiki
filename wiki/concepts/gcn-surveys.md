# GCN Surveys

Surveys for great crested newts (GCN) are required before planning applications can be determined where there is a reasonable likelihood of GCN presence. This page covers when surveys are needed, what methods are used, and the validity requirements that apply.

---

## When Are Surveys Required?

A survey is triggered if **any** of the following apply:

- Distribution records (e.g. from Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records) suggest GCN may be present
- A suitable water body (pond or ditch) exists **within 500 metres** of the development
- The development includes refuges within 500m of aquatic habitats

The **500m rule** is the most commonly applied trigger in practice. Any pond or ditch within this distance should be assessed for suitability before a decision is made on whether surveys are needed.

---

## Survey Scope

- **Core survey zone**: water bodies within **250m** of the development boundary
- **Extended zone**: up to **500m** where there are no significant dispersal barriers (roads, urban areas, watercourses)

All water bodies within the relevant zone should be assessed for habitat suitability before deciding which require full GCN surveys.

---

## Survey Methods

### Habitat Suitability Index (HSI)
A desk-based and field assessment of pond and surrounding habitat quality. Scores ponds on factors including size, water quality, shading, and surrounding terrestrial habitat. The HSI score informs whether full presence/absence surveys are needed and helps predict population size.

### eDNA Sampling
Environmental DNA (eDNA) sampling involves collecting water samples from a pond and testing for GCN DNA shed into the water. It is:
- **Time-limited**: valid only between **mid-April and end of June**
- Non-invasive — no direct capture of newts required
- The standard modern method for confirming presence or absence
- A single negative eDNA result from a suitable pond is generally sufficient to confirm absence (subject to survey conditions being met)

### Population Surveys
Where GCN presence is confirmed or strongly suspected, population surveys may be needed to assess population size and inform impact assessment. Methods include:
- Torch surveys (night-time visual counts in ponds)
- Bottle trapping
- Refuge checks (artificial refuges placed in terrestrial habitat)
- Egg searches

Population surveys are typically conducted between **mid-March and mid-May**.

---

## Survey Data Currency

Survey data must be **no older than 4 survey seasons** to be considered valid for planning purposes. Data older than this must be refreshed — this is a common source of delay on planning applications where older ecological surveys are submitted.

A "survey season" runs from approximately mid-March to mid-June (the active aquatic season for GCN).

---

## Survey Standards

Surveys should meet industry standards. Relevant guidance is published by:
- Natural England (standing advice)
- The Chartered Institute of Ecology and Environmental Management (CIEEM)
- The Herpetological Conservation Trust

Surveyors conducting GCN surveys must hold a **Natural England survey licence** — it is illegal to handle GCN or disturb their habitat without one, even for survey purposes.

---

## Gloucestershire Context

The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) holds GCN distribution data for the county. Developers and LPAs should consult GCER records as the first step in assessing whether the 500m survey trigger applies. GCER data may also inform whether a development falls within a [[District Level Licensing]] scheme area.
