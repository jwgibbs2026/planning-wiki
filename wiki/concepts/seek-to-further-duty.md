# Seek to Further Duty

The "seek to further" duty is a legal obligation on **relevant authorities** to actively seek to further the purposes of protected landscape designation — specifically, the conservation and enhancement of natural beauty. It applies to Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty (AONBs) and National Parks.

---

## Legal basis

The duty was introduced by **Section 245 of the Levelling Up and Regeneration Act 2023**, which amended **Section 85 of the CROW Act 2000**. It came into force on **26 December 2023** and does not require secondary regulations to be operative.

The duty **replaces** the previous weaker "duty of regard" (which only required relevant authorities to have regard to the purposes of the designation). The new duty is:
- **Active and pro-active** — not merely passive avoidance of harm
- **Outcome-focused** — requires positive steps to further the purposes
- **Applicable inside and outside** the designation boundary
- **Proportionate** — requires documented evidence of compliance

Secondary regulations specifying how relevant authorities must comply are expected during the lifetime of the CNL Management Plan 2025–2030 (i.e., by approximately 2030).

---

## Who is a "relevant authority"?

Relevant authorities include local planning authorities, government departments, public bodies, and infrastructure providers whose decisions or activities could affect the CNL. This includes:
- Local planning authorities (LPAs) making planning decisions
- Highways authorities
- Water companies
- Government departments (including Defra and MHCLG)
- Infrastructure providers

---

## What the duty requires

The duty goes beyond simply avoiding harm. Relevant authorities must:
1. Take positive steps to further the conservation and enhancement of natural beauty
2. Document their reasoning proportionately
3. Apply the duty at both the **plan-making stage** (Local Plans, Neighbourhood Plans) and the **development management stage** (individual planning decisions)
4. Apply the duty to decisions affecting land **outside** the designation boundary where those decisions could affect the CNL

The duty does **not** preclude decisions that are net-harmful to the CNL, but it requires positive evidence that all reasonable steps have been taken to further the purposes before such a decision is made.

---

## Failure to apply the duty is an error of law

Failure to apply the duty is an **error of law** and can render a planning decision unlawful. This was illustrated by the Greater Anglia/Manningtree station case in Dedham Vale, where the Secretary of State accepted that failure to apply the duty in an EIA screening decision was an error in law.

A legal opinion from Landmark Chambers (2024, instructed by Campaign for National Parks) confirmed that it would "arguably amount to an error of law" to fail to consider NPPF paragraph 190 at the site allocations stage of plan-making.

---

## Compliance in practice

Evidence from the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] is concerning:
- Only **10%** of planning decisions in 2024 (out of 114 decisions the CNL Board commented on) explicitly referred to the s.85 "seek to further" duty
- Only **6%** adequately addressed it
- 56% of planning decisions were made in line with CNL Board recommendations (2023/24)

## Gloucestershire context

All local planning authorities with land in the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] — including Cotswolds District Council, Stroud District Council, Tewkesbury Borough Council, Cheltenham Borough Council, and [[Gloucestershire County Council]] — are relevant authorities subject to this duty. Parish and town councils preparing Neighbourhood Development Plans within the CNL are also expected to apply the duty.
