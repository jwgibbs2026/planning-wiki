# Ecological Impact Assessment

An Ecological Impact Assessment (EcIA) is the systematic process used to identify, predict, evaluate, and communicate the likely effects of a development on ecological receptors (species and habitats), and to identify measures to avoid, mitigate, or compensate for those effects. It is required for most planning applications that affect land with ecological value.

## The EcIA Process

### Stage 1 — Preliminary Ecological Appraisal (PEA)
Also called a Phase 1 Habitat Survey or desk study. A qualified ecologist visits the site to:
- Map habitats using the standard UK Habitat Classification
- Identify features with potential to support protected or notable species (bat roosts, reptile habitat, potential GCN ponds, etc.)
- Review existing data sources (GCER, NBN Atlas, MAGIC)
- Recommend targeted surveys where protected species potential is identified

The PEA produces a report that LPAs use to determine what further surveys are needed before an application can be determined.

### Stage 2 — Targeted Species Surveys
Based on the PEA findings, species-specific surveys are commissioned — for example:
- Bat surveys (emergence/re-entry surveys, transect surveys)
- Great crested newt eDNA or population surveys
- Dormouse nest tube surveys
- Water vole habitat assessment and field sign surveys
- Reptile surveys
- Breeding bird surveys

Survey timing is critical — most species have defined survey seasons, and surveys conducted at the wrong time of year are invalid.

### Stage 3 — Impact Assessment
The ecologist assesses the significance of predicted effects on ecological receptors, considering:
- **Magnitude**: how large is the effect?
- **Sensitivity**: how important is the receptor (nationally, regionally, locally)?
- **Significance**: combining magnitude and sensitivity to produce an overall significance judgment

### Stage 4 — Mitigation and Enhancement
The ecologist identifies measures to:
1. **Avoid** impacts (relocating the development footprint)
2. **Mitigate** unavoidable impacts (timing restrictions, habitat management, protective fencing)
3. **Compensate** for residual impacts (replacement habitat, Biodiversity Net Gain)

### Stage 5 — Reporting
The full EcIA report is submitted with the planning application. It must contain sufficient information for the LPA to make an informed decision.

## Professional Standards

EcIA must be carried out by suitably qualified ecologists. Professional standards are set by:
- **CIEEM** (Chartered Institute of Ecology and Environmental Management) — the professional body; their Competency Framework applies
- **BS 42020:2013** — British Standard for biodiversity in planning and development
- CIEEM's *Guidelines for Ecological Impact Assessment in the UK and Ireland*

LPAs and statutory consultees (Natural England, Environment Agency) will check that surveys follow current professional standards.

## Link to the Planning System

The EcIA report feeds directly into the LPA's decision-making:
- It identifies whether protected species are present, triggering the need for [[EPS Mitigation Licence|EPS licences]] or standing advice conditions
- It calculates the [[Biodiversity Metric|biodiversity metric]] baseline and proposed post-development score
- It informs [[habitats-regulations-assessment|HRA]] screening where SACs/SPAs are nearby
- It identifies impacts on Local Wildlife Sites or [[SSSI|SSSIs]]
- It supports or undermines the applicant's case depending on findings

## Gloucestershire Context

In Gloucestershire, the EcIA process routinely triggers targeted surveys for bats (very common), great crested newts (lowland areas with ponds), otters (near watercourses), dormice (hedgerows and woodland), and breeding birds. Early engagement with GCER to obtain existing species data is standard practice and helps scope the survey programme efficiently.
