# Species of Principal Importance

Species of Principal Importance (also called Section 41 species, or S41 species) are animals and plants listed under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006 as being of principal importance for the purpose of conserving biodiversity in England. The list identifies species of particular conservation concern that require action to maintain or recover their populations. It is maintained by Natural England and currently includes over 900 species and 65 habitats.

---

## Legal Basis

Section 41 of the NERC Act 2006 requires the Secretary of State to publish a list of living organisms and habitats of principal importance for conserving biodiversity in England. Public authorities — including local planning authorities — must have regard to this list when exercising their functions. This is part of the broader Biodiversity Duty on public bodies.

The S41 list is a material consideration in planning decisions. Where a development proposal would affect S41 species or their habitats, LPAs must take this into account when determining applications.

S41 status operates alongside — and independently of — the legal protections under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017, and the Protection of Badgers Act 1992.

---

## Relationship to Other Protections

S41 status is not a substitute for other legal protections — it is an additional layer:

- A species can be both EPS (under the Habitats Regulations) and S41 — for example, hazel dormice, great crested newts, and bats
- A species can be S41 without being EPS — for example, water voles (WCA 1981 Schedule 5, not EPS) and many bird species
- S41 status applies even where the species does not trigger a specific licensing requirement

In planning terms, S41 status means that harm to the species or its habitat is a material consideration that must be weighed in the planning balance — it cannot simply be ignored. This applies even where a species does not have the higher-tier EPS protection: hedgehogs, swifts, and house sparrows are S41 species but not EPS, and their presence on a development site is still a material planning consideration.

---

## Relationship to European Protected Species

Species of Principal Importance and [[European Protected Species]] are separate categories with different legal effects:

| Feature | EPS | Species of Principal Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Legal basis | Habitats Regulations 2017 | NERC Act 2006, S41 |
| Effect | Specific prohibitions; licensing required | Duty to have regard; material consideration in planning |
| Enforcement | Criminal offence to harm | No direct criminal sanction; planning policy lever |
| Examples | All bats, great crested newt, dormouse, otter | Bats (also EPS), hedgehog, swift, house sparrow, water vole, many others |

Many species appear on both lists. For bats, EPS status provides the stronger protection, but S41 listing reinforces the obligation on LPAs to consider bat conservation in planning decisions. Water voles are a notable case: they are **endangered** on England's mammal conservation list — a higher conservation urgency than many other S41 species — yet they sit outside the EPS tier, making their S41 status particularly important as a planning material consideration.

---

## Relationship to the Biodiversity Duty

The Biodiversity Duty under the Environment Act 2021 requires public authorities (including LPAs) to conserve and enhance biodiversity. S41 species are a key reference point for what "biodiversity" means in this context.

---

## Practical Implications for Planning

- **Survey trigger**: The presence of S41 species or their habitats near a development site is a reason to require ecological survey, even where species-specific EPS triggers may not apply
- **Material consideration**: Harm to S41 species can be a reason to refuse planning permission or impose conditions
- **Mitigation**: Harm to S41 species should be avoided or mitigated; residual harm must be weighed in the planning balance
- **Biodiversity Net Gain**: S41 species habitats are often priority habitats in the BNG metric; protecting and enhancing them contributes to BNG targets
- **NDP policies**: Neighbourhood Development Plans can include policies specifically protecting S41 species habitats — hedgerows, woodland edges, wetlands, and farmland habitats are all relevant in Gloucestershire

## Bat Species on the S41 List

Several bat species are listed as Species of Principal Importance, including some designated as vulnerable, endangered, or critically endangered on the GB Red List:

- Greater horseshoe bat
- Lesser horseshoe bat
- Bechstein's bat
- Barbastelle
- Brown long-eared bat

For these species, the combination of EPS status and S41 listing means that planning impacts must be assessed with particular care.

---

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire supports populations of several high-priority S41 species. Notable bat populations include lesser horseshoe bat and barbastelle in the Forest of Dean, and greater horseshoe bat in the Cotswolds. Many other S41 species — including hazel dormice, water voles, otters, great crested newts, and badgers — are also present across the county.

Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) holds distribution data for S41 species across the county. The Local Nature Recovery Strategy for Gloucestershire, when published, will identify priority areas and actions for S41 species recovery — this will be a material consideration in future planning decisions.
