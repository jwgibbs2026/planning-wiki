# Swifts in Planning

The common swift (*Apus apus*) is a [[Species of Principal Importance]] under the NERC Act 2006 (S41 species) and is protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (WCA 1981) as a wild bird. It has declined by approximately 60% in the UK since 1995 and is on the Red List (UK Birds of Conservation Concern). Swifts have become a specific and growing planning constraint for building renovation, demolition, and conversion.

## Why Swifts Are Different

Unlike most wild birds, swifts nest in buildings — specifically in **holes, gaps, and cavities** in older buildings, behind fascia boards, in soffits, and beneath roof tiles. They are **highly site-faithful**: the same birds return to the exact same nest hole year after year, for decades. Losing a nest site is effectively permanent — swifts are very unlikely to colonise new or different buildings even if nest boxes are provided nearby.

This site-fidelity means that **demolition or renovation of buildings with swift colonies can cause long-term population loss** from an area, even if the works are carried out outside the nesting season.

## Legal Protection

Swifts are wild birds protected under Section 1 of the WCA 1981:
- **Intentional killing, injuring, or taking** of swifts is an offence
- **Intentional taking, damaging, or destroying of nests in use or being built** is an offence
- **Intentional taking or destroying of eggs** is an offence

There is **no development licence** for wild birds — works that could damage or destroy an active nest cannot proceed during the nesting season regardless of planning permission. See [[Wild Birds in Planning]].

Unlike [[European Protected Species]], there is no derogation licence process for swifts. The only lawful approach is timing works to avoid the nesting season.

## Nesting Season

Swifts arrive in the UK from late April (occasionally late May in poor years) and depart by late August. The nesting season for planning purposes is broadly **May to August** — this is when nests and eggs are active and legal protection bites.

Unlike most garden birds, swifts are strict summer visitors. Building works carried out between September and late April are generally safe from the nesting season constraint, though the timing of return varies.

## Survey Triggers

A swift survey should be considered where:
- **Any building over 20–30 years old** is proposed for demolition, significant roof works, re-roofing, soffit replacement, or loft conversion
- Buildings with **gaps beneath tiles, in soffits, or behind fascia** that could provide nesting access
- Buildings in areas with **known swift colonies** (records from GCER/NBN Atlas or local swift surveys)
- Rural buildings (farm buildings, churches, village halls) — swifts are widespread in Gloucestershire's rural settlements

Swifts are audible and visible during the breeding season — screaming parties flying low around buildings in June and July are diagnostic. A dawn or dusk watch at the building is the primary survey method.

## Survey Method

Swift surveys are primarily **emergence/re-entry watches** at the building:
- **Timing**: dusk (when birds enter to roost) and dawn (when birds emerge)
- **Season**: May to August
- **Duration**: typically 1 hour at dawn or dusk
- **Evidence**: direct observation of birds entering or exiting the building; screaming parties; feathers, droppings, or nesting debris inside

A single negative survey does not rule out swifts — surveys should cover the full season if initial results are inconclusive.

## Mitigation

Where swifts are present or the building has suitable nesting habitat, mitigation includes:

1. **Timing works outside the nesting season** (September–April) — for any works affecting potential or confirmed nest sites
2. **Retention of existing nest holes** — where roof works are necessary, existing gaps and cavities should be retained or reinstated
3. **Swift bricks and boxes** — purpose-built nest cavities integrated into new or renovated buildings; swift bricks (hollow building blocks with an internal chamber) are the most durable option; external boxes are less permanent but acceptable
4. **Ratio**: typically 2–3 swift bricks provided for each nest site lost as a minimum; often more for new developments in areas with known colonies

## Planning Policy

Many LPAs are now requiring swift bricks or boxes as conditions on planning permissions for:
- New residential and commercial buildings in areas with swift records
- Conversion of older buildings in villages and towns
- Any development that removes known swift nest sites

The [[NPPF]] requires planning to protect and enhance biodiversity; swifts as a red-listed S41 species carry weight as a material consideration. The Cotswolds National Landscape Management Plan 2025-2030 specifically references swift-friendly design.

## Gloucestershire Context

Swifts are widespread breeding birds across Gloucestershire's market towns and villages — Cirencester, Cheltenham, Stroud, Gloucester, Tewkesbury, and many rural settlements have established colonies. Churches and older stone buildings are particularly important. Colony surveys have been conducted in several Gloucestershire towns by local swift groups.
