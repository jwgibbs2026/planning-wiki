# Biodiversity Gain Plan

A Biodiversity Gain Plan (BGP) is the formal document that a developer must submit — and the Local Planning Authority must approve — to demonstrate that a development meets the mandatory Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) requirement. It is a statutory pre-commencement condition attached to every planning permission subject to BNG.

## Legal Basis

The BGP requirement is set out in **Schedule 7A of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990**, as inserted by the Environment Act 2021. The LPA **cannot** allow development to commence until the BGP has been submitted and approved. This is a true pre-commencement condition — it cannot be waived or discharged by a general condition.

## What a BGP Must Contain

The BGP must set out:

1. **Baseline habitat** — the pre-development habitat units calculated using the [[Biodiversity Metric]], as agreed with or checked by the LPA
2. **Onsite habitat creation/enhancement** — what habitat will be created or improved within the development site, and its predicted post-development unit value
3. **Offsite habitat units** — where onsite gain is insufficient, the BGP must specify off-site habitat parcels registered on the **Biodiversity Gain Site Register** (or a S106 agreement or [[Conservation Covenants|conservation covenant]] securing the habitat)
4. **Statutory biodiversity credits** — only where off-site habitat units cannot be sourced; credits are purchased from the government and fund strategic habitat creation nationally
5. **Habitat management and monitoring plan** — how habitats will be managed to reach and maintain target condition, by whom, and for the full 30-year minimum period
6. **Responsibility and funding** — who is legally responsible for on-site and off-site habitats and how management is funded in perpetuity

## The Priority Order for Habitat Gain

The BGP must follow the statutory priority order:
1. **On-site** habitat creation and enhancement first
2. **Off-site** registered habitat units if on-site is insufficient
3. **Statutory biodiversity credits** only as a last resort

An LPA can refuse to approve a BGP that jumps straight to credits without demonstrating that on-site and off-site options are genuinely insufficient.

## Approving the BGP

The LPA must approve or refuse the BGP within **8 weeks** of submission (or a longer period agreed with the applicant). The LPA will check:
- The [[Biodiversity Metric]] calculation is correct and the baseline is accurate
- Habitat types proposed are appropriate to the site and local context
- Off-site habitats are genuinely secured (registered on the Gain Site Register or secured by S106/covenant)
- The management plan is credible and funding is assured
- The 10% gain (or 20% in the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]]) is achieved

The LPA may consult Natural England on the BGP, particularly for complex sites or where nationally important habitats are involved.

## Monitoring and Reporting

Once approved, the BGP locks in the habitat commitments for the lifetime of the development. Developers (or their successors) must:
- Submit **monitoring reports** to the LPA at specified intervals (typically year 2, year 5, then every 5 years to year 30)
- Demonstrate habitats are reaching target condition
- Rectify habitats that fail to reach condition within the agreed timescale

The LPA can take enforcement action if habitats fail and are not remediated. This is a relatively new enforcement area and many LPAs are still building monitoring capacity.

## Relationship to the Biodiversity Metric

The BGP is built on the outputs of the [[Biodiversity Metric]] calculation:
- The metric spreadsheet calculates baseline units and predicted post-development units
- The BGP translates those numbers into a legally binding habitat delivery plan
- They are distinct documents: the metric is the calculation tool; the BGP is the planning document committing to delivery

## Exemptions

Not all development requires a BGP. Exemptions include:
- **Householder applications** (extensions, outbuildings to a dwellinghouse)
- **Permitted development** not requiring planning permission
- **Small sites** — phased in; check current statutory instrument for current thresholds
- **Biodiversity net gain exemptions** apply to some temporary development and development with minimal ecological footprint

> Note: the small sites exemption has been subject to phased implementation — verify current status against the most recent statutory instrument.

## Gloucestershire Context

Each Gloucestershire LPA has its own approach to reviewing BGPs. As BNG is relatively new (mandatory from February 2024), LPA capacity varies. In the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]], the 20% requirement means BGPs from sites in or near the Cotswolds must demonstrate higher gain than the national minimum.

The GCER can advise on habitat type selection that aligns with LNRS priorities, which may attract the strategic significance multiplier in the [[Biodiversity Metric]].
