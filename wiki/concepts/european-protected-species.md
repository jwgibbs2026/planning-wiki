# European Protected Species

European Protected Species (EPS) are animals and plants afforded the highest tier of legal protection in England. The designation originates from the EU Habitats Directive and is implemented in domestic law through the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010 (as consolidated and amended in 2017), which remains in force as retained EU law following Brexit.

Understanding EPS status is essential for anyone involved in planning — developers, LPAs, parish councils, and communities — because the presence of EPS on or near a development site triggers specific legal obligations that cannot be waived or ignored.

---

## Legal Framework

The Habitats Regulations make it an offence to:

- Deliberately capture, injure, or kill an EPS animal
- Deliberately disturb EPS animals (including impairing their ability to survive, breed, rear young, hibernate, or migrate; or significantly affecting local distribution or abundance)
- Damage or destroy a **breeding site or resting place** of an EPS animal — even when the animals are not present at the time
- Deliberately destroy their eggs
- Possess, transport, sell, or exchange EPS animals or plants

The protection of breeding sites and resting places is particularly significant in planning. A bat roost in a building is protected whether or not bats are physically present when works begin. The same applies to great crested newt ponds, otter holts, and dormouse nesting sites. This protection applies year-round, regardless of whether the species is currently using the site.

Importantly, these prohibitions apply regardless of whether harm is intentional. A developer who proceeds without adequate survey and mitigation may commit an offence even if no harm was intended.

Additional protection for most EPS also applies under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

## Species with EPS Status in England

Species are listed as EPS in Schedule 2 of the Habitats Regulations. The EPS most commonly encountered in planning applications in England include:

| Species | Key Planning Trigger |
|---|---|
| **Bats** (all 18 species) | Roosts in buildings and trees; dark corridors; lighting impacts |
| **Great crested newt** | Ponds within 500m; terrestrial habitat within 250–500m |
| **Hazel dormouse** | Hedgerows, woodland, and scrub habitat; connectivity |
| **Otter** | Watercourses; culverting; bank works; riparian habitat |
| **White-clawed crayfish** | Watercourses in suitable catchments |
| **Sand lizard** | Suitable heathland or brownfield habitat |
| **Smooth snake** | Suitable heathland habitat |

See [[Bats in Planning]], [[Great Crested Newts in Planning]], [[Otters in Planning]], and [[Hazel Dormice in Planning]] for detailed species-specific guidance.

## EPS and the Planning System

EPS status makes a species a **mandatory material consideration** in planning decisions.

### LPA Gatekeeping Duty

Local planning authorities must:
1. Identify whether EPS could be affected by a proposed development
2. Request surveys where there is reasonable likelihood of EPS presence
3. Assess the likely impact of the development on the species
4. Apply the mitigation hierarchy (avoid → mitigate → compensate)
5. Confirm that Natural England will be able to issue a mitigation licence **before** granting permission

This last obligation is procedurally significant. LPAs cannot simply grant permission and leave licensing to be sorted out later. Planning permission should not be granted where it would be impossible to satisfy the three-part licensing test.

### The Three-Part Licensing Test

Natural England can only issue an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] if all three conditions are satisfied:

1. **Overriding public interest** — the development is for an imperative reason of overriding public interest (IROPI), for preserving public health or safety, or another permitted purpose under the Regulations
2. **No satisfactory alternative** — there is no other way to achieve the objective that would cause less harm to EPS
3. **Favourable conservation status** — the licence will not be detrimental to the favourable conservation status of the species

If any of these conditions cannot be met, a licence cannot be issued — and development that would harm EPS cannot lawfully proceed.

See [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] for the full licensing process, including [[District Level Licensing]] as an alternative route for great crested newts.

## Surveys and the Precautionary Principle

A key principle in EPS planning is that **absence of records does not indicate absence of species**. LPAs and developers cannot avoid survey obligations by pointing to a lack of existing data. Where there is reasonable potential for EPS to be present, surveys must be carried out.

Surveys must be:
- Seasonally appropriate (different species require surveys at different times of year)
- Carried out by suitably licensed and qualified surveyors
- Sufficient to identify species, population size, and site importance

Survey data currency rules differ by species and should not be conflated:

| Species | Survey currency rule |
|---|---|
| Bats | Varies by survey type; typically within 3 years |
| Great crested newts | Four-season data required |
| Otters | Typically within 1–2 years for active sites |
| Hazel dormouse | Up to 3 years if habitats unchanged |

## Key EPS in Gloucestershire Planning

Four species account for the vast majority of EPS issues encountered in English planning applications.

### Bats
All 18 UK bat species are EPS and are the most commonly encountered EPS in planning. Survey triggers include roost features in buildings and trees, dark flight corridors, and lighting impacts. A detailed seasonal survey calendar applies under the BCT Good Practice Guidelines. See [[Bats in Planning]] and [[Bat Surveys]].

### Great Crested Newts
Great crested newts (GCN) are the second most commonly encountered EPS in planning. Distinctive features include the 500m pond survey trigger, eDNA methodology, HSI scoring, and the 2-pond minimum compensation rule. [[District Level Licensing]] (DLL) offers a population-scale alternative to site-by-site licensing. See [[Great Crested Newts in Planning]] and [[GCN Surveys]].

### Otters
Otters are increasingly encountered in planning near watercourses. Water proximity is the primary survey trigger. Distinctive compensation measures include mammal ledges, artificial holts, and underpasses. **There is no District Level Licensing equivalent for otters** — every case requires a site-by-site licence from Natural England. See [[Otters in Planning]].

### Hazel Dormouse
The hazel dormouse is a vulnerable Red List mammal with EPS status. Survey triggers include woodland, hedgerow, and scrub habitats. See [[Hazel Dormice in Planning]].

## Key Distinctions from Other Protected Species Regimes

EPS protection under the Habitats Regulations is distinct from several other wildlife protection regimes:

- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Wild birds are protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, not the Habitats Regulations. Critically, **no development licence exists** for bird harm — avoidance is mandatory.
- **[[Badgers in Planning]]**: Badgers are protected under the dedicated Protection of Badgers Act 1992, not the Habitats Regulations. The three-part EPS test does not apply.
- **[[Water Voles in Planning]]**: Water voles are protected under WCA 1981 (Schedule 5), not as EPS. A simplified Class Licence (CL31) route exists for small-scale impacts.
- **[[Species of Principal Importance]]**: Listing under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006 creates a separate duty for public bodies and makes species a material consideration in planning even where EPS procedural obligations do not strictly apply.
- **Biodiversity Net Gain**: BNG requirements apply independently of EPS obligations.

## Multi-Species EPS Sites

Many development sites — particularly rural sites, riparian sites, and brownfield land — trigger surveys for more than one species simultaneously. Where multiple EPS are present, the following tensions can arise:

### Timing conflicts
- **Bats and otters**: Bat mitigation typically restricts nighttime working and lighting near roosts. Otter mitigation also restricts nighttime working (otters are crepuscular/nocturnal) and requires low lighting near watercourses. These requirements generally reinforce each other — both push toward daytime working with controlled lighting.
- **Bats and barn owls**: Bat surveys require dusk emergence/dawn re-entry watches, but barn owl survey watches are also at dusk/dawn. Both can be combined in the same survey visit for efficiency.
- **Dormice and construction clearance**: Dormouse exclusion requires slow, progressive clearance during the active season (April–October). If bats are also present in adjacent trees, tree-felling may require bat emergence surveys before each tree is felled.

### Licence sequencing
Where multiple EPS licences are needed, they must be applied for separately from Natural England — there is no combined multi-species licence. The most restrictive species' timing requirements govern the programme. Where a site has bats (seasonal survey requirements), GCN (eDNA survey only valid April–June), and dormice (nest tube surveys May–October), the survey programme must be planned at least a full season ahead of the planning application.

### Practical advice
For sites with multiple EPS triggers, instruct an ecologist early — ideally at pre-application stage — to design a single coordinated survey programme covering all species, avoiding the need for repeat site visits across multiple seasons.

## Gloucestershire Context

EPS are encountered regularly in Gloucestershire planning applications. Bats are present across the county; great crested newts are widespread in lowland areas; dormice are found in hedgerow-rich landscapes; otters are present along the county's river network. The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds species distribution data, which can help identify survey triggers. Natural England is the statutory body responsible for issuing EPS licences and providing standing advice to LPAs.
