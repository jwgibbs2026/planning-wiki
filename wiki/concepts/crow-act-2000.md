# CROW Act 2000

The Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000 (CROW Act) introduced the "right to roam" over open access land in England and Wales, and strengthened protections for [[SSSI|Sites of Special Scientific Interest]].

## Right to Roam

The Act introduced a new right of public access to:
- Mountains
- Moors
- Heath
- Down
- Registered common land

This right does not extend to cultivated land, private gardens, or most forestry. It is separate from the Public Rights of Way network (footpaths, bridleways).

## SSSI Strengthening

The Act significantly strengthened the legal protection for SSSIs by:
- Making it an offence to carry out operations that damage an SSSI without prior consent from Natural England
- Introducing civil sanctions (and criminal sanctions for the most serious damage)
- Giving Natural England powers to take management agreements and restoration orders

## Wildlife Crime

The Act also introduced provisions to tackle wildlife crime, including the power to create "wildlife enhancement schemes" and take action against persistent offenders.

## Relevance to Planning

LPAs and developers must ensure that development proposals in or near SSSIs comply with the SSSI protection provisions of the CROW Act. Damage to an SSSI during construction is a serious criminal offence.
