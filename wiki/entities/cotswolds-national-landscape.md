# Cotswolds National Landscape

The Cotswolds National Landscape (CNL) is the **largest Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty (AONB) in England** and the third largest protected landscape in England. It covers 787 square miles (2,038 km²) and spans 15 local authority areas across Gloucestershire, Oxfordshire, Wiltshire, Warwickshire, Worcestershire, Bath & North East Somerset, and South Gloucestershire.

The designation was created in 1966 and extended in 1990. The area was rebranded as a "National Landscape" in June 2020, though AONB remains the legal designation. It is classified as an IUCN Category V Protected Landscape.

---

## Governance

The [[Cotswolds National Landscape Board]] (legal name: Cotswolds Conservation Board) is the statutory body responsible for managing the CNL. It was established by Parliamentary Order in 2004 and is one of only two Conservation Boards in England (the other being the Chilterns Conservation Board). The Board does not own or directly manage any land in the CNL.

The Board has two statutory purposes:
1. Conserve and enhance the natural beauty of the CNL
2. Increase public understanding and enjoyment of its special qualities

It also has a duty to foster the economic and social wellbeing of people living in the CNL. The [[Sandford Principle]] applies: when the two statutory purposes conflict, conservation takes priority.

## Gloucestershire context

[[Gloucestershire County Council]] covers 63.7% of the CNL area. Cotswolds District Council covers 43.8% and is the largest single local planning authority within the CNL. Stroud District Council covers 10.9%, Tewkesbury Borough Council covers 8.5%, and Cheltenham Borough Council covers 0.5%.

## Special qualities

The CNL's special qualities (defined in the Management Plan) include:
- Limestone geology and its use as a building material
- The Cotswold escarpment and its views
- High wolds — open elevated farmed landscape
- River valleys (mostly Thames headwaters)
- Dry stone walls
- Flower-rich grasslands (limestone, floodplain meadow, neutral)
- Ancient broadleaved woodland along the escarpment crest
- Variations in stone colour
- Tranquillity
- Extensive dark sky areas
- Distinctive Cotswold vernacular settlements
- Accessible landscape for quiet recreation (including the Cotswold Way National Trail)
- Archaeological and historic associations spanning 6,000 years
- Cultural heritage (Arts and Crafts Movement, Cotswolds Olimpicks)

## Nature and ecology

The CNL is a stronghold for a wide range of priority habitats and species. The [[Cotswolds Nature Recovery Plan]] (adopted October 2021) sets out the highest priority habitats as veteran trees, ancient woodland, and ancient unimproved limestone grassland. The current wildlife-rich habitat extent is 48,000 Ha (23% of the CNL); the target for a robust nature recovery network is 82,000 Ha (40%) by 2050, rising to over 190,000 Ha if arable fields with environmental measures are included.

Wildflower-rich grassland has declined dramatically — from 40% of the CNL area in the 1930s to less than 1.5% today, primarily due to agricultural intensification.

Six Local Nature Recovery Strategies are coincident with the CNL. The CNL Management Plan is used to influence all six.

Priority species include bats, hazel dormice, water voles, white-clawed crayfish, farmland birds (skylark, lapwing, corn bunting), pearl-bordered fritillary, marsh fritillary, pasqueflower, and Cotswold pennycress.

## Water environment

The CNL is divided across 7 catchments, 4 water companies, and 3 Environment Agency regions. Most of the CNL is designated as a Drinking Water Safeguard Zone and a Nitrate Vulnerable Zone. There are 14 groundwater bodies (50% in good condition, 50% in poor condition). None of the CNL's rivers achieve good chemical status; only 11.7% achieve good ecological status. The Cotswolds Aquifer is a large limestone aquifer supplying water inside and outside the CNL.

## Planning significance

The CNL Management Plan 2025–2030 is a **material consideration in planning decisions**. The CNL has the highest level of protection in the planning system, equivalent to National Parks. Under the [[Seek to Further Duty]] (in force since 26 December 2023), relevant authorities must actively seek to further the conservation and enhancement of the CNL's natural beauty — not merely avoid harm.

The Biodiversity Net Gain requirement within the CNL is **20%** — double the national mandatory minimum of 10%. This is justified in Appendix 9 of the Management Plan on the basis that 10% BNG only ensures no net loss, not genuine enhancement.

Major development in the CNL is subject to the exceptional circumstances test under NPPF paragraphs 190–191 and footnote 67. This is not a simple planning balance — proposals must be refused unless exceptional circumstances and public interest criteria are both met.

10 of 15 LPAs (76%) have adopted Local Plans with policy-level reference to the CNL Management Plan. Only 10% of planning decisions in 2024 explicitly referred to the "seek to further" duty; only 6% adequately addressed it.
