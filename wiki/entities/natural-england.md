# Natural England

Natural England is the government's statutory adviser on the natural environment in England. Created by the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006, it operates as an executive non-departmental public body (arm's-length body) sponsored by the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra). Its role spans nature conservation, landscape protection, public access to the countryside, and the regulation of activities affecting protected species and habitats.

---

## Role in the Planning System

Natural England is a statutory consultee in the planning process. LPAs must consult Natural England on planning applications that could affect:
- Sites of Special Scientific Interest ([[SSSI]])
- European designated sites (Special Areas of Conservation, Special Protection Areas, Ramsar sites)
- [[European Protected Species]] and other protected species
- Nationally significant habitats and landscapes
- National Parks and Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty
- Biodiversity Net Gain metric and habitat bank registration

Natural England's primary planning functions are:

- **Statutory consultee** — responses carry significant weight as material planning considerations
- **Standing advice publisher** — provides guidance to LPAs on protected species and habitats that is a material planning consideration
- **EPS licence issuer** — grants [[EPS Mitigation Licence|EPS mitigation licences]] for activities affecting [[European Protected Species]]
- **SSSI notifier** — designates and manages [[SSSI|Sites of Special Scientific Interest]]
- **BNG metric** — publishes the statutory Biodiversity Metric used to calculate Biodiversity Net Gain and oversees the BNG register
- **LNRS oversight** — supports the preparation of Local Nature Recovery Strategies
- **S41 list** — maintains the [[Species of Principal Importance]] list
- **Ancient Woodland Inventory** — maintains the inventory of ancient woodland in England
- **GI Framework** — published the Green Infrastructure Framework setting out 15 principles for planning and creating green infrastructure

For most protected species matters, Natural England provides **standing advice** — published guidance that LPAs and developers should follow without needing to consult Natural England directly on each case. Standing advice is a material planning consideration.

## Standing Advice

For many common protected species and habitat types, Natural England publishes **standing advice** — guidance that LPAs should apply as a material consideration without needing to consult Natural England directly on every application. This advice is authoritative; LPAs and developers are expected to follow it, and departing from it without good reason creates legal risk.

## Licensing

Natural England is the licensing authority for protected species in England. It issues licences across several legal frameworks:

### EPS Mitigation Licences
Issued for activities affecting [[European Protected Species]] (including bats, great crested newts, otters, and hazel dormice) where the three-part test is met (overriding public interest; no satisfactory alternative; favourable conservation status maintained). Also available as:
- **Class licences** — streamlined routes for lower-risk activities
- **Bat Earned Recognition (Bat ER)** — accreditation for competent surveyors and developers

### Survey Licences
Required for ecologists handling certain protected species during surveys, including hazel dormice.

### Badger Licences
Issued under the Protection of Badgers Act 1992 for development affecting badger setts — see [[Badgers in Planning]].

### Standard Mitigation Licences and Class Licences
Issued for species protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (not EPS), including:
- Water voles — site-by-site standard mitigation licences, or **Class Licence CL31** for impacts on less than 50 metres of bank — see [[Water Voles in Planning]]

### Wild Bird Licences
Limited licences are available for public health, air safety, scientific research, and crop protection only. **No development licence exists for wild birds.** See [[Wild Birds in Planning]].

The most commonly encountered EPS in planning, and their available licensing routes, are:

| Species | Licensing Route | DLL Available? |
|---|---|---|
| Bats | Site-by-site EPS licence | Being explored |
| Great Crested Newts | Site-by-site or [[District Level Licensing]] | Yes |
| Otters | Site-by-site EPS licence only | No |
| Hazel Dormice | Site-by-site EPS licence | No |

LPAs must be satisfied that a licence will be granted before approving development that would affect EPS. Natural England's assessment of licence likelihood is therefore a critical input to planning decisions.

For Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects, Natural England confirmation is required separately for all species.

## SSSI Notification and Management

Natural England notifies SSSIs — the most important wildlife sites in England. It has powers to:
- Notify new SSSIs and extend existing ones
- Issue management notices to landowners
- Consent or object to operations likely to damage SSSIs
- Take enforcement action against SSSI damage

---

## Biodiversity Net Gain

Natural England administers key elements of the Biodiversity Net Gain system introduced by the Environment Act 2021, including:
- The statutory biodiversity metric
- Registration of off-site habitat banks
- Guidance on BNG implementation

## Green Infrastructure Framework

Natural England has published a Green Infrastructure Framework setting out 15 principles for planning and creating green infrastructure. See Natural England GI Framework.

## Gloucestershire Context

Natural England's Gloucestershire and Avon area team covers Gloucestershire and is the point of contact for:
- EPS licence applications affecting Gloucestershire sites
- SSSI management and consents
- Advice on Local Nature Recovery Strategy development
- Confirmation of [[District Level Licensing]] availability in Gloucestershire districts

Key designated sites in Gloucestershire include:
- Severn Estuary SAC/SPA/Ramsar
- Cotswolds Beechwoods SAC
- Forest of Dean and Wye Valley SACs
- Numerous SSSIs across all six districts

Key local ecological issues include:
- Great crested newt populations across the county
- Bat commuting routes and roosts in the Severn Vale and Cotswolds
- Otter and water vole populations on the Severn and its tributaries
- Ancient woodland in the Forest of Dean and Cotswold escarpment
- Hazel dormouse populations in hedgerow-rich and woodland landscapes

For most protected species matters in Gloucestershire, developers and LPAs should follow Natural England's standing advice rather than consulting the area team directly. The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records works alongside Natural England as the county-level repository for species distribution and habitat data.
