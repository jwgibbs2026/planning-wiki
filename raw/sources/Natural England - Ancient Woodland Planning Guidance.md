# Ancient Woodland, Ancient Trees and Veteran Trees: Planning Guidance

**Source**: Natural England and Forestry Commission standing advice (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/ancient-woodland-ancient-trees-and-veteran-trees-advice-for-making-planning-decisions
**Type**: Standing advice — material planning consideration

---

## Overview

This guidance from Natural England and the Forestry Commission provides standing advice for assessing planning applications affecting ancient woodland, ancient trees, or veteran trees in England. It is a material planning consideration for local planning authorities.

## Ancient Woodland Definition

Any area that has been wooded continuously since at least 1600 AD. This includes:

- Ancient semi-natural woodland with native trees and shrubs
- Plantations on ancient woodland sites with retained ancient features
- Wood pastures identified as ancient
- Historic parkland (protected as heritage assets)

These ecosystems are irreplaceable habitats valued for wildlife, soils, carbon storage, genetic diversity, recreation, and cultural significance.

## Ancient and Veteran Trees

**Ancient Trees:** Exceptionally valuable individual or grouped trees characterised by great age, size, significant wood decay, biodiversity value, and cultural/heritage significance.

**Veteran Trees:** Trees with significant decay features, such as branch death and hollowing, contributing to exceptional biodiversity and heritage value. All ancient trees are veteran trees, but not vice versa.

Ancient and veteran trees frequently occur outside woodlands — in hedgerows, parks, orchards, and pastures.

## Planning Decision Framework

Local authorities should refuse permission if development causes loss or deterioration unless:

1. **Wholly exceptional reasons** exist, AND
2. A suitable compensation strategy is in place

The guidance emphasises that ancient woodland, ancient trees and veteran trees are **irreplaceable**, so compensation cannot alone justify approval.

## Assessing Development Impacts

### Direct Effects

Development can damage these assets through:
- Physical destruction of trees, soils, ground flora
- Root and soil damage/compaction
- Pollution increases (air, light, noise)
- Water table changes
- Habitat fragmentation and wind damage

### Indirect Effects

Development can cause deterioration by:
- Severing ecological connections between woodlands
- Reducing adjacent semi-natural habitat
- Increasing disturbance and pollution
- Increasing trespassing and trampling
- Requiring management that harms habitat

## Mitigation Hierarchy

The guidance requires application of a three-stage approach:

1. **Avoidance:** Select alternative sites or redesign schemes to prevent impacts entirely
2. **Mitigation:** Where unavoidable impacts occur, implement protective measures — screening barriers, noise reduction, buffer zones, footpath rerouting
3. **Compensation:** As a last resort — woodland creation, habitat restoration, invasive species removal, long-term management plans

## Buffer Zone Requirements

- **Ancient woodlands:** At least 15 metres from the boundary of the woodland to avoid root damage — larger zones needed for additional impacts such as air pollution
- **Individual ancient or veteran trees:** At least 15 times larger than the diameter of the tree, or 5 metres from canopy edge (whichever is larger)

Buffer zones should consist of semi-natural habitats and contribute to ecological networks.

## Compensation Measures

Compensation can only partially offset losses and serves as a final resort:

- Creating native woodland or wood pasture
- Improving existing woodland condition
- Removing invasive species
- Restoring plantations on ancient woodland sites
- Creating ecological connections via green bridges or hedgerows
- Developing long-term management plans with monitoring

It is not possible to replicate the same conditions at another site — translocation of ancient woodland is unacceptable.

## Evidence Requirements

Developers must provide:
- Tree surveys following British Standard BS 5837
- Ecological surveys following best practice guidance
- Sufficient evidence to support planning decisions, potentially including fieldwork and historical map analysis
