# Nature Restoration Fund — Implementation Plan

**Source**: MHCLG / Natural England (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/nature-restoration-fund-implementation-plan/implementing-the-nature-restoration-fund
**Type**: Government implementation plan
**Legislation**: Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025
**Status**: Secondary legislation and digital service in development; first EDPs expected Spring–Summer 2026

---

## Overview

The Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025 established the Nature Restoration Fund (NRF) as an alternative mechanism for developers to meet environmental obligations. Rather than site-specific mitigation, the NRF pools developer payments to fund strategic, large-scale conservation through Natural England.

---

## Core Mechanism

The NRF operates through **Environmental Delivery Plans (EDPs)** — strategic conservation packages covering specific geographic areas or habitat/species types.

- Developer payments are pooled; Natural England contracts conservation delivery through competitive tendering
- Upon approval of an EDP, participating developers are exempt from individual habitat assessments or species licences for covered impacts
- Participation is voluntary in most cases

---

## Three Implementation Phases

### Phase 1: EDP Creation
- Natural England develops draft EDPs using scientific evidence
- Each EDP covers: environmental features, development impacts, maps, conservation measures, and charging schedules
- Minimum 28-day public consultation
- Secretary of State approves only if conservation measures will "materially outweigh the negative effects of development"

### Phase 2: Developer Use
- Developers access online platforms for levy estimates
- EDP approval replaces required habitat assessments or species licences
- Payment on agreed schedule (e.g. after development begins)
- Natural England contracts conservation delivery via competitive tendering

### Phase 3: Monitoring and Reporting
- Annual organisational reports track fund progress
- EDP-specific reports at midpoint and completion
- Backup measures if targets appear insufficient
- Secretary of State can amend or revoke EDPs if the improvement test is unlikely to be met

---

## Key Safeguards

- **Overall improvement test**: Conservation measures must materially outweigh negative effects
- Continuous monitoring and mandatory remedial actions
- Published remedial statements if targets are at risk
- Secretary of State power to amend or revoke EDPs

---

## Initial Priority: Nutrient Pollution

First EDPs address nutrient neutrality requirements, which currently block approximately 16,500 homes per year. Targeted catchments include:
- Peak District Dales, Poole Harbour, River Avon, Axe, Camel, Clun, Eden, Kent, Lambourne, Lugg, Mease
- Solent, Somerset Levels and Moors, Stodmarsh, Teesmouth and Cleveland Coast, The Broads

Natural England's existing Nutrient Mitigation Scheme will transition into relevant EDPs.

## Implementation Timeline

| Period | Actions |
|--------|---------|
| Winter 2025 | Pre-market engagement; stakeholder consultation; secondary legislation development |
| Spring–Summer 2026 | Digital service user testing; guidance publication; secondary legislation before Parliament; formal nutrient EDP consultations; first EDPs go live |

---

## Future Expansion

Government intends to expand beyond nutrient pollution to cover other protected species and habitats. Great crested newt District Level Licensing is a likely next phase (building on its 25-year outcomes framework). Parliament will receive initial learnings before additional EDPs on other environmental issues are introduced.

---

## Guiding Principles

- Evidence-based nature recovery
- Unlocking sustainable development
- National and local stakeholder engagement
- Supporting nature markets through competitive delivery
- Community access to restored landscapes
- Rigorous impact monitoring
