## Overview

The Gloucestershire Local Nature Recovery Strategy (LNRS) is the statutory spatial strategy for nature recovery across the county, required by the Environment Act 2021. Gloucestershire County Council led its preparation with expert partners. It maps existing biodiversity value and identifies opportunities for nature recovery across the whole county — city and countryside, woodland, wetland, hills and valleys.

The document has 84 pages in two parts:
- **Part 1**: Biodiversity and Opportunities for Nature Recovery (this document)
- **Part 2**: Potential Measures (detailed site-specific habitat recommendations)

---

## Purpose and Legal Status

The LNRS is a statutory document with three core legal functions:

1. **Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG)**: Recommends where and what habitat creation should be incorporated in BNG projects. LPAs and developers must use the LNRS to identify priority locations for off-site BNG.
2. **Biodiversity Duty for Public Authorities**: Local authorities and other public bodies must "have regard to" the LNRS in local planning processes, Strategic Environmental Assessments, and Infrastructure Delivery Plans.
3. **Agri-environment funding**: Guides farmers, landowners, and land managers to the best locations for habitat management funding (e.g. Sustainable Farming Incentive, Countryside Stewardship).

> The strategy does not force owners or managers to make changes — it shows priority opportunities and options.

## Gloucestershire's Biodiversity Context

### Key statistics
- 36,028 hectares of woodland (69% under sustainable management)
- 75% of land in agricultural or horticultural use
- Urban areas: ~11% of county
- Dominant natural habitat: broadleaved woodland (~13%)
- 90% decline in coppiced woodland from 1900–1970
- 97% decline in lowland meadow in England and Wales from 1930–1984
- <1.5% of wildflower-rich meadows remain in the Cotswolds (down from 40%)

### Designated sites
**International**: 
- Wye Valley Woodlands SAC (beech forests, limestone woodland)
- Walmore Common SPA (overwintering Bewick's swans)
- Severn Estuary SAC/SPA/Ramsar (estuaries, salt meadows, migratory birds)
- Cotswold Beechwoods SAC (westernmost extensive beech forests in UK)
- Rodborough Common SAC (species-rich calcareous grassland)
- Dixton Wood SAC (violet click beetle)

**National**: 
- Cotswold Commons and Beechwoods NNR (7.5 km², largest nature reserve in the Cotswolds)
- Highbury Wood NNR
- Numerous SSSIs

**Local**: Local Nature Reserves (LNRs) and Local Wildlife Sites — protected through national and local planning policy.

### Priority species
Gloucestershire is a stronghold for: large blue butterfly, white-clawed crayfish, dormice, various bat species, curlew, adders, nightjars, rare ferns and bryophytes.

## Six Key Messages

### 1. Safeguarding, Managing and Enhancing Existing Biodiversity-Rich Sites
Existing high-quality habitat must be protected and improved first — implementing the Lawton Report (2010) principle: "bigger, better, more and more joined."

### 2. Landscape-Scale Connectivity
The existing protected site network alone is insufficient to prevent declines — sites are too small and isolated. Connected ecological networks are essential for species movement and climate adaptation. The strategy maps "Areas that Could Become of Particularly Importance for Biodiversity" for habitat creation.

### 3. Climate Emergency
Climate and nature crises are inter-linked. The LNRS recommends:
- Carbon sequestration habitats (woodland, wetland, grassland)
- Shade and microclimate habitats for species adapting to warming
- Habitats with high carbon storage: calcareous grassland, standing water, lowland fen, rivers and streams, coastal saltmarsh

### 4. Our Relationship with Water
Gloucestershire's landscape is heavily drained and rivers have been straightened and modified. Solutions:
- River and watercourse re-naturalisation
- Floodplain restoration
- Natural Flood Management (NFM) — including woody debris blockages, wooded floodplains
- Explore beaver reintroduction for NFM
- Treat riparian land as functionally linked to the watercourse

### 5. The Value of Mixed and Wilder Habitats
"Messy" mosaic habitats — scrub, species-rich grassland with varying sward heights, bare ground — support the widest range of species. Ecotones (gradual transitions between habitats) are particularly valuable. Low-intensity conservation grazing is preferred over manual management.

### 6. Biodiversity in Developments and Settlements
Urban areas must contribute to ecological connectivity. Requirements for new development:
- Wild areas and linear wildlife corridors as wide as possible
- Biodiverse SuDS (reduces flooding, creates green space and connectivity)
- Building with Nature standards or Natural England's Green Infrastructure Framework
- Cotswold Water Park restoration opportunities

## Other Key Pressures

- **Recreational disturbance**: Bats, rare ferns, Schedule 1 birds, and otter habitats particularly sensitive; artificial lighting a major issue
- **Invasive non-native species**: Himalayan balsam, giant hogweed, Crassula helmsii, American signal crayfish (devastates white-clawed crayfish), American mink
- **Deer population increase**: Preventing woodland regeneration; no natural predators; 2–4 deer/km² threshold needed for woodland establishment
- **Fungi and soil health**: Under-recorded; fungi essential ecosystem engineers
- **Historic environment**: Archaeological sites must be considered in habitat creation; consult Gloucestershire Historic Environment Record
- **Minerals extraction**: Cotswold Water Park restoration opportunities for high-value habitat (wetland, open mosaic)

## Wider Ecological Connections

Gloucestershire is part of larger ecological networks:
- **Limestone and Big Chalk**: Calcareous landscape connections into Wiltshire, Somerset, and the Chilterns
- **Migrating birds**: Severn Estuary international importance; breeding and overwintering species
- **River catchments**: Severn, Wye, Thames headwaters — connecting Gloucestershire to wider English landscapes

## Ecosystem Services Identified

The LNRS links potential measures to:
- Carbon sequestration (woodland, peatland)
- Food provision and water supply
- Soil erosion prevention and soil health
- Flood regulation (Natural Flood Management)
- Local climate regulation (shading, urban cooling)
- Access to biodiversity-rich green spaces (human wellbeing)
- Raw materials (sustainable woodland products)

---

## Planning and Development Use

The LNRS explicitly addresses planning and development (section 1.1.2):
- BNG projects should target LNRS priority habitat creation areas for off-site units
- LPAs must "have regard to" the LNRS in local planning processes
- New developments should incorporate wildlife corridors and biodiverse SuDS
- Suitable Alternative Natural Green Spaces (SANGS) can relieve recreational pressure on sensitive sites
- Building with Nature and Natural England GI Framework recommended as design standards
