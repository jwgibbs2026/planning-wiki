# Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL) Guidance

**Source**: MHCLG (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/community-infrastructure-levy
**Type**: National planning guidance
**Legislation**: Planning Act 2008; CIL Regulations 2010 (as amended)
**Status**: Current guidance

---

## Overview

The Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL) is a charge that local authorities in England can impose on new development to fund infrastructure needed to support growth. It operates in areas where councils have adopted a published charging schedule.

---

## How CIL Works

### Applicability
Most new development creating net additional floor space of 100 sq m or more, or creating a new dwelling, is potentially liable for the levy. The 100 sq m threshold does not apply to single dwellings of any size.

### Who Pays
Landowners bear ultimate liability, but developers may assume this responsibility. Where no one assumes liability, it defaults to landowners automatically and becomes due immediately on commencement.

---

## Charging Schedules

LPAs set CIL rates (expressed as £ per sq m) for different development types. Rates must:
- Strike an appropriate balance between additional investment for development and protecting development viability
- Be justified by evidence on infrastructure needs, development viability, and other funding sources
- Undergo independent public examination before adoption

Differential rates are permitted by geographic zone, development type, scale, or use — but must not be used to pursue policy objectives and must be justified by viability evidence.

---

## Reliefs and Exemptions

| Category | Relief |
|----------|--------|
| Minor development (<100 sq m) | Exempt (except single dwellings) |
| Residential extensions/annexes | Owner-occupiers may qualify — must apply before work begins |
| Charitable development | 100% relief where used wholly/mainly for charitable purposes |
| Social housing | Full relief for qualifying social housing |
| Self-build | Full exemption if criteria met and applied before commencement |
| Exceptional circumstances | Discretionary hardship relief |

**Important**: Most exemptions require a commencement notice before work begins. Failure to submit incurs a surcharge of 20% of the notional chargeable amount (capped at £2,500).

---

## Collection and Payment

- The charging authority (usually the LPA) collects the levy
- Instalment options available if liability is assumed before commencement
- Immediate payment required if liability defaults to landowners
- Levy becomes due upon development commencement

---

## Neighbourhood Portion

A proportion of CIL collected in areas with a neighbourhood plan must be passed to the relevant parish or town council (typically 25%; 15% without a neighbourhood plan, uncapped at 25% with one). This is the "neighbourhood portion" and can be spent on local infrastructure and amenities.

---

## Use of Funds

From December 2020, LPAs must publish annual infrastructure funding statements detailing:
- Infrastructure needs and costs
- Anticipated developer contributions
- How CIL will be allocated
- Other funding sources

CIL can fund a wide range of infrastructure including roads, schools, healthcare, green spaces, flood defences, and biodiversity projects.

---

## Relationship with Section 106

CIL and S106 operate alongside each other:
- **CIL**: General, area-wide infrastructure charge; lower administrative burden
- **S106**: Site-specific obligations tied to individual developments

Both can be pooled to fund the same piece of infrastructure, provided documented in infrastructure funding statements.
