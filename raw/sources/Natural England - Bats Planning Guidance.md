# Bats: Advice for Making Planning Decisions

**Source**: Natural England standing advice (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/bats-surveys-and-mitigation-for-development-projects
**Type**: Protected species standing advice — material planning consideration

---

## Overview
This Natural England guidance provides material planning considerations for local planning authorities (LPAs) assessing development proposals affecting bats in England.

## How Bats Are Protected

All bat species are European Protected Species (EPS) under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017.

**Prohibited Activities:**
- Deliberately killing, injuring, disturbing or capturing bats
- Damaging or destroying breeding sites and resting places
- Possessing, controlling or transporting bats or parts thereof
- Selling or exchanging bats

Additional protections exist under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 regarding disturbance in occupied shelters and obstruction of access to roosting places.

Several bat species are listed as species of principal importance, with some designated as vulnerable, endangered, or critically endangered. Developers must comply with legal protections and may require a bat mitigation licence.

## When to Request a Survey

Surveys are necessary if development could negatively affect bat roosts, foraging habitats, or commuting habitats. Request surveys when:

- Distribution records suggest bats may be present
- Built structures exist providing roosting or commuting opportunities
- Trees, water bodies, or rock formations offer bat habitat
- Development includes lighting near bat habitats
- Wind farm proposals are near designated bat sites or habitats

**Absence of records does not indicate absence of bats.**

### Bats in Buildings
Survey buildings that may contain roosts, particularly those with:
- Minimal artificial lighting
- Proximity to woodland or water
- Uneven roof tiles and large timbers
- Cracks, crevices, and small openings
- South-facing walls with hanging tiles

### Bats in Trees
Survey trees before felling if they:
- Are ancient, veteran, or within ancient woodland
- Have natural cavities (ash, beech, oak)
- Show damage from rot, weathering, or wildlife
- Have loose or creviced bark
- Support dense ivy coverage

### Wind Farm Proposals
Survey wind turbines within 50 metres of bat commuting, foraging, or roosting habitats.

## Survey Requirements

Surveys must:
- Use appropriate seasonal timing
- Identify species and population size
- Determine roost types and importance
- Map flight routes and foraging areas

**Survey methods include:**
- Roost inspections
- Emergence and re-entry recording
- Activity monitoring and back-tracking
- Radio tagging and tracking

Surveyors must hold current, appropriate licensing credentials.

## Assessing Development Impact

### Species Vulnerability
Assessment requires considering both conservation status and site importance at local and national levels. Example: Bechstein's bat (localised, southern distribution) creates greater national significance concerns than common pipistrelle populations.

### Avoidance, Mitigation, and Compensation

**Avoidance measures:**
- Redesign to preserve roosts
- Maintain supporting habitat features
- Adjust work timing (avoiding maternity/hibernation seasons)
- Modify working methods to prevent harm

**Mitigation approaches:**
- Create new roosting sites within existing structures
- Install new roosting facilities elsewhere
- Redesign lighting to avoid roosts and flight corridors
- Establish dark commuting corridors
- Enhance insect-rich habitats

**Roost destruction conditions:**
If unavoidable, ensure:
- No net loss of occupied/unoccupied roosts
- Like-for-like roost replacement
- Continued population function

## Licensing Requirements

Developers may need:
- EPS bat mitigation licences
- Natural England bat mitigation class licences
- Bat Earned Recognition scheme (Bat ER) credentials

LPAs must verify mitigation conditions don't conflict with licence requirements and ensure Natural England will issue necessary permissions before granting planning approval.

## Biodiversity Enhancement

LPAs should encourage developers to:
- Create or enhance on-site habitats
- Integrate biodiversity improvements through design
- Align with relevant nature conservation plans

## Site Management and Monitoring

Wildlife licences typically require monitoring to verify mitigation implementation and bat return rates. Monitoring duration should match impact severity. Management plans ensure roosting sites remain functional and accessible.
