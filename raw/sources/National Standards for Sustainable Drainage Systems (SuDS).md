# National Standards for Sustainable Drainage Systems (SuDS)

**Source**: MHCLG / Defra (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/national-standards-for-sustainable-drainage-systems/national-standards-for-sustainable-drainage-systems-suds
**Type**: Statutory guidance
**Status**: In force — applies to all new developments requiring planning permission in England (excluding trunk roads and retrofits)

---

## Overview

These national standards govern how surface water drainage must be designed for new development in England. They require a "SuDS approach" that mimics natural drainage, managing runoff at or near source close to the surface rather than routing it underground. Compliance must be demonstrated from the earliest planning stages.

Relevant stakeholders: designers, developers, local planning authorities (as SuDS Approving Bodies), Lead Local Flood Authorities (LLFAs), sewerage companies.

---

## Seven Core Standards

### Standard 1: Runoff Destinations (hierarchy)
Surface water must discharge via the highest feasible priority:
1. Collection for non-potable reuse (highest)
2. Infiltration to ground
3. Discharge to above-ground surface water bodies
4. Discharge to surface water sewers
5. Discharge to combined sewers (lowest)

Higher cost alone is not a valid reason to use a lower-priority destination. Developers must demonstrate all higher options have been exhausted.

### Standard 2: Everyday Rainfall Management (Interception)
Systems must manage at least the first 5 mm of rainfall without runoff to surface waters or piped systems. Targets:
- 80% interception: May–October (summer)
- 50% interception: November–April (winter)

Acceptable methods: green roofs, rainwater harvesting, permeable surfaces, swales, bioretention areas, detention basins.

### Standard 3: Extreme Rainfall and Flooding
- Protect on-site development from drainage system flooding
- For 50% AEP events: limit discharge to greenfield rates or 3 litres/second/hectare
- For 1% AEP events: controlled discharge matching greenfield conditions
- Climate change allowances must use current Environment Agency guidance (Upper End Allowance factors)

### Standard 4: Water Quality
- Robust water quality risk assessments required
- Management trains addressing diffuse particulates and chemicals
- Pre-treatment for infiltration systems
- High-risk land uses may need proprietary treatment or discharge to foul sewers

### Standard 5: Amenity
- Designs must contribute to placemaking and environmental enhancement
- Vegetation inclusion to combat urban heat
- Support for walking, cycling, recreation, and informal play where feasible
- Educational signage explaining flood functions and environmental benefits

### Standard 6: Biodiversity
- Create diverse, resilient local ecosystems contributing to net biodiversity gains
- Support local habitat and species through nature recovery strategies
- Biodiversity risk and opportunity assessments required (proportionate to site sensitivity)
- Follow mitigation hierarchy: avoid, mitigate, compensate
- Integrate with Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) metric calculations where applicable
- Ensure habitat connectivity and species protection measures

### Standard 7: Construction, Operation, Maintenance, and Structural Integrity
- Management and maintenance plans must support all other standards
- Designers must assess failure scenarios and risks
- Structural components: minimum design life matching development lifetime
- Maintenance access required for all personnel, vehicles, and equipment
- Monitoring provisions agreed with approving bodies for larger developments

## Design Principles

### Natural Water Management
SuDS should mimic natural drainage — managing runoff at or near source, as close to the surface as practicable. Avoids traditional underground pipe conveyance and delivers multiple environmental and social co-benefits.

### Early and Integrated Design
Surface water management must be considered from earliest site appraisal and integrated with:
- Existing topography, hydrology, and watercourses
- Road and building layouts
- Biodiversity and green infrastructure delivery
- Climate resilience planning

### SuDS Management Train
Most effective approach: series of drainage features operating near runoff source, working together to control flow rates and volumes, provide water quality benefits, encourage biodiversity, and integrate into landscape design.

## Regulatory Framework

**Planning**: Applicants must demonstrate compliance with national standards from conceptual planning stages. LPAs act as SuDS Approving Bodies. Early engagement with LPAs helps identify local strategies and requirements.

**Adoption and Maintenance**: Appropriate bodies must adopt and maintain surface water systems serving multiple properties. Since April 2020, sewerage companies can adopt SuDS under Ofwat's Code for Adoption Agreements.

**Building Regulations**: Approved Document H, Section H3 applies within property curtilages.

**Environmental Permits**: Required under Environmental Permitting Regulations for discharges containing pollutants. Clean rainwater from roofs and small hardstandings may be exempt if treated.

**Biodiversity Net Gain**: Mandatory 10% net gain (effective February 2024) applies to relevant developments. SuDS habitats can contribute toward BNG targets.

**Lead Local Flood Authorities**: Developers should engage LLFAs for flood risk and drainage information, including Local Flood Risk Management Strategies and Strategic Flood Risk Assessments.

## Key Design Notes

- **Pumping**: Permitted only by exception where gravity drainage is impossible
- **Infiltration depths**: Base of infiltration features must remain 1 metre above maximum groundwater levels
- **Previously developed sites**: Relaxation up to 5× greenfield runoff rates may be agreed with evidence
- **Contaminated land**: Higher-priority destinations should not be discounted where practicable remediation is possible
