# Local Wildlife Sites — Natural Environment Planning Guidance

**Source**: MHCLG / Natural England (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/natural-environment
**Type**: National planning practice guidance (PPG)
**Status**: Current guidance

---

## What Are Local Wildlife Sites?

Local Wildlife Sites (LWSs) — sometimes called "Local Sites" — are areas of substantive nature conservation value locally designated by local authorities and partnerships. They contribute to ecological networks and nature recovery and can be in rural, urban, or coastal settings. They vary considerably in size.

Local Geological Sites (LGSs) are the equivalent for geodiversity.

These are non-statutory designations (unlike SSSIs), but they carry significant weight in planning decisions.

---

## Planning Authority Responsibilities

Local planning authorities (LPAs) must:
- **Identify and map** Local Wildlife Sites in development plans
- **Include protective policies** against harm or loss of LWSs
- **Enhance** sites and their connections to wider ecological networks
- **Establish partnerships** with landowners and relevant bodies for ongoing management

---

## Selection Criteria

Sites are assessed against standard criteria:
- **Size/extent**: Larger sites support viable populations; smaller sites are valuable where greenspace is limited
- **Diversity**: Reflect the wildlife and habitat characteristics of the area
- **Naturalness**: Support natural features and ecological processes
- **Rarity**: Include habitats or species that are locally exceptional
- **Fragility**: Sensitivity to climate change and human impacts
- **Typicalness**: Exemplify characteristic landscape habitats
- **Recorded history**: Links to cultural, industrial, or research heritage
- **Connectivity**: Contribution to functional ecological networks
- **Educational value**: Opportunities for learning and connection with nature

## Priority Habitats (Section 41 Habitats)

Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities (NERC) Act 2006 establishes a statutory list of habitats and species of principal importance in England.

- LPAs must consider the potential effect of development on Section 41 habitats and species
- Priority habitats include ancient woodland, species-rich grassland, lowland meadows, hedgerows, chalk streams, and others
- Most priority habitats are mapped on the MAGIC website

### Open Mosaic Habitats
Defra recognises Open Mosaic Habitats — priority habitats occurring on brownfield land of high ecological value. Natural England's Open Mosaic Habitat Inventory supports detailed site assessments.

---

## Information Sources for LPAs

- **Local Environmental Records Centres (LERCs)** — collated local biodiversity data (e.g. GCER in Gloucestershire)
- **MAGIC website** — national environmental information (SSSIs, priority habitats, protected areas)
- **Local Nature Partnerships** — ecological network identification

---

## Assessment and Mitigation

When assessing development impacts on LWSs and priority habitats, the mitigation hierarchy applies:

1. **Avoidance**: Locate development away from sensitive sites
2. **Mitigation**: Minimise harm through design or secured conditions
3. **Compensation**: Last resort — provide equivalent or greater biodiversity value

"Where a development cannot satisfy the requirements of the mitigation hierarchy, planning permission should be refused."

---

## Integration with Local Nature Recovery Strategies

LPAs must "have regard to" the Local Nature Recovery Strategy (LNRS) when:
- Making planning decisions
- Preparing Local Plans

The LNRS identifies where habitat enhancement would be most beneficial and helps target biodiversity improvements. Local Wildlife Sites often coincide with LNRS opportunity areas.

---

## Gloucestershire Context

In Gloucestershire, Local Wildlife Sites are:
- Identified and mapped by Gloucestershire County Council in partnership with GWT and GCER
- Included as a material planning consideration in all six district Local Plans
- Informed by GCER data searches, which ecological consultants routinely obtain when assessing applications
- Referenced in the Gloucestershire Local Nature Recovery Strategy (2026) as part of the existing protected site network
