# Otters: Advice for Making Planning Decisions

**Source**: Natural England standing advice (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/otters-surveys-and-mitigation-for-development-projects
**Type**: Protected species standing advice — material planning consideration

---

## How Otters Are Protected

Otters are European protected species under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017. Key prohibitions include:

- Deliberately kill, injure, disturb or capture them
- Damage or destroy their breeding sites and resting places
- Possessing, controlling, or transporting them or their remains
- Selling or offering them for sale

Additional protections exist under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. Otters are listed as species of principal importance in England under the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006.

Developers must comply with legal protections and may need to apply for an otter mitigation licence.

## When to Request a Survey

Surveys become necessary when:

- Distribution records indicate potential otter presence (check National Biodiversity Network Atlas)
- Development affects water bodies, rivers, streams, lakes, or marshland
- Development impacts nearby habitats through noise, light, or other environmental effects

Survey work may include presence/absence assessments and habitat evaluations. Ecologists must be qualified and experienced, with appropriate licensing for non-routine activities.

## Assessing Development Effects on Otters

Development proposals must include information addressing:

- Likelihood of otter impacts
- Development scale and proximity to otter habitat

**Potential Harm Sources:**
- Habitat loss or degradation near water
- Habitat fragmentation or access obstruction
- Loss of holts and resting places
- Disturbance to feeding and movement routes
- Water quality degradation affecting food sources

## Avoidance, Mitigation, and Compensation

**Avoidance strategies include:**
- Avoiding work near water bodies and known otter habitat
- Creating buffer zones along rivers
- Scheduling work outside nighttime hours

**Mitigation measures may involve:**
- Retaining otter habitats in water and banks
- Installing otter-proof fencing

**Compensation measures could include:**
- Constructing artificial holts
- Building viaducts or underpasses for crossing barriers
- Installing mammal ledges on bridges and culverts
- Restoring or improving habitats

## Planning and Licensing Conditions

Before granting permission, local planning authorities must:

- Ensure conditions align with otter mitigation licence requirements
- Verify Natural England will likely issue a licence
- Add mitigation and compensation strategies as planning conditions

## Site Management and Monitoring

Management plans should:

- Maintain habitat suitability through active management
- Monitor for movement barriers
- Track human-related pressures post-development
- Verify mitigation effectiveness through surveys and remedial action
