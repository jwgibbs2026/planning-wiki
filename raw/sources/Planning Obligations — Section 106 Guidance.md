# Planning Obligations — Section 106 Guidance

**Source**: MHCLG (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/planning-obligations
**Type**: National planning guidance
**Legislation**: Section 106, Town and Country Planning Act 1990
**Status**: Current guidance

---

## What Are Planning Obligations?

Planning obligations (also called "section 106 agreements" or "developer contributions") are legal obligations entered into to mitigate the impacts of a development proposal. They can be:
- Formal agreements between developers and local planning authorities (LPAs)
- Unilateral undertakings by developers alone

They are legally binding, run with the land, and are enforceable.

---

## The Three Statutory Tests

Planning obligations can only be used to justify granting permission if all three requirements are met:

1. **Necessary** to make the development acceptable in planning terms
2. **Directly related** to the development
3. **Fairly and reasonably related** in scale and kind to the development

These tests appear in Regulation 122 of the Community Infrastructure Levy Regulations 2010 (as amended) and in the National Planning Policy Framework.

---

## How Section 106 Works

- Discussions should begin early in the planning process
- Obligations are negotiable and can reflect site-specific circumstances
- Applicants are not required to agree, but refusal may lead to permission being denied
- LPAs must keep copies publicly available on planning registers
- Annual infrastructure funding statements must report on spending

---

## Relationship with Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL)

S106 and CIL serve complementary roles:
- **CIL**: Fixed charge for general infrastructure across an area; applies to small and large developments
- **S106**: Project-specific funding directly related to particular developments

Authorities can pool funding from both sources for the same infrastructure, provided this is documented in infrastructure funding statements.

---

## Affordable Housing

Planning obligations for affordable housing apply only to **major residential developments** (10+ homes or 0.5+ hectares). Small developments should use CIL instead. Extensions and annexes are exempted entirely.

---

## Biodiversity and Nature Uses

S106 obligations are commonly used to secure:
- Off-site biodiversity net gain (BNG) where on-site delivery is not feasible
- Habitat management and monitoring endowments
- Nature reserve contributions and green infrastructure delivery
- Ecological mitigation and compensation for protected species
- Long-term management of retained or created habitats

These uses are consistent with the three tests: mitigation of the development's specific impacts, directly related to the site.
