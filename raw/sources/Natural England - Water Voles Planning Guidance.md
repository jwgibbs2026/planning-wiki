# Water Voles: Advice for Making Planning Decisions

**Source**: Natural England standing advice (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/water-voles-surveys-and-mitigation-for-development-projects
**Last updated**: 7 April 2025
**Type**: Protected species standing advice — material planning consideration

---

## Overview

This Natural England guidance assists local planning authorities in assessing planning applications affecting water voles. It serves as a material planning consideration under England's protected species framework.

## Legal Protection

Water voles receive protection under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. Prohibited activities include:

- Intentionally kill, injure or take them
- Damaging or destroying shelter structures
- Disturb them while they occupy a structure or place used for shelter
- Obstructing access to protective structures

Water voles are classified as endangered on England's mammal conservation list and designated as a species of principal importance.

## Survey Requirements

Surveys should be requested when:

- Historical records or distribution data indicate possible presence
- Suitable habitat exists, including soft earth banks, vegetation growth, slow-flowing water, or predator escape areas
- The qualified ecologist meets CIEEM competency standards

**Absence of a record does not mean there are no water voles.**

## Development Impact Assessment

Developers must demonstrate how proposals avoid or mitigate harm through information addressing:

- Likelihood of water vole impact
- Effects on watercourse conditions and nearby work areas

Harmful activities include habitat destruction, shelter damage, water quality changes, and habitat fragmentation.

## Mitigation and Compensation

**Avoidance strategies** should prioritise redesign to prevent negative effects and maintain habitat connectivity.

**Mitigation measures** may include displacement through vegetation removal to encourage relocation to connected alternative habitat.

**Compensation** (required when impact cannot be mitigated) may involve habitat provision, water quality improvements, bank enhancement, or mink control.

Translocation is considered only as a last resort.

## Licensing

- Standard mitigation licence required for projects affecting water voles
- Class licence CL31 available for projects affecting less than 50 metres of bank
- Natural England confirmation needed only for Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects

## Biodiversity Enhancement

Developers should incorporate new or enhanced habitats, improve design quality, and align with relevant nature conservation strategies.

## Site Management and Monitoring

Required management plans should address vegetation maintenance, pollution/human impact risks, water quality preservation, and population monitoring post-development.
