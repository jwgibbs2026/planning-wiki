# Wild Birds: Advice for Making Planning Decisions

**Source**: Natural England standing advice (gov.uk)
**URL**: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/wild-birds-surveys-and-mitigation-for-development-projects
**Type**: Protected species standing advice — material planning consideration

---

## Overview

This Natural England guidance helps local planning authorities assess planning applications affecting wild birds. It serves as a material planning consideration for England.

## How Wild Birds Are Protected

All wild birds receive protection under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. Prohibited activities include:

- Kill, injure or take wild birds
- Damaging or destroying nests in use or being built
- Take or destroy the egg of any wild bird
- Possession of wild birds or their parts
- Selling birds or advertising their sale

Schedule 1 birds receive additional protection against intentional or reckless disturbance near active nests or during nest-building.

Some species qualify for extra consideration as:
- Species of principal importance under the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006
- Red-listed species on conservation concern lists

**Key requirement:** Developers must comply with all wild bird legal protections; Natural England cannot issue licences permitting development harm to birds.

## When to Request Surveys

Surveys become necessary when developments affect:

**Habitat types:**
- Natural habitats, such as wetland, woodland, scrub, meadow or moorland
- Mature gardens
- Trees over 100 years old or with cavities
- Buildings supporting nesting birds

**Species requiring surveys:**
- Barn owls and Schedule 1 birds
- Species listed under section 41 of conservation legislation
- Red or amber-listed species on ornithological concern lists

Surveyors must be qualified ecologists with relevant licences for Schedule 1 species.

## Assessing Development Effects

### Avoidance Strategies
- Locating developments away from breeding areas
- Timing work outside nesting seasons (generally March–August)
- Protecting established habitats like woodlands and wetlands
- Removing habitat features before breeding seasons

### Mitigation Measures
- Displacing birds (outside breeding season) through blocking nest access, vegetation clearing, or deterrents
- Installing replacement features:
  - Nest boxes (ideally integrated into brickwork) for birds in conservation need
  - Peregrine and black redstart ledges
  - Native tree and shrub plantings
  - Biodiverse green roofs
  - Hedgerow connections

**Compensation requirement:** Replacement habitat must achieve no net loss of habitat with like-for-like replacement or superior alternatives established before work begins.

## Planning and Licensing Conditions

No development licences exist permitting bird harm. Limited licences apply only for public health, air safety, scientific research, crop protection, or other specific purposes — not construction activities.

Authorities need not consult Natural England on condition wording or approval processes.

### Biodiversity Enhancement
Planners should require developers to create or enhance habitats and follow relevant conservation strategies to meet biodiversity duties.

## Site Management and Monitoring

Management plans must address:
- Vegetation control and maintenance
- Nest box upkeep and accessibility
- Follow-up surveys verifying mitigation effectiveness with remedial measures as needed
