# Planning Obligations — Section 106 Guidance

This page explains how Section 106 agreements work and how they're used to secure things for nature. The main [[Planning Obligations]] page gives the wider context.

A Section 106 agreement (named after the section of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 that creates it) is a legally binding deal between a developer and the [[Local Planning Authority]] to deal with the impacts of a development. It "runs with the land", so it stays binding even if the site changes hands, and the authority can enforce it.

## The three tests

An authority can only ask for an obligation if it meets all three of these tests. The obligation must be:

1. **Necessary** to make the development acceptable in planning terms;
2. **Directly related** to the development; and
3. **Fairly and reasonably related** in scale and kind to the development.

## How Section 106 is used for nature

Where on-site measures aren't enough, Section 106 agreements often secure:

- off-site [[Biodiversity Net Gain]] when it can't all be delivered on the site itself;
- long-term management and monitoring of habitats, with money set aside to pay for it;
- mitigation and compensation for protected species; and
- contributions to nature reserves or [[Green Infrastructure|green infrastructure]].

## How it differs from CIL

Section 106 is negotiated and specific to a site; the [[Community Infrastructure Levy]] is a fixed charge per square metre that funds infrastructure across the wider area. Section 106 is the usual route for biodiversity and protected-species measures, while CIL tends to fund things like roads, schools, and open space. The two can be combined for the same project where this is properly documented.
