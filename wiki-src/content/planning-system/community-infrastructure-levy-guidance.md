# Community Infrastructure Levy — Guidance

This page explains how the [[Community Infrastructure Levy]] (CIL) works in practice — who pays it, how the rate is set, what's exempt, and the share that goes to your local community. The main [[Community Infrastructure Levy]] page gives the wider context.

## Who pays, and how much

CIL is a charge on most new development that creates 100 square metres or more of extra floorspace, or a new home. Each [[Local Planning Authority]] sets its own rate (in pounds per square metre) through a published "charging schedule", balancing the need to fund infrastructure against keeping development viable.

## The neighbourhood share

A slice of the money comes straight back to the local area. Where a [[Neighbourhood Development Plan]] is in place, the parish or town council receives **25%** of the CIL raised from development in its area; without a plan, the figure is **15%**. This is one of the most tangible reasons for a community to produce a neighbourhood plan.

## Reliefs and exemptions

Some development pays a reduced charge or none at all. Mandatory reliefs cover social housing, charitable development, minor development, and residential extensions; discretionary relief can apply to self-build and exceptional circumstances. Several of these — charitable development, social housing, self-build, and exceptional circumstances — require a commencement notice **before** work begins. Miss that notice and a 20% surcharge applies (capped at £2,500).
