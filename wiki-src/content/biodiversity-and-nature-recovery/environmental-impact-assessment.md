# Environmental Impact Assessment

An Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is a formal process for identifying, describing, and assessing the significant effects of certain types of development on the environment. For applicable development, a planning application cannot be determined without an EIA having been carried out and an Environmental Statement (ES) submitted.

## Legal Basis

EIA in England is governed by the **Town and Country Planning (Environmental Impact Assessment) Regulations 2017** (the EIA Regulations). These implement the EU EIA Directive in domestic law.

## When EIA Is Required

EIA applies to two categories of development ("Annex I" and "Annex II" in the Regulations):

**Annex I (always require EIA)**: Large-scale projects where significant effects are assumed:
- Motorways, express roads, long-distance railways
- Large airports
- Power stations (above threshold)
- Large-scale waste facilities

**Annex II (require EIA if significant effects likely)**: A much wider list including:
- Housing development above a threshold
- Industrial estates
- Quarrying and mineral extraction
- Infrastructure projects
- Agricultural intensification above thresholds

For Annex II projects, the LPA carries out a **screening opinion** — a formal decision on whether the project is likely to have significant environmental effects requiring EIA.

## The Screening Opinion

Before or alongside a planning application, a developer can request a screening opinion from the LPA. The LPA must decide:
- Is the project in Annex II?
- Is it likely to have significant effects on the environment?

If yes to both: EIA is required. If no: EIA is not required (but [[ecological-impact-assessment|EcIA]] may still be needed).

Screening considers the **characteristics of the project** (size, scale, complexity), **location** (proximity to sensitive receptors including designated sites), and **potential impacts** (nature, scale, transboundary effects).

## The Scoping Opinion

Once EIA is confirmed, the developer can request a **scoping opinion** — the LPA's view on what the Environmental Statement should cover. Scoping ensures the ES focuses on the most significant topics and avoids unnecessary information.

## The Environmental Statement

The ES is the formal document submitted with the planning application. It must assess **significant effects** on:
- **Population and human health**
- **Biodiversity** (flora and fauna) — usually the largest chapter
- **Land, soil, water**
- **Air**
- **Climate**
- **Material assets**
- **Cultural heritage**
- **Landscape and visual impact**
- **Interaction between the above**

The biodiversity chapter of the ES is effectively a detailed [[ecological-impact-assessment|Ecological Impact Assessment (EcIA)]], including all protected species surveys, habitat assessments, and mitigation proposals.

## EIA and Other Assessments

EIA sits alongside but does not replace other assessment requirements:
- **[[habitats-regulations-assessment|Habitats Regulations Assessment (HRA)]]**: required separately if the project may affect SACs or SPAs; informs but is separate from the ES
- **[[ecological-impact-assessment|EcIA]]**: the ecological component of the ES
- **Flood Risk Assessment**: required for development in flood zones; may be a chapter in the ES or a standalone document

## Non-EIA Development

The majority of planning applications do not require EIA. For these, ecology is addressed through a standalone [[ecological-impact-assessment|EcIA]] report and Biodiversity Net Gain assessment rather than a full Environmental Statement.

## Gloucestershire Context

EIA-scale development in Gloucestershire typically includes:
- Strategic housing allocations (large sites in Local Plans)
- Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects (NSIPs) — renewable energy, road schemes
- Major retail or commercial development
- Quarrying and mineral extraction in the Cotswolds Water Park area

The Cotswolds Water Park (Cotswold Water Park) — Gloucestershire and Wiltshire — is the largest man-made lake complex in the UK, created through mineral extraction restoration; ongoing extraction operations require EIA.
