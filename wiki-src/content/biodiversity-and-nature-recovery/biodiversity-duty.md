# Biodiversity Duty

The biodiversity duty is a legal requirement on public bodies in England to consider biodiversity in everything they do, set policies to conserve and enhance it, and report on what they have done.

## Legal Basis

The duty was originally introduced by **Section 40** of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006. The Environment Act 2021 significantly strengthened it, adding:
- A requirement to publish a **Biodiversity Report** every five years
- A requirement to agree and publish biodiversity objectives
- A duty to act to deliver those objectives

## Who It Applies To

The biodiversity duty applies to all public bodies in England, including:
- Local Planning Authorities
- County councils
- Parish councils
- Government departments and agencies
- NHS bodies
- Highways England
- Water authorities

## What It Requires

Public bodies must:
1. **Consider** what they can do to conserve and enhance biodiversity in all their functions
2. **Agree** policies and specific objectives
3. **Act** to deliver those objectives
4. **Report** on what they have done (for larger bodies at least)

Not acting on this duty is a failure to comply with the law.

## What This Means for Planning

LPAs must fulfil the biodiversity duty not just through their planning decisions, but also in how they manage their own land (car parks, verges, cemeteries, parks), commission contracts, and procure services.

This is a significant lever. Community groups can challenge LPAs that are failing to fulfil their duty — including making complaints to the Office for Environmental Protection.
