# Biodiversity Net Gain

Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) is an approach to development that requires the natural environment to be left in a measurably better state after development than before. Since November 2023, a minimum 10% net gain for biodiversity is mandatory for most new developments in England.

## The Requirement

Under the Environment Act 2021, all new housing and commercial developments must demonstrate at least **10% BNG** before planning permission is granted. This is measured using the **Biodiversity Metric** (a tool produced by Natural England) which scores habitats by type, condition, size, and distinctiveness.

The 10% is a national minimum. Local Plans and NDPs can require a higher percentage where evidence supports it.

## How It Works

1. **Baseline survey** — the developer surveys the existing habitats on the site and calculates their baseline biodiversity score
2. **Impact assessment** — the proposed development's impact on those habitats is calculated
3. **On-site mitigation** — wherever possible, the developer designs the scheme to retain and enhance habitats on site (the "mitigation hierarchy")
4. **Off-site compensation** — if on-site measures are not enough to reach 10%, the developer can purchase off-site biodiversity credits from habitat banks or directly fund habitat creation elsewhere
5. **Statutory biodiversity credits** — as a last resort, developers can purchase government-administered credits

## The Mitigation Hierarchy

The BNG rules follow the standard ecological mitigation hierarchy:
1. **Avoid** — design out impacts on habitats
2. **Mitigate** — reduce the impacts that cannot be avoided
3. **Compensate** — offset unavoidable residual impacts

## Irreplaceable Habitats

BNG does not apply to **irreplaceable habitats** (ancient woodland, ancient and veteran trees, peatland, coastal/floodplain grazing marsh, sand dunes, blanket bog). These cannot be offset — their loss must be justified by exceptional circumstances and compensated through bespoke measures.

## Strategic Significance

BNG delivery should align with the Local Nature Recovery Strategy — off-site credits should be created in locations where they deliver the greatest ecological benefit. This is one of the most important tools for directing nature investment.

## The 20% BNG Requirement in the Cotswolds National Landscape

The [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] Management Plan (adopted February 2025) requires **20% BNG** for all new developments in the CNL — double the national mandatory minimum. This applies to all development requiring planning permission within the CNL boundary.

**How it works in practice:**
- The 20% target uses the same statutory [[Biodiversity Metric]] tool as the national 10% requirement — there is no separate metric
- It is enforced through individual district Local Plans that cover the CNL area (Cotswolds District Council, Stroud, Cheltenham, and others)
- A [[Biodiversity Gain Plan]] must be submitted and approved by the LPA showing how 20% is achieved — the same pre-commencement process as for national BNG
- On-site gain is prioritised; off-site habitat units and statutory credits follow in the same hierarchy as national BNG
- The 20% requirement was set by the CNL Management Plan as a policy aspiration; its legal force depends on whether individual district Local Plans have adopted it as a policy

> Note: verify which Gloucestershire district Local Plans have formally adopted the 20% BNG requirement and at what stage their plans are. Until adopted in a Local Plan, the 20% figure has weight as a material consideration from the CNL Management Plan but may not be binding.

## Pushing for More Than 10%

Community groups and NDPs can push for higher BNG requirements in their area. Evidence from the Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records and local habitat surveys can support a policy requiring 20% or more in ecologically sensitive areas. The CNL's 20% policy is a useful precedent for communities arguing for higher targets.
