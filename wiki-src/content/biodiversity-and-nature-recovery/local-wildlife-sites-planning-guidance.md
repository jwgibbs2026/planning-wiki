# Local Wildlife Sites — Natural Environment Planning Guidance

This page explains the national planning guidance on [[Local Wildlife Sites]] and priority habitats — what they are, how councils should protect them, and how they weigh in planning decisions. The main [[Local Wildlife Sites]] page gives the wider context.

## What a Local Wildlife Site is

A Local Wildlife Site is an area of real nature conservation value, identified locally by the council and its partners. Unlike a [[SSSI]], it isn't a statutory designation — but it still carries significant weight in planning decisions, and these sites form part of the wider web of habitats that nature recovery depends on. Sites are chosen on things like their size, diversity, rarity, naturalness, history, and how well they connect to other habitat.

## What the council must do

Councils are expected to map these sites in their development plans, include policies that protect them from harm or loss, look for chances to enhance them and their connections, and work with the landowners involved.

## Priority habitats

Alongside Local Wildlife Sites, the law lists "priority habitats" of principal importance in England (under Section 41 of the NERC Act 2006), and councils must consider the effect of development on them. They include ancient woodland, species-rich grassland, lowland meadows, hedgerows, chalk streams, and the open mosaic habitats that grow up on brownfield land.

## The mitigation hierarchy

Decisions follow the familiar order — **avoid** harm first, then **mitigate** what's left, and only **compensate** as a last resort. The guidance is blunt about the consequence: where a development can't satisfy that hierarchy, permission should be refused.

## Joining up with nature recovery

Councils must "have regard to" the [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy]] when they decide applications and prepare Local Plans, and Local Wildlife Sites often sit within the strategy's opportunity areas — so protecting them usually supports the wider recovery plan too.
