# Nature Recovery Network

The Nature Recovery Network (NRN) is a national framework for restoring, enhancing, and connecting wildlife-rich habitats across England. It is one of the government's key tools for reaching the "30x30" target — protecting and recovering nature on at least 30% of England's land by 2030.

## What Is It?

The NRN is not a single designation or a fixed boundary. It is an evolving network of:
- Existing wildlife-rich areas (protected sites, wildlife-managed land)
- Land being restored to good ecological condition
- Priority areas identified in Local Nature Recovery Strategies where habitat creation would have the greatest benefit
- Connecting habitats and wildlife corridors that link these areas together

## The Mapping

The NRN is mapped using LNRS evidence bases and existing data on habitats, species, and designated sites. The mapping identifies:
- Core areas: existing high-quality habitat (e.g. [[SSSI|SSSIs]], nature reserves)
- Opportunity areas: land where restoration or creation is most needed
- Connectivity corridors: routes that link habitats together

## Role in Planning

The NRN is a material consideration in planning decisions. Developments should avoid, mitigate, and compensate for impacts on the NRN. Local Plans and NDPs should include policies that protect and expand the NRN.

Biodiversity Net Gain delivery should be directed towards NRN opportunity areas where possible, guided by the Local Nature Recovery Strategy.

## Why Wildlife Corridors Matter

Wildlife needs to be able to move across the landscape — to find food, mates, and refuge from changing conditions. Isolated habitat patches are less resilient. The NRN aims to create a joined-up system where species can disperse and adapt.

Hedgerows, river corridors, road verges, parks, and gardens all play a role in connecting the NRN. This is why protecting hedgerows (under the Hedgerows Regulations 1997) and creating Green Infrastructure are so important.
