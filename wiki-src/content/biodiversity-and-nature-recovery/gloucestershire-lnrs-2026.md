# Gloucestershire Local Nature Recovery Strategy 2026

This is Gloucestershire's first [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy]] — the county's plan, required by the [[Environment Act 2021]] and published in February 2026, for where and how nature should recover. It maps the wildlife the county already has and pinpoints the best places to restore more. Councils must "have regard to" it when they decide planning applications and prepare Local Plans, which makes it directly relevant to development.

## Why it's needed

The picture it paints is sobering. Three-quarters of the county's land is farmed, and habitats have been stripped back over the last century — nationally, lowland meadow fell by 97% between 1930 and 1984, and less than 1.5% of the Cotswolds' wildflower meadows remain. The strategy is the plan to turn that around.

## The six key messages

The strategy boils down to six ideas:

1. Look after the wildlife-rich places we still have.
2. Join nature up at a landscape scale — bigger, better, more, and more connected.
3. Use habitats to help with the climate emergency, storing carbon and buffering change.
4. Work with water — re-naturalising rivers, restoring floodplains, and slowing flooding naturally.
5. Value mixed and wilder habitats, not just neat ones.
6. Build nature into new development — wildlife corridors, biodiverse [[SuDS]], and [[Building with Nature]].

## What it means for development

When [[Biodiversity Net Gain]] is delivered off-site, those gains should be steered toward the priority areas the strategy identifies, so they join up rather than scatter. New schemes are expected to weave in wildlife corridors and nature-friendly drainage. Gloucestershire holds national strongholds for species like the large blue butterfly, white-clawed crayfish, dormice, and several bats, and the strategy is built around protecting and reconnecting the places they depend on.
