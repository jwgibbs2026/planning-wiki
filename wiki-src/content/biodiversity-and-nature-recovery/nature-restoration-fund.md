# Nature Restoration Fund

The Nature Restoration Fund (NRF) is a new way for developers to meet their environmental obligations. Instead of designing and paying for nature measures on each individual site, a developer can pay into a single national fund, and Natural England uses that money to deliver conservation at a larger, more effective scale. It was created by the [[Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025]].

## The Problem It Solves

Some environmental requirements — particularly protected species licences and nutrient neutrality — can hold up or block planning permission for long periods. Dealing with them site by site is often inefficient: small ecological measures scattered across many individual sites tend to achieve less for nature than a single, well-planned scheme covering a whole area.

The NRF is designed to cut through that. A developer makes one payment, and Natural England takes responsibility for delivering the actual conservation work.

## How It Works

The fund is built around **Environmental Delivery Plans (EDPs)**. An EDP is a conservation plan that Natural England draws up for a particular area or a particular environmental problem. Each one moves through three stages:

1. **Setting up an EDP.** Natural England writes a draft plan setting out the conservation measures, which development impacts it covers, and how much developers will pay. The draft goes out for a **28-day public consultation**, then to the Secretary of State, who can only approve it if the conservation benefits **"materially outweigh"** the harm from the development it covers.
2. **Using an EDP.** Once a plan is in force, a developer within its scope can choose to pay a levy instead of producing their own assessments. An **online levy calculator** sets the amount, and payment follows an agreed schedule. Paying in means the developer no longer has to carry out a site-specific [[Habitats Regulations Assessment|HRA]] or obtain an [[EPS Mitigation Licence|EPS licence]] for the impacts the plan covers. Taking part is mostly voluntary.
3. **Monitoring.** Natural England reports each year on whether the conservation work is on track. If it looks like targets will be missed, backup measures kick in, and the Secretary of State can amend or revoke the plan.

## First Priority: Nutrient Neutrality

The first EDPs tackle nutrient neutrality, a requirement that currently blocks around **16,500 homes a year** across England. The catchments targeted first include the Severn/Avon — which covers part of Gloucestershire — along with the Solent, Somerset Levels, The Broads, and others. Natural England's existing Nutrient Mitigation Scheme will be folded into these plans.

## Timeline and Current Status

The fund is being introduced in stages, with the underlying secondary legislation still being finalised:

| When | What happens |
|------|--------------|
| Winter 2025 | Early engagement with the market and stakeholder consultation |
| Spring–Summer 2026 | Digital service tested, secondary legislation made, and the first EDPs go live |

## Gloucestershire Context

The Severn and Avon catchment is one of the priority areas for the first wave of EDPs, which makes the NRF directly relevant to Gloucestershire, where nutrient neutrality has held back development near certain rivers. Once the Severn/Avon plan is running (expected from 2026), developers in affected parts of the county should be able to pay a levy instead of buying individual nutrient neutrality credits.

## Future Scope

The government intends to widen the fund beyond nutrient neutrality to cover other species and habitats. [[District Level Licensing]] for [[Great Crested Newts in Planning|great crested newts]] is a likely next step.
