# Habitats Regulations Assessment

A Habitats Regulations Assessment (HRA) is the process required under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017 before any plan or project that may significantly affect a Special Area of Conservation (SAC) or Special Protection Area (SPA) can be approved. It is one of the strongest legal protections in the English planning system.

## Why It Exists

SACs and SPAs are internationally designated wildlife sites — the most important in Europe. The law requires that decision-makers (LPAs, the Secretary of State, and others) are certain that a plan or project will not harm the ecological integrity of these sites before giving consent. Unlike most planning tests, the burden is high: the authority must be certain of no adverse effect, not merely satisfied that harm is unlikely.

## The Two-Stage Process

### Stage 1 — Screening (Likely Significant Effects Test)
The first question is whether the plan or project is *likely to have a significant effect* on a SAC or SPA, alone or in combination with other plans or projects.

- If the answer is **no**: no further HRA is required
- If the answer is **yes or uncertain**: proceed to Stage 2

### Stage 2 — Appropriate Assessment
If significant effects cannot be ruled out, the competent authority (usually the LPA) must carry out an Appropriate Assessment. This assesses whether the plan or project would *adversely affect the integrity* of the SAC or SPA.

- Natural England must be consulted during Appropriate Assessment
- The authority must consider the plan or project's effects in combination with other plans and projects
- If adverse effects on integrity cannot be ruled out: **consent must be refused** unless the imperative reasons of overriding public interest (IROPI) test is passed

### Stage 3 — IROPI (exceptional cases only)
Where adverse effects on integrity are certain or likely, consent can only be granted if:
1. There are **imperative reasons of overriding public interest** (IROPI)
2. **Compensatory measures** are secured to maintain the overall coherence of the Natura 2000 network
3. Natural England and the Secretary of State are notified

IROPI is a very high bar — in practice, very few projects pass it.

## Who Must Carry Out HRA

Any "competent authority" making decisions that could affect SACs or SPAs. In practice this includes:
- **LPAs** — for planning applications and Local Plans
- **Planning Inspectorate** — for NSIPs and Local Plan examinations
- **Highway authorities, Environment Agency, Natural England** — for their own consenting decisions

## Gloucestershire Relevance

Gloucestershire contains or is adjacent to several internationally designated sites that trigger HRA requirements:

| Site | Type | Key qualifying features |
|------|------|------------------------|
| Severn Estuary | SAC/SPA/Ramsar | Estuaries, migratory birds, Atlantic salt meadows |
| Wye Valley Woodlands | SAC | Beech forests, limestone woodland |
| Cotswold Beechwoods | SAC | Beech forest assemblages |
| Rodborough Common | SAC | Species-rich calcareous grassland |
| Walmore Common | SPA | Overwintering Bewick's swans |

**Nutrient neutrality** — development near the Severn and Avon catchments may require an HRA because nutrient runoff could affect the Severn Estuary SAC/Ramsar. This is currently addressed through the Nature Restoration Fund (from 2026) or individual nutrient mitigation credits.

## HRA and the Nature Restoration Fund

The Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025 introduces Environmental Delivery Plans (EDPs) that can replace the requirement for site-specific HRAs for development types covered by an approved EDP. The first EDPs target nutrient neutrality — directly reducing the HRA burden on qualifying developments in affected catchments.
