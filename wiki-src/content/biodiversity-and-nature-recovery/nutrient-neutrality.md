# Nutrient Neutrality

Nutrient neutrality is a planning requirement that applies in certain river catchments where new development must demonstrate it will not increase the amount of nutrients (nitrates or phosphates) entering internationally designated water bodies. It arises from [[habitats-regulations-assessment|Habitats Regulations Assessment]] obligations and has blocked thousands of housing developments across England.

## Why It Exists

Excess nutrients — primarily nitrates from sewage and agriculture, and phosphates from similar sources — cause **eutrophication** in rivers, lakes, and estuaries. This encourages algal blooms that deprive water of oxygen, killing aquatic life and degrading internationally designated sites (SACs, SPAs, and Ramsar sites).

Following a 2018 European Court of Justice ruling (the *Dutch N case*), the UK courts confirmed that LPAs cannot approve development that would increase nutrient loads to affected sites without first demonstrating it is genuinely neutral. This applies through the [[habitats-regulations-assessment|Habitats Regulations Assessment]] process.

## How Nutrient Neutrality Works

For development in an affected catchment, the developer must show the scheme is **nutrient neutral** — meaning the additional nutrients it generates are fully offset. Steps:

1. **Calculate the development's nutrient load** — based on occupancy, sewage treatment standards, and runoff
2. **Identify mitigation measures** — typically purchasing nutrient credits from:
   - Wetland or woodland creation that removes nutrients from the catchment
   - Agricultural land taken out of intensive use
   - Upgraded wastewater treatment
3. **Demonstrate equivalence** — the credits must fully offset the calculated load
4. The LPA cannot grant permission until nutrient neutrality is demonstrated

## Affected Catchments

Nutrient neutrality currently applies in around 74 local authority areas across England, wherever development drains to an affected internationally designated site. In the south-west/Gloucestershire region, the **Severn Estuary SAC/SPA/Ramsar** is the key designated site. Parts of Gloucestershire within the Severn and Avon catchments may be affected.

## The Logjam Problem

Nutrient neutrality has been a significant constraint on housing delivery — Natural England estimates it has affected approximately **16,500 homes per year** nationally. The credits market has been slow to develop and expensive, creating delays for developers and LPAs.

## The Nature Restoration Fund Solution

The Nature Restoration Fund (Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025) directly addresses this. Environmental Delivery Plans (EDPs) for affected nutrient catchments — including the Severn/Avon — will:
- Pool developer payments into a central fund
- Natural England will commission large-scale strategic nutrient mitigation (wetland creation, land management change)
- Participating developers receive exemption from individual nutrient neutrality assessments
- First EDPs expected Spring–Summer 2026

Natural England's existing Nutrient Mitigation Scheme (which sold individual nutrient credits) will transition into these EDPs.

## Gloucestershire Context

The Severn Estuary is a SAC, SPA, and Ramsar site — qualifying features include estuarine habitats and migratory/overwintering birds. Development in Gloucestershire that discharges (directly or via combined sewers) to the Severn catchment may be subject to nutrient neutrality requirements, depending on the catchment boundary and the sensitivity of the affected site. LPAs check affected postcode areas; developers should check with the relevant LPA and Natural England early in the process.
