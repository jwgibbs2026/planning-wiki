# Local Nature Recovery Strategy

A Local Nature Recovery Strategy (LNRS) is a statutory spatial strategy that identifies priorities and opportunities for nature recovery at a local level. LNRS were introduced by the Environment Act 2021.

## What Is It?

Each LNRS maps:
- Areas where nature is already thriving
- Areas where habitat creation or restoration would have the greatest benefit
- Priorities for local action to support nature's recovery

Together, all the LNRS across England will form the spatial backbone of the Nature Recovery Network.

## Who Produces It?

A "responsible authority" — typically a county council — leads on preparing the LNRS for their area, working with a wider group of stakeholders including wildlife trusts, farmers, landowners, local authorities, and communities.

In Gloucestershire, **Gloucestershire County Council** is the responsible authority. The **Gloucestershire LNRS was published on 23 February 2026**, with Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust, Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records, and a wide stakeholder group as key partners.

### Six Key Messages of the Gloucestershire LNRS

1. **Safeguard existing biodiversity-rich sites** — protect and improve before creating new
2. **Landscape-scale connectivity** — bigger, better, more, joined (Lawton Report 2010)
3. **Climate emergency** — prioritise carbon-storing habitats; help species adapt to warming
4. **Relationship with water** — re-naturalise rivers and floodplains; natural flood management; explore beaver reintroduction
5. **Mixed and wilder habitats** — mosaic habitats, ecotones, conservation grazing
6. **Biodiversity in developments** — wildlife corridors, biodiverse [[SuDS]], Building with Nature standards

### Internationally Designated Sites in Gloucestershire
Wye Valley Woodlands SAC, Walmore Common SPA, Severn Estuary SAC/SPA/Ramsar, Cotswold Beechwoods SAC, Rodborough Common SAC, Dixton Wood SAC.

### Priority Gloucestershire Species
Large blue butterfly, white-clawed crayfish, dormice, multiple bat species, curlew, adders, nightjars, rare ferns.

## How Does It Connect to Planning?

The LNRS is a material consideration in planning decisions. Local Plans and NDPs should reflect LNRS priorities in their nature policies. Biodiversity Net Gain delivery should align with LNRS-identified opportunity areas where possible.

## Why It Matters

The LNRS is one of the most important tools for directing where Biodiversity Net Gain off-site credits are created, where habitat restoration should happen, and where developers' contributions to nature should be invested.

Communities can feed wildlife records and local knowledge into the LNRS evidence base through the Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records.
