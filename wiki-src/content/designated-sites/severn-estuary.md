# Severn Estuary

The Severn Estuary is one of the most important estuarine environments in Europe and carries multiple international wildlife designations. It forms the southern and western boundary of Gloucestershire and is a key driver of planning constraints across the county — particularly through [[nutrient-neutrality|nutrient neutrality]] and [[habitats-regulations-assessment|Habitats Regulations Assessment]] requirements.

## Designations

| Designation | Status | Key qualifying features |
|-------------|--------|------------------------|
| **Special Area of Conservation (SAC)** | International | Estuaries, mudflats and sandflats, Atlantic salt meadows, Sabellaria reefs, river lamprey, twaite shad |
| **Special Protection Area (SPA)** | International | Overwintering and migratory birds: dunlin, redshank, curlew, shelduck, wigeon, Bewick's swan |
| **Ramsar site** | International (wetlands) | Internationally important wetland; migratory waterbirds; unique tidal range |
| **SSSI** | National | Underpins all of the above; largest SSSI by area in England |

The Severn Estuary has the **second highest tidal range in the world** — up to 15 metres at spring tides. This drives unique sediment dynamics, salt marsh development, and intertidal habitat of global rarity.

## Planning Implications

### Habitats Regulations Assessment
Any plan or project that may significantly affect the Severn Estuary SAC, SPA, or Ramsar must undergo a [[habitats-regulations-assessment|Habitats Regulations Assessment]]. This includes:
- Development in the catchment that could increase nutrient loading or sediment
- Development affecting migratory fish passage (river lamprey, twaite shad)
- Coastal and flood defence works
- Energy infrastructure proposals (tidal barrages, solar farms near the shoreline)

### Nutrient Neutrality
The Severn Estuary's SAC and Ramsar designations mean that increased nutrients (nitrates, phosphates) from development in the Severn and Avon catchments can trigger [[nutrient-neutrality|nutrient neutrality]] requirements. LPAs across Gloucestershire must screen development against the affected catchment boundary. The Nature Restoration Fund's first Environmental Delivery Plans target this issue.

### Port and Industrial Development
The Severn Estuary and its ports (Sharpness, Gloucester Docks, and the estuary approaches) fall under complex planning regimes including the Planning Inspectorate's NSIP process for larger schemes, and the Marine Management Organisation for marine licensing.

## Ecology

The estuary supports:
- **Internationally important bird populations**: hundreds of thousands of waders and wildfowl during winter
- **Migratory fish**: river lamprey, twaite shad, European eel, salmon, sea trout
- **Saltmarsh habitats**: rare Atlantic salt meadows particularly around the Gwent Levels and Aust
- **Invertebrates**: Sabellaria (honeycomb worm) reefs in the outer estuary
- **Otters**: the Severn and its tributaries support significant otter populations

## Key Sites Within the Estuary

- **Walmore Common SPA**: inland SPA north of Gloucester; overwintering Bewick's swans
- **Slimbridge WWT**: internationally important wildfowl site; adjacent to the estuary
- **Berkeley/Sharpness**: industrial/port area with important intertidal habitats
- **Aust Warth and New Passage**: important saltmarsh and mudflat

## Gloucestershire Councils Affected

All Gloucestershire councils with land in the Severn catchment must consider the Severn Estuary SAC/SPA/Ramsar in HRA screening. Gloucester City Council, Tewkesbury Borough Council, Stroud District Council, and Forest of Dean District Council are the most directly affected.
