# Natural England — Otters Planning Guidance

When a development near water could affect otters, [[Natural England]] sets out "standing advice" that the local planning authority must weigh when deciding the application. This page explains that advice in plain terms; the main [[Otters in Planning]] page gives the fuller story.

## How otters are protected

Otters are a [[European Protected Species]] under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017. It is an offence to kill, injure, capture, or disturb them, or to damage or destroy the places they breed and rest. They get extra protection under the [[Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981]], and because they are a [[Species of Principal Importance]] they remain a planning consideration even where a licence isn't needed.

## When a survey is needed

Expect a survey where records suggest otters could be present, or where the work affects rivers, streams, lakes, marshland, or nearby habitat (including indirectly, through noise or light). The [[NBN Atlas]] is the first place to check for records. Surveyors must be suitably qualified, and some work — such as inspecting a holt — needs its own survey licence.

## What could harm otters

The advice asks you to think about habitat loss or damage near water, blocked movement routes, loss of holts, disturbance to feeding, and anything that worsens water quality and so reduces the fish otters rely on.

## What you should do, in order

- **Avoid** — keep work away from water and known habitat, leave buffer strips along rivers, and avoid working at night.
- **Mitigate** — retain bankside and in-water habitat, and fence otters out of the construction area.
- **Compensate** — where harm is unavoidable, build artificial holts, provide underpasses or mammal ledges so otters can pass roads and culverts safely, and restore riverside habitat.

## What the planning authority must do

Before granting permission, the authority needs to be satisfied that a [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] is likely to be granted (the standard three-part test), and it should secure the mitigation and compensation through planning conditions that match the licence. Licensing is site by site — there is no district-level shortcut for otters. Afterwards, a management plan should keep the habitat in good condition and check the measures are working.

## Gloucestershire relevance

Gloucestershire's many watercourses — the Severn, Avon, Churn, Coln, Leadon, and Windrush among them — mean otter triggers apply to a wide range of schemes across all six districts. Check the [[Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records|GCER]] for local records and the [[NBN Atlas]] for the national picture at the pre-application stage.
