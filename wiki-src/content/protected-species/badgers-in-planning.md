# Badgers in Planning

Badgers are a protected species in England and must be considered in the planning process wherever development may affect them or their setts. This page covers the legal framework, survey triggers, impact assessment, mitigation hierarchy, and licensing requirements.

---

## Legal status

Badgers are protected under the **[[Protection of Badgers Act 1992]]** — a dedicated piece of legislation separate from the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 and the Habitats Regulations. This is an important distinction: badgers are **not** European Protected Species (EPS), and the three-part EPS test used for bats, great crested newts, and otters does **not** apply.

Under the Protection of Badgers Act 1992, it is unlawful to:
- Take, injure, ill-treat, or kill a badger (or attempt to do so)
- Treat a badger cruelly
- Obstruct, disturb, damage, or destroy a badger sett or any part of it
- Sell, possess, or control a badger or any part of one (alive or dead)
- Mark, tag, or ring a badger

Both the animal and its sett are protected. A developer may require a **badger licence** from Natural England to carry out activities that would otherwise be unlawful.

## Survey triggers

A badger survey should be requested by the LPA when:
- Historical or distribution records indicate badger activity in or near the development area
- Suitable habitat exists for sett building or foraging (woodland, scrub, hedgerows, grassland edges)

[[NBN Atlas]] is the recommended first check for historical badger records. [[Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records|GCER]] may also hold local distribution data for Gloucestershire.

---

## Survey requirements

Surveys must:
- Document current badger usage of the site
- Identify and map sett locations
- Classify sett types (main, annex, subsidiary, outlier)
- Estimate territorial boundaries

Ecologists carrying out surveys must demonstrate competence, typically through [[CIEEM]] qualifications.

### Survey confidentiality

Unlike surveys for bats, great crested newts, or otters, **badger survey results must be kept confidential** and not made publicly available. This is because badgers face a significant persecution risk, and publicising sett locations could facilitate illegal interference. This is a species-specific procedural requirement that planners and ecologists must observe.

## Impact assessment

The LPA should assess whether the proposed development would cause:
- **Sett damage or loss** — direct physical destruction or obstruction of setts
- **Habitat isolation** — development layouts that cut off badger territories or prevent movement between foraging areas
- **Foraging area reduction** — loss of grassland, woodland edge, or other foraging habitat
- **Disturbance during occupancy** — noise, artificial lighting, vibration, or chemical use near active setts

---

## Avoidance and mitigation hierarchy

### Avoidance (preferred)

Layout design should be the first tool. Specific avoidance measures include:
- Designing site layouts to prevent **territorial isolation** — ensuring badger territories are not severed by development
- **Eliminating artificial lighting near setts** — badgers are sensitive to light disturbance; this connects directly to [[Dark Corridors]] policy
- Maintaining vegetation cover around setts and foraging routes
- Minimising noise and vibration near active setts
- Keeping machinery away from sett entrances
- Avoiding dusk-to-dawn working periods when badgers are most active

### Mitigation

Where avoidance is not fully achievable:
- Maintain foraging spaces within or adjacent to the development
- Establish **habitat connectivity** via tunnels, underpasses, or green corridors — connecting to Green Infrastructure principles
- Create **artificial setts** as compensation for sett loss (see below)

### Translocation — explicitly discouraged

Unlike otters and great crested newts, where translocation or displacement is a recognised mitigation option, **translocation of badgers is explicitly discouraged** by Natural England. There are two reasons:

1. **Bovine tuberculosis (bTB)**: Badgers are a wildlife reservoir of bovine TB (*Mycobacterium bovis*). Moving badgers from one area to another risks spreading bTB to cattle herds and to badger populations in the receptor area. Defra's bTB control programme — which includes the controversial badger culling policy in High Risk Areas — means that Natural England will not sanction translocation in any area where bTB is a concern.

2. **Welfare and territorial behaviour**: Badgers are highly territorial animals that will fight resident badger groups in the receptor area, causing significant injury and welfare harm.

**Gloucestershire is within Defra's bTB High Risk Area** — this means translocation of badgers from Gloucestershire development sites to any location within the High Risk Area is not an option. The practical consequence is that mitigation must rely entirely on sett avoidance, timed displacement (encouraging badgers to leave naturally before works), and artificial sett provision.

## Artificial setts

Where a sett must be lost or disturbed, an **artificial sett** may be constructed as compensation. This is analogous to artificial holts for otters. Artificial setts must be:
- Located in suitable habitat with access to foraging areas
- Constructed to an appropriate specification
- Monitored post-construction to confirm uptake

---

## Licensing

Where development cannot avoid affecting badgers or their setts, a **badger licence** from Natural England is required. Key points:

- The licence operates under **Section 10 of the [[Protection of Badgers Act 1992]]**, not the Habitats Regulations
- The three-part EPS test (no satisfactory alternative; imperative reasons of overriding public interest; favourable conservation status maintained) does **not** apply
- There is **no District Level Licensing equivalent** for badgers — all licensing is site-by-site, as with otters
- Planning conditions must be coordinated with licence requirements **before** planning permission is granted
- Planners must confirm Natural England's licensing capacity before granting permission

### What the Badger Licence Test Requires

Under s.10 of the Protection of Badgers Act 1992, Natural England may grant a licence for development purposes where:
1. **Development is lawfully authorised** — planning permission or prior approval must already be granted (or be in the process of being granted) before a licence is issued
2. **The impact is minimised** — the method and timing of works must minimise disturbance to badgers and setts as far as practicable
3. **The development cannot reasonably be carried out** without disturbing or interfering with the sett

This test is less demanding than the three-part EPS test — there is no requirement to demonstrate imperative reasons of overriding public interest or favourable conservation status. However, Natural England will require a **Method Statement** specifying exactly how the works will be carried out to minimise impact, and may require monitoring of artificial sett uptake post-development.

## Gloucestershire context

Badgers are widespread across Gloucestershire, occurring in woodland, farmland, and urban fringe habitats. The county's mix of pastoral farmland, ancient woodland, and hedgerow networks provides extensive suitable habitat. Developers across all six Gloucestershire districts should treat badger survey triggers seriously.

[[Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records|GCER]] may hold local badger distribution data alongside records for bats, great crested newts, and otters. [[NBN Atlas]] should be checked as a minimum for any site with suitable habitat.

The connection to [[Dark Corridors]] policy is particularly relevant in Gloucestershire, where several LPAs are developing or have adopted policies on artificial lighting — these policies benefit badgers as well as bats.
