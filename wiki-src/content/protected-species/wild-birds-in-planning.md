# Wild Birds in Planning

Wild birds are protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (WCA 1981). All wild bird species receive baseline legal protection, and certain species receive enhanced protection. Unlike most other protected species in the planning system, **there is no development licence that permits harm to wild birds** — avoidance is mandatory.

---

## Legal basis

### Baseline protection — all wild birds
Under WCA 1981, it is an offence to:
- Kill, injure, or take any wild bird
- Damage or destroy a nest in use or being built
- Take or destroy the egg of any wild bird
- Possess a wild bird, its eggs, or parts
- Sell or advertise wild birds for sale

This baseline applies to every wild bird species in England — not just rare or listed species. Any development that risks committing these offences must address them.

### Schedule 1 — enhanced disturbance protection
Species listed in Schedule 1 of WCA 1981 receive additional protection. It is an offence to intentionally or recklessly disturb a Schedule 1 bird at or near an active nest, or to disturb dependent young. This applies even if the nest itself is not damaged.

Surveys for Schedule 1 species must be carried out by qualified ecologists holding the relevant survey licence.

### Species of Principal Importance (S41)
Many bird species are listed under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006 as Species of Principal Importance. Their presence on a development site is a material planning consideration. See [[Species of Principal Importance]].

### Red and amber-listed species
Birds assessed as being of high (red) or medium (amber) conservation concern under the Birds of Conservation Concern assessment are also survey triggers. Red-listed species overlap significantly with the S41 list.

---

## The critical distinction: no development licence

This is the most important practical difference between wild bird protection and the [[European Protected Species]] framework.

For EPS species (bats, great crested newts, otters), Natural England can issue an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] permitting development harm where the three-part test is met. **No equivalent licence exists for wild birds.** Natural England can only issue bird licences for:
- Public health or public safety
- Air safety
- Scientific research or education
- Crop protection or preventing spread of disease

Construction activities are not a permitted reason. This means developers cannot licence their way out of bird offences. If a development would harm nesting birds, it must be redesigned or retimed — there is no fallback licensing route.

---

## When surveys are required

Natural England's standing advice identifies the following survey triggers:

### Habitat-based triggers
- Natural habitats: wetland, woodland, scrub, meadow, moorland
- Mature gardens
- Trees over 100 years old or with cavities
- Buildings supporting nesting birds (including barns, churches, old houses)

### Species-based triggers
- Barn owls (see [[Barn Owls in Planning]])
- Schedule 1 birds
- S41 / Species of Principal Importance birds
- Red or amber-listed species on ornithological concern lists

If any of these triggers apply, a survey by a qualified ecologist is required before the LPA can determine the application.

## Nesting season: March to August

The general breeding season for most wild birds in England runs from **March to August**. During this period:
- Vegetation clearance (trees, scrub, hedgerows, rank grassland) should be avoided
- Intrusive works near nesting habitat should be avoided
- If works must proceed, a pre-clearance check by a qualified ecologist is required

This is the single most universally applicable rule for development sites. Almost any site with vegetation, buildings, or trees could support nesting birds. Timing works outside the nesting season is the simplest and most reliable avoidance measure.

> **Note:** Some species nest outside this window. **Barn owls** are the most important exception for planning purposes — they can nest from April to October and their chicks may still be present in September. Works to buildings or trees with barn owl potential must avoid April–October, not just March–August. See [[Barn Owls in Planning]]. Crossbills and ravens can nest in winter. Schedule 1 species always require site-specific timing advice from a qualified ecologist.

---

## Mitigation hierarchy

### 1. Avoidance
- Locate development away from breeding areas
- Time all vegetation clearance and intrusive works outside March–August
- Protect established habitats (woodland, wetland, hedgerows) within the site layout

### 2. Displacement (outside breeding season only)
Where nesting habitat must be removed, birds can be displaced before the breeding season begins by:
- Blocking nest access points
- Clearing vegetation progressively outside March–August
- Using deterrents (e.g. visual or auditory)

**Displacement is only lawful outside the breeding season.** Blocking nest access or clearing vegetation while birds are nesting would constitute an offence under WCA 1981.

### 3. Replacement features
Where habitat loss cannot be avoided, replacement features must be provided:
- **Nest boxes** — ideally integrated into brickwork rather than surface-mounted; specified for target species
- **Peregrine and black redstart ledges** — for urban and industrial sites where these Schedule 1 / red-listed species may be present
- **Native tree and shrub planting** — to replace lost nesting and foraging habitat
- **Biodiverse green roofs** — vegetated roof surfaces supporting nesting and foraging birds; also relevant to Green Infrastructure and Biodiversity Net Gain
- **Hedgerow connections** — linking habitat patches to support movement and foraging

---

## No net loss and pre-establishment

Replacement habitat must:
1. Achieve **no net loss** of habitat — like-for-like or superior alternatives
2. Be **established before works begin** — not delivered post-development

This pre-establishment requirement is stricter than some BNG delivery timelines. It reflects the fact that there is no licensing fallback: if birds are present when works start, an offence has already been committed.

---

## Site management and monitoring

Management plans for sites with bird mitigation must address:
- Vegetation control and maintenance schedules
- Nest box upkeep and accessibility checks
- Follow-up surveys to verify mitigation effectiveness
- Remedial measures if monitoring shows mitigation has failed

---

## Biodiversity enhancement

Natural England's guidance states that planners should require developers to create or enhance habitats beyond mere mitigation, in line with biodiversity duties under the Environment Act 2021. Nest boxes integrated into new buildings, green roofs, and native planting are all straightforward enhancement measures that can be required by planning condition.

These measures also connect directly to Biodiversity Net Gain requirements and are practical hooks for Neighbourhood Development Plan policies.

## Gloucestershire context

Wild bird protection is relevant to virtually every development in Gloucestershire. Key local considerations include:

- **Rural conversions and demolitions** — barns, farm buildings, and old houses commonly support nesting swallows, house sparrows, starlings, and barn owls. Survey requirements apply before any demolition or conversion work.
- **Hedgerow removal** — Gloucestershire's agricultural landscape contains extensive hedgerow networks supporting yellowhammers, linnets, and other red-listed farmland birds. Hedgerow removal triggers survey requirements.
- **Woodland and scrub clearance** — the Forest of Dean and Cotswold escarpment woodlands support Schedule 1 species including peregrine and goshawk.
- **Brownfield sites** — urban and industrial sites in Gloucester and Cheltenham may support black redstart (Schedule 1, red-listed).
- **Wetland and riparian habitats** — the Severn Vale and Cotswold Water Park support a range of Schedule 1 and red-listed wetland birds.

The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) and [[NBN Atlas]] hold distribution data for Schedule 1 and red-listed species that should be checked as part of any ecological assessment.
