# Protected Species

Protected species are animals and plants that receive legal protection under UK and retained EU law. In the planning context, the presence of protected species on or near a development site is a material planning consideration that can affect whether and how permission is granted. Local planning authorities must take protected species into account when determining applications.

---

## Legal Frameworks

Protected species in England fall under three distinct legal frameworks. Understanding which framework applies to a given species is essential, because the procedural obligations — and crucially, the availability of a licensing route — differ significantly.

### 1. European Protected Species (EPS) — Habitats Regulations

The highest level of protection, derived from the EU Habitats Directive and retained in domestic law through the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010 (as amended 2017). Species listed in Schedule 2 of those Regulations receive EPS status. It is illegal to kill, injure, disturb, or destroy the habitats of EPS — even when the animals are not present.

A **three-part derogation test** must be satisfied before a licence can be granted:
1. No satisfactory alternative exists
2. There are imperative reasons of overriding public interest
3. Favourable conservation status of the species will be maintained

Any development affecting EPS requires an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] from Natural England if impacts cannot be avoided. The LPA must be satisfied that a licence will be granted before approving development.

Key EPS in English planning:
- **Bats** (all 18 UK species) — see [[Bats in Planning]]
- **Great crested newt** — see [[Great Crested Newts in Planning]]
- **Otter** — see [[Otters in Planning]]
- Dormouse
- White-clawed crayfish

See [[European Protected Species]] for full detail.

### 2. Wild Birds — Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981

All wild birds are protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (WCA 1981). Schedule 1 birds receive enhanced disturbance protection (e.g. peregrine, goshawk, kingfisher, barn owl). Many birds are also [[Species of Principal Importance]] (S41) or red/amber-listed on conservation concern assessments.

**Critical distinction: there is no development licence for wild birds.** Natural England cannot issue licences permitting construction harm to wild birds. Avoidance — particularly timing works outside the nesting season (broadly March–August) — is mandatory, not optional. There is no licensing fallback.

See [[Wild Birds in Planning]] for full detail.

### 3. Other WCA 1981 Species — Non-EPS

The Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 also protects a wider range of species under Schedules 5 (animals) and 8 (plants) that are not EPS. Protections generally prohibit killing, injuring, or taking listed species. For these species, a **standard mitigation licence** is required where impacts cannot be avoided; the three-part EPS derogation test does **not** apply.

Key planning-relevant examples:
- **Water voles** — Schedule 5 WCA 1981; not EPS; Class Licence CL31 available for small-scale impacts. See [[Water Voles in Planning]].
- Various reptiles, invertebrates, and plant species.

### Separate Framework — Protection of Badgers Act 1992

**Badgers** are protected under a dedicated piece of legislation — the [[Protection of Badgers Act 1992]] — rather than WCA 1981 or the Habitats Regulations. No EPS three-part test applies, but a separate badger licence is required where setts will be disturbed or destroyed. See [[Badgers in Planning]].

### Species of Principal Importance (Section 41)

[[Species of Principal Importance]] are listed under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006. They are a material consideration in planning even where EPS procedural obligations do not apply. The list includes over 900 species — all bat species, great crested newts, water voles, otters, hedgehog, swift, house sparrow, barn owl, and many others.

Gloucestershire's varied habitats — riparian corridors, ancient woodland, lowland meadows, ponds, and urban green spaces — support a wide range of protected species. The county supports nationally important populations of several EPS.

## The Precautionary Principle

Across all Natural England standing advice, a consistent message applies: **absence of a record does not mean absence of a species**. If suitable habitat exists, surveys are required regardless of whether records are held by [[NBN Atlas]] or Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records. LPAs should request surveys where there is a reasonable likelihood of presence.

---

## LPA Obligations

Local planning authorities must:
1. Establish whether protected species are present (or likely to be present)
2. Assess the likely impact of the development
3. Consider whether the development can proceed lawfully
4. Apply the mitigation hierarchy (avoid → mitigate → compensate)
5. Treat protected species impacts as a material consideration
6. For EPS: be satisfied that any required licence will be granted before approving development

Where a development would cause harm to protected species and no lawful route exists (as with wild birds during the nesting season), the LPA may need to refuse permission or require redesign.

Natural England publishes standing advice for the most commonly encountered protected species. Communities can use these obligations to challenge applications where protected species have not been adequately considered.

## Gloucestershire Records and Data

Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) is the county repository for protected species data. Developers and LPAs should consult GCER data alongside [[NBN Atlas]] as a first step in assessing whether protected species surveys are required. GCER data can also be used by communities to identify gaps in applicants' ecological assessments.
