# Natural England — Bats Planning Guidance

When a development could affect bats, [[Natural England]]'s standing advice tells the local planning authority what to take into account — and because it is standing advice, the authority is expected to apply it. This page explains it plainly; the main [[Bats in Planning]] page gives the fuller picture.

## How bats are protected

Every bat species is a [[European Protected Species]] under the [[Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010]] — the strongest wildlife protection there is in England. It is an offence to kill, injure, disturb, or capture a bat, or to damage or destroy a roost, even when the roost is empty. Several bats are also a [[Species of Principal Importance]].

## When a survey is needed

A survey is expected wherever development **could** affect a roost or the habitat bats use to feed and move around. The key point is the precautionary one: **no records does not mean no bats**. Common triggers include buildings or [[Ancient and Veteran Trees|old trees]] that could hold roosts, woodland and water nearby, and new lighting close to bat habitat.

## What you should do, in order

- **Avoid** — redesign to keep roosts, and time works away from the maternity and hibernation seasons.
- **Mitigate** — build new roost features into the scheme, keep commuting routes dark (see [[Dark Corridors]]), and design lighting to spill as little as possible.
- **Compensate** — where a roost must be lost, replace it like for like so there is no net loss of roosts.

Beyond the minimum, schemes should add habitat that helps bats, in step with the [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy]] and [[Biodiversity Net Gain]].

## Getting permission and a licence

Most works affecting bats need an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]]. Crucially, the [[Local Planning Authority]] must satisfy itself that a licence is **likely to be granted** before approving the scheme — it can't leave that to chance.

## Gloucestershire relevance

Bats are the protected species most often met in Gloucestershire applications: the county's ancient woodland, veteran trees, water meadows, and historic buildings give them plenty of habitat. Check the [[Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records|GCER]] for local records. Because of the precautionary rule, a developer can't dodge a survey simply by pointing to a lack of existing records — a useful point for communities responding to applications.
