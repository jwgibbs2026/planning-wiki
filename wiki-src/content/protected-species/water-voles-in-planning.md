# Water Voles in Planning

Water voles (*Arvicola amphibius*) are one of Britain's most rapidly declining mammals. They are legally protected and classified as **endangered** on England's mammal conservation list. Any development near suitable riparian habitat must consider the potential presence of water voles and follow a structured process of survey, impact assessment, mitigation, and — where necessary — licensing.

---

## Legal status

Water voles are protected under the **Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981** (WCA 1981). The following activities are prohibited:

- Intentionally killing, injuring, or taking a water vole
- Damaging or destroying any structure or place used for shelter or protection
- Disturbing a water vole while it occupies a shelter structure
- Obstructing access to any shelter structure

### Important distinction: WCA 1981, not EPS

Water voles are **not** European Protected Species (EPS) under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010. This is a critical legal distinction:

- The **three-part derogation test** (no satisfactory alternative, imperative reasons of overriding public interest, favourable conservation status) that applies to bats, great crested newts, and otters does **not** apply to water voles
- Water voles use a **standard mitigation licence** rather than an EPS mitigation licence
- A simplified **Class Licence CL31** is available for small-scale impacts (see Licensing section below)

Water voles are also designated as a **[[Species of Principal Importance]]** under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006, making them a material consideration in planning decisions.

## Survey triggers

Natural England advises that surveys should be requested when **either** of the following applies:

1. **Historical records or distribution data** indicate possible presence — check [[NBN Atlas]] and Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records as a first step
2. **Suitable habitat** exists within or adjacent to the development site

### Suitable habitat indicators

The following features indicate potential water vole habitat and should trigger a survey request:

- **Soft earth banks** alongside watercourses (suitable for burrow construction)
- **Slow-flowing or still water** — rivers, streams, ditches, ponds, canals
- **Dense bankside vegetation** — rushes, sedges, grasses providing food and cover
- **Predator escape areas** — vegetation structure allowing rapid retreat from mink, herons, and other predators

> **Key principle**: Absence of a record does not mean there are no water voles. The precautionary approach applies — if habitat is suitable, surveys are required regardless of whether records exist.

Surveys must be carried out by a **qualified ecologist** meeting CIEEM competency standards.

## Development impact assessment

Developers must demonstrate how their proposals avoid or mitigate harm. The impact assessment should address:

- The likelihood of water voles being present
- Effects on watercourse conditions (water quality, flow, bank structure)
- Effects on nearby work areas and habitat connectivity

### Types of harm

| Harm type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Habitat destruction | Bank removal, culverting, infilling of ditches |
| Shelter damage | Destruction of burrow systems |
| Water quality changes | Pollution, sedimentation, altered hydrology |
| Habitat fragmentation | Disconnecting populations through development footprint |

Habitat fragmentation is a particular concern: water voles live in linear riparian territories and isolated populations are highly vulnerable to local extinction.

---

## Mitigation hierarchy

The standard mitigation hierarchy applies: **avoidance first, then mitigation, then compensation**. Translocation is a last resort only.

### 1. Avoidance

Redesign the development to prevent negative effects entirely. Maintain habitat connectivity — avoid severing riparian corridors. This is the preferred approach and should be explored before any other measure.

### 2. Mitigation — displacement

The primary mitigation technique is **displacement through vegetation removal**:

- Bankside vegetation is removed progressively in advance of works
- This encourages water voles to relocate naturally to connected alternative habitat nearby
- Works must not begin until displacement is confirmed
- Connected alternative habitat must be available and suitable before displacement begins

This approach avoids the need to physically handle animals and is less stressful than translocation.

### 3. Compensation

Where impact cannot be fully mitigated, compensation measures may include:

- **Habitat provision** — creation or enhancement of new riparian habitat
- **Water quality improvements** — reducing pollution inputs to watercourses
- **Bank enhancement** — creating soft earth banks suitable for burrowing
- **Mink control** — removal of American mink (*Neovison vison*), a major predator of water voles

#### Mink control as compensation

Mink control is an unusual and species-specific compensation measure. American mink were introduced to Britain and are a primary driver of water vole decline — they can enter burrows and kill entire colonies. Controlling mink populations in the vicinity of a development site is recognised by Natural England as a legitimate compensation measure where direct habitat impacts cannot be fully avoided. This reflects the ecological reality that predation pressure can be as damaging as habitat loss.

### 4. Translocation (last resort)

Physical capture and relocation of water voles to a receptor site is considered only as a **last resort** when all other measures have been exhausted. This mirrors the approach in [[Badgers in Planning]] and [[Otters in Planning]]. Translocation requires a standard mitigation licence.

## Licensing

### Standard mitigation licence

A standard mitigation licence from Natural England is required for any project that cannot avoid impacting water voles after applying the mitigation hierarchy. Unlike EPS licensing, the three-part test does not apply — but the developer must still demonstrate that harm has been minimised.

### Class Licence CL31 — simplified route for small impacts

For projects affecting **less than 50 metres of bank**, a simplified **Class Licence CL31** is available. This avoids the need for a full standard mitigation licence application and significantly reduces the administrative burden for small-scale developments.

This is a meaningful practical tool that has no equivalent in the bat, GCN, otter, or badger licensing frameworks. Developers and their ecologists should check whether CL31 applies before pursuing a full licence application.

### Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects

For NSIPs, Natural England confirmation is required separately. This is consistent with the approach for other protected species.

---

## Post-development monitoring

Management plans must address:

- **Vegetation maintenance** — keeping bankside habitat suitable for water voles
- **Pollution and human impact risks** — preventing degradation of water quality
- **Water quality preservation** — ongoing monitoring of watercourse condition
- **Population monitoring** — confirming water voles are using the site post-development

---

## Biodiversity enhancement

Beyond mitigation, developers should incorporate new or enhanced habitats and align biodiversity enhancement with relevant nature conservation strategies — in Gloucestershire, this means the Local Nature Recovery Strategy (when published) and any relevant district-level biodiversity policies.

---

## Gloucestershire context

Water voles are present in Gloucestershire, particularly in the river catchments of the Severn and its tributaries, and in the ditch and drainage networks of the Severn Vale. The Cotswold water park and associated wetland habitats may also support populations.

To assess whether surveys are required for a specific site:

1. Check [[NBN Atlas]] for water vole records within the relevant search radius
2. Contact Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) for county-level distribution data
3. Assess the site for suitable habitat indicators (soft earth banks, slow-flowing water, dense vegetation)

If the Local Nature Recovery Strategy for Gloucestershire identifies water vole recovery corridors when published, developments in those areas will face heightened scrutiny.
