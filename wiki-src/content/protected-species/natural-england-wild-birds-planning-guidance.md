# Natural England — Wild Birds Planning Guidance

When a development could affect wild birds, [[Natural England]]'s standing advice tells the local planning authority what to take into account. This page explains it plainly; see [[Wild Birds in Planning]] and [[Barn Owls in Planning]] for the fuller picture.

## The rule that changes everything: no development licence

For [[European Protected Species]] such as bats, great crested newts, and otters, a licence can permit carefully controlled harm. **For wild birds, no such licence exists.** The only licences available cover things like public health, air safety, and crop protection — never construction. In practice this means harm to birds must be *avoided*; it cannot be licensed away. There is no [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] equivalent for birds.

## What's protected

All wild birds are protected under the [[Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981]], not just rare ones. It is an offence to kill or injure a bird, or to take or destroy an active nest or its eggs. Birds listed on **Schedule 1** get extra protection from disturbance near the nest, and surveys for them must be done by a licensed ecologist. Many birds are also [[Species of Principal Importance]].

## The single most useful rule

Avoid vegetation clearance and intrusive work during the **nesting season, roughly March to August**. For almost any site with hedges, trees, or scrub, timing the work outside this window is the simplest way to stay on the right side of the law.

## When a survey is needed

Expect a survey where the work affects natural habitat (wetland, woodland, scrub, meadow, moorland), mature gardens, old or cavity-bearing trees, or buildings used by nesting birds — and wherever barn owls, Schedule 1 species, or red/amber-listed birds may be present.

## What you should do, in order

- **Avoid** — keep away from breeding areas and work outside March–August.
- **Displace** — outside the breeding season only, block access to nest sites and clear vegetation.
- **Replace** — provide nest boxes (ideally built into the brickwork), ledges for species like peregrines and black redstarts, native planting, and biodiverse green roofs.

New habitat must be in place **before** works begin and achieve no net loss — which ties directly into [[Biodiversity Net Gain]].
