# Dark Corridors

Dark corridors are unlit or low-light landscape features — hedgerows, tree lines, watercourses, woodland edges — that allow bats and other light-sensitive wildlife to move through the landscape between roosts and foraging areas. They are a key mitigation and Green Infrastructure feature in planning for bat-friendly development.

---

## Why Darkness Matters for Bats

Many bat species are highly sensitive to artificial light. Light pollution disrupts their ability to commute between roosts and foraging areas, can prevent access to roost entrances, and reduces the insect prey available in darker areas. Some species — including lesser horseshoe bat and greater horseshoe bat — will abandon roosts or foraging areas if lighting is introduced nearby.

Maintaining dark corridors is therefore not simply a design preference; it is often a legal requirement where [[European Protected Species]] are present.

---

## Dark Corridors in Planning

Natural England standing advice explicitly identifies the establishment of dark commuting corridors as a mitigation measure for bat impacts. This means:

- Lighting schemes must be designed to avoid illuminating bat roosts, roost entrances, and flight lines
- New development should maintain or create unlit routes connecting habitats
- Where lighting is unavoidable, it should be directed away from bat commuting routes, use warm-spectrum LEDs, and be on timers or motion sensors

LPAs can require dark corridor provision through planning conditions or as part of a Biodiversity Net Gain strategy.

---

## Connection to Green Infrastructure

Dark corridors are a component of Green Infrastructure — the network of green and blue spaces that provides multiple benefits for people and wildlife. A well-designed GI network will incorporate:
- Unlit hedgerow and tree-line connections between habitats
- Riparian corridors along watercourses (also key bat foraging habitat)
- Woodland edge habitats maintained without intrusive lighting

The Building with Nature standards developed by Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust and UWE include principles for habitat connectivity that align with dark corridor requirements.

---

## Insect-Rich Habitats

Dark corridors are most effective when combined with insect-rich habitats. Bats are insectivores; their foraging success depends on prey availability. Mitigation plans should therefore include:
- Native wildflower planting along corridor edges
- Retention of deadwood and veteran trees (invertebrate habitat)
- Avoidance of pesticide use in corridor areas
- [[SuDS]] features (ponds, wet margins) which support aquatic invertebrates — a key prey source

---

## Lighting Policy in Local Plans

Several local plans include policies on light pollution and dark skies. Where these exist, they provide an additional policy basis for requiring dark corridor provision beyond bat-specific guidance. Communities developing neighbourhood development plans can include dark corridor policies to protect locally important bat commuting routes.

---

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire contains significant areas of low light pollution, particularly in the Forest of Dean, the Cotswolds, and the upper Severn valley. These areas support populations of light-sensitive bat species including lesser horseshoe bat and barbastelle. Maintaining dark corridors connecting ancient woodland, hedgerow networks, and watercourses is particularly important in these landscapes.

Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds data on bat commuting routes that can inform dark corridor design and planning conditions.
