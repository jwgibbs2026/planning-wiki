# Reptiles in Planning

Reptiles are among the most frequently encountered protected species in ecological surveys, particularly on brownfield land, gardens, railway embankments, and scrubby grassland. All six native reptile species have some level of legal protection under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

## Protected Species Status

| Species | Protection | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Slow worm (*Anguis fragilis*) | Schedule 5 — killing and injuring prohibited | Most common; found in gardens, compost heaps, embankments |
| Grass snake (*Natrix helvetica*) | Schedule 5 — killing and injuring prohibited | Wetland edges, compost heaps, near water |
| Common lizard (*Zootoca vivipara*) | Schedule 5 — killing and injuring prohibited | Heathland, grassland, brownfield |
| Adder (*Vipera berus*) | Schedule 5 — killing and injuring prohibited | Heathland, moorland edges, chalk downland |
| Sand lizard (*Lacerta agilis*) | Schedule 5 + [[European Protected Species|EPS]] | Heathland and sand dunes; not in Gloucestershire |
| Smooth snake (*Coronella austriaca*) | Schedule 5 + EPS | Heathland; not in Gloucestershire |

Schedule 5 protection (under the WCA 1981) prohibits **intentional killing or injuring** but does **not** require a development licence as EPS do. There is no equivalent of the three-part EPS test for slow worms or grass snakes. However, development that would harm reptiles in a reckless (not just intentional) way may still be unlawful.

## Survey Triggers

A reptile survey should be considered where habitat assessment identifies:
- **Brownfield land** (rubble, scrub, rank grassland — classic slow worm habitat)
- **Embankments** (railway, road, or garden embankments with south-facing aspect)
- **Rough grassland and scrub** — particularly on south-facing slopes
- **Compost heaps or log piles**
- **Heathland or calcareous grassland** edges
- **Woodland rides and clearings**
- **Buffer zones adjacent to known reptile habitat**

The GCER and [[NBN Atlas]] hold reptile records and should be checked as part of any extended Phase 1 survey.

## Survey Method

The standard survey method uses **artificial refugia (ARs)** — corrugated metal sheeting or roofing felt — placed on the ground in suitable habitat. Reptiles bask beneath ARs on warm mornings.

- **Minimum standard**: 7 visits during the active season; at least 5 visits between April–June (peak season)
- **Active season**: March–October (broadly); peak is April–June when reptiles are most active
- **Survey timing**: mornings, ideally when air temperature is 9–17°C and partially overcast — reptiles are not active in very hot conditions or rain
- **ARs placement**: at least 7 days before surveys begin; at density of ~10 per hectare in suitable habitat

Results are reported as: absent, low, medium, or high population.

## Mitigation Hierarchy

Where reptiles are present and habitat will be lost, mitigation follows the standard hierarchy:

1. **Avoid** — retain habitat where possible; design out direct impacts
2. **Displacement (soft clearance)** — gradual vegetation clearance during active season to encourage reptiles to move out of the footprint before construction
3. **Translocation** — capture and move reptiles to a prepared receptor site when displacement alone is insufficient; requires a suitably qualified ecologist; receptor site must be of equal or better quality

Translocation of reptiles does **not** require a licence (unlike EPS translocation) but must be carried out competently and the receptor site must be agreed with the LPA.

## Biodiversity Net Gain

Reptile habitat (scrub, rank grassland, brownfield) must be accounted for in the [[Biodiversity Metric]] calculation. Loss of reptile habitat contributes to the deficit that BNG must offset. Receptor sites for translocation can also contribute to off-site BNG habitat accounts if established on a long-term basis.

## Gloucestershire Context

The most commonly encountered reptile in Gloucestershire is the **slow worm** — abundant in gardens and rough grassland throughout the county. Grass snakes are frequent near the wetland habitats of the Severn Vale, Slimbridge, and the Cotswolds Water Park. Adders occur on the limestone grassland and scrub of the Cotswolds escarpment.

Sand lizard and smooth snake have no established Gloucestershire population.
