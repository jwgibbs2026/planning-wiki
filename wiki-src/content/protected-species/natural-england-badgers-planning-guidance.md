# Natural England — Badgers Planning Guidance

When a development could affect badgers, [[Natural England]] publishes "standing advice" that the local planning authority must take into account when it decides the application. This page explains what that advice says and what it means in practice. It sits alongside the main [[Badgers in Planning]] page, which gives the wider picture.

## The law that applies

Badgers are protected by their own piece of legislation — the [[Protection of Badgers Act 1992]] — rather than the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 or the Habitats Regulations. This matters: the three-part licence test used for bats, great crested newts, and otters does **not** apply to badgers. Instead, it is an offence to kill, injure, or take a badger, or to damage, destroy, or block a sett.

## When a survey is needed

A survey is expected whenever a site has habitat that could suit badgers, or where past records suggest they are present. The [[NBN Atlas]] is the recommended first place to check for distribution records, and the [[Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records|GCER]] holds the local picture. Survey findings are usually kept confidential, because badgers are a frequent target of persecution.

## What you should do, in order

The emphasis is on designing harm out before mitigating it:

- **Avoid** — lay the site out to keep clear of setts, remove lighting near them, and retain the vegetation badgers use.
- **Mitigate** — where avoidance isn't fully possible, protect setts during construction and keep routes open.
- **Compensate** — as a last resort only. Moving badgers (translocation) is actively discouraged because of the risk of spreading bovine TB and the welfare problems involved — a constraint unique to this species.

## Getting permission and a licence

Any licence to interfere with a sett is granted site by site — there is no streamlined district-level route for badgers, as there is for newts. Planning conditions must line up with the licence before permission is granted, so the two are best dealt with together.
