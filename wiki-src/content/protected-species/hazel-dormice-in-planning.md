# Hazel Dormice in Planning

Hazel dormice (*Muscardinus avellanarius*) are one of England's most legally protected mammals. They are a [[European Protected Species]] (EPS) and a [[Species of Principal Importance]] under the NERC Act 2006. Their presence on or near a development site triggers a specific set of procedural obligations for developers and local planning authorities (LPAs).

Natural England's standing advice on hazel dormice is a material consideration in planning decisions.

---

## Legal Status

Hazel dormice receive protection under two overlapping legal frameworks:

**Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010** (2017 consolidation — the operative instrument): As EPS, it is an offence to:
- Deliberately kill, injure, disturb, or capture dormice
- Damage or destroy breeding sites or resting places
- Possess, transport, sell, or offer dormice for sale (dead or alive)

**Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981**: Provides additional domestic protection, including Schedule 5 listing.

**[[Species of Principal Importance]]**: Dormice are listed under Section 41 of the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006. This makes their conservation a material consideration in planning decisions, even where EPS obligations do not fully apply.

**Red List status**: Hazel dormice are classified as Vulnerable on England's Red List for Mammals — a declining species with a contracting range.

---

## Survey Triggers

An ecological survey is required when **either** of the following applies:

1. **Historical records or distribution data** suggest dormice are present or have been recorded near the site (check [[NBN Atlas]] and Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records)
2. **Suitable habitat is present**: woodland, hedgerow, or scrub habitat within or adjacent to the development footprint

The habitat-based trigger is relatively broad. The presence of suitable habitat alone — even without confirmed records — is sufficient to require survey. This reflects the precautionary approach applied to EPS.

---

## Survey Methods

See [[Dormouse Surveys]] for full detail. In summary:

- **Visual nest and hazelnut searches**: Appropriate for minor projects or initial scoping. Dormice leave distinctive tooth marks on hazelnuts; nests are woven from grass, honeysuckle bark, and leaves.
- **Nest tubes**: Artificial tubes placed along hedgerows and woodland edges; checked periodically for signs of use (hair, droppings, nests).
- **Nest boxes**: Similar to bird boxes but designed for dormice; checked during the active season.
- **Footprint tracking tunnels**: Tunnels baited to capture footprints on tracking paper; useful for confirming presence without handling animals.

Surveyor competency should meet CIEEM standards. Handling dormice requires a survey licence from Natural England.

---

## Survey Currency

Surveys up to **three years old** are acceptable if habitats on the site remain unchanged. This is the dormouse-specific rule — note that great crested newts require four-season data, so survey currency rules are species-specific and should not be conflated.

If habitats have changed materially since the survey, new surveys will be required regardless of age.

---

## Presence Assumption Rule

If dormice are found **anywhere** on a site, their presence must be assumed throughout **all suitable habitat** on that site. This is a critical precautionary principle with significant practical implications:

- Survey scope must cover all suitable habitat, not just the immediate development footprint
- Mitigation design must account for the full extent of potentially occupied habitat
- A partial survey finding cannot be used to limit mitigation obligations

---

## Impact Assessment

Developers must demonstrate how their proposals avoid or mitigate harm to dormice. Harmful activities include:

- Removal of woodland, hedgerow, or scrub habitat
- Disturbance from noise or artificial light during the active season (April–October)
- Loss of foraging habitat (particularly hazel, honeysuckle, bramble, and other food plants)
- Hedgerow removal that physically isolates dormouse populations, preventing movement between habitat patches

---

## Mitigation Hierarchy

Mitigation must follow the standard hierarchy — avoidance first, compensation last:

1. **Habitat redesign**: Retain as much woodland, hedgerow, and scrub as possible; redesign the development layout to avoid the most sensitive areas
2. **Timing adjustments**: Schedule clearance works outside the active season where possible; dormice hibernate from approximately October to April
3. **Habitat improvement**: Thinning, coppicing, and planting to enhance retained habitat; increase structural diversity and food plant availability
4. **Nest box installation**: Provide artificial nesting sites to supplement natural nest availability
5. **[[Green Bridges]]**: Where development creates a barrier (e.g., a road), green bridges or other connectivity structures can reconnect fragmented populations
6. **Translocation**: Moving dormice from the development site to a suitable receptor site. This is a **last resort** — it requires specialist expertise, a suitable and available receptor site, and a licence from Natural England. Translocation success rates are variable and it does not substitute for habitat retention.

---

## Licensing

Before any works that would affect dormice (or their breeding/resting sites) can proceed, the developer must obtain an **[[EPS Mitigation Licence]]** from Natural England.

The standard **three-part test** applies — all three conditions must be met:

1. The development must be for an imperative reason of overriding public interest (IROPI), or for the purpose of preserving public health or public safety, or for other imperative reasons
2. There must be no satisfactory alternative
3. The favourable conservation status of the species must be maintained

LPAs should coordinate planning conditions with Natural England's licensing requirements before granting permission. A planning permission does not authorise works that would otherwise be unlawful under the Habitats Regulations.

---

## Long-Term Monitoring

Approved mitigation schemes must include a long-term monitoring plan covering:

- Population status (presence, breeding evidence, population trends)
- Vegetation management (ensuring retained and created habitat remains suitable)
- Foraging and nesting habitat availability
- Effectiveness of mitigation measures (nest boxes, green bridges, habitat improvements)

Monitoring is typically required for a number of years post-development and results should be reported to Natural England.

---

## Gloucestershire Context

Hazel dormice have a patchy but significant distribution in Gloucestershire. Key habitats include:

- **Woodland edges and rides**: The Forest of Dean and Cotswold escarpment woodlands provide core habitat; dormice favour structurally diverse woodland with a shrub layer
- **Hedgerow networks**: The hedgerow-rich landscapes of the Cotswolds and Severn Vale are important for dormouse movement between woodland patches; hedgerow removal is a significant threat
- **Scrub**: Transitional scrub habitats, particularly with hazel, honeysuckle, and bramble, are important foraging areas

Developers and LPAs should check Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records and [[NBN Atlas]] for local distribution data before concluding that dormice are absent. Given the species' declining range, precautionary survey is strongly advisable wherever suitable habitat exists.

Dormouse habitat connectivity — particularly hedgerow networks linking woodland patches — is a strong candidate for protection and enhancement policies in Neighbourhood Development Plans covering rural Gloucestershire parishes.
