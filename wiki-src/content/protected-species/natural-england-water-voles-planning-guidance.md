# Natural England — Water Voles Planning Guidance

When a development could affect water voles, [[Natural England]]'s standing advice tells the local planning authority what to take into account. This page explains it plainly; the main [[Water Voles in Planning]] page has the full detail.

## How water voles are protected

Water voles are protected under the [[Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981]] — **not** the European Protected Species framework. That difference matters: the three-part licence test used for bats, newts, and otters does not apply here. A separate licensing regime is used, including a simplified **Class Licence CL31** for small works affecting less than 50 metres of bank — a practical shortcut not available for the other species. Water voles are also a [[Species of Principal Importance]], reflecting how steeply their numbers have fallen.

## When a survey is needed

A survey is expected where there are past records, distribution data, or suitable habitat — soft earth banks, slow-flowing water, good bankside vegetation, and places to escape predators. Work on the precautionary principle: no records does not mean no water voles. The [[NBN Atlas]] is the first check, and [[Water Vole Surveys]] explains the method.

## What you should do, in order

- **Avoid** — keep work away from occupied banks.
- **Displace** — where some disturbance is unavoidable, cut back vegetation ahead of works so animals move to connected habitat nearby.
- **Compensate** — including, unusually, controlling American mink, the predator most responsible for water vole decline.
- **Translocate** — only as a genuine last resort.

## Licensing

Most schemes use a standard mitigation licence, but the **Class Licence CL31** offers a lighter-touch route for impacts on less than 50 metres of bank. A management plan should monitor the site afterwards.
