# Bat Surveys

Bat surveys are ecological assessments carried out to determine whether bats are present on or near a development site, what species are involved, how the site is used, and what the likely impact of development would be. They are required whenever development **could** affect bat roosts, foraging habitat, or commuting routes.

---

## When Are Surveys Required?

Natural England standing advice is clear: the trigger for surveys is **potential impact**, not confirmed presence. The precautionary principle applies — **absence of records does not indicate absence of bats**.

LPAs should request surveys when any of the following are present:

**Buildings:**
- Minimal artificial lighting; proximity to woodland or water
- Uneven roof tiles, large timbers, cracks, crevices, or small openings
- South-facing walls with hanging tiles

**Trees:**
- Ancient, veteran, or within ancient woodland
- Natural cavities (ash, beech, and oak are particularly important)
- Damage from rot, weathering, or wildlife; loose or creviced bark; dense ivy

**Other features:**
- Water bodies (rivers, ponds, ditches) — key foraging habitat
- Proposed lighting near bat habitat
- Wind turbines within 50 metres of bat habitat

---

## What Surveys Must Establish

A bat survey must be sufficient to:
- Identify species present and population size
- Determine roost types (maternity, hibernation, transitional, day roost) and their importance
- Map flight routes and foraging areas
- Assess the likely impact of the proposed development

This information is needed both to inform the planning decision and to support any application for an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]].

---

## Survey Methods

Common survey methods include:

| Method | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Roost inspection (internal) | Identify roost features, droppings, staining |
| Emergence/re-entry surveys | Count bats leaving/returning at dusk/dawn; identify species by echolocation |
| Activity monitoring (static detectors) | Record bat activity over time across a site |
| Transect surveys | Map commuting and foraging routes |
| Radio tagging and tracking | Trace individual bats to roost locations |

The choice of methods depends on the development type, the features present, and the species likely to be encountered.

---

## Seasonal Timing

Bat surveys must be carried out at the right time of year. Different roost types are active at different seasons:

| Season | Survey Focus |
|---|---|
| Spring (April–May) | Emergence from hibernation; early maternity roost activity |
| Summer (May–August) | Maternity roosts at peak; best for emergence surveys |
| Autumn (August–October) | Swarming behaviour; transitional roosts |
| Winter (November–March) | Hibernation roosts; limited survey options |

Surveys submitted outside appropriate seasonal windows may be rejected by LPAs or Natural England as insufficient.

---

## Surveyor Credentials

Bat surveys must be carried out by suitably licensed and qualified surveyors. Surveyors handling bats must hold a bat handling licence issued by Natural England. The **Bat Earned Recognition (Bat ER)** scheme accredits surveyors and organisations demonstrating competence — this may enable faster processing of [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] applications.

---

## Using Survey Results in Planning

Survey results inform:
1. Whether the development will affect bats and how significantly
2. What mitigation measures are needed (see [[Bats in Planning]])
3. Whether an [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] is required
4. What conditions should be attached to any planning permission

Communities and parish councils can scrutinise survey reports submitted with planning applications. Key questions to ask:
- Was the survey carried out at the right time of year?
- Does it cover all relevant features (buildings, trees, water)?
- Was it carried out by a licensed surveyor?
- Does it adequately address all species likely to be present?

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) holds bat distribution data for the county. This data should be consulted when scoping surveys — it can help identify which species are likely to be present and therefore what survey methods are most appropriate. GCER data can also be used by communities to identify whether a developer's survey scope appears inadequate.
