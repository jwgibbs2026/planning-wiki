# Otters in Planning

Otters are one of the most legally protected mammals in England and one of the three species most commonly encountered in planning decisions involving [[European Protected Species]] (EPS). Any development near watercourses, rivers, streams, lakes, or marshland must consider whether otters are present and whether the proposal could harm them or their habitats.

---

## Legal Status

Otters hold the highest tier of legal protection available in England:

- **European Protected Species** under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010 (2017 consolidation) — prohibitions on deliberate killing, injury, disturbance, capture, and destruction of breeding sites and resting places
- **Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981** — additional domestic protection
- **Species of Principal Importance** (S41 list) under the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act 2006 — a material consideration in planning decisions even where EPS licensing is not triggered

These protections apply to otters themselves, their holts (resting and breeding places), and their movement routes.

---

## Survey Triggers

A survey is required when any of the following apply:

1. **Distribution records** suggest otters may be present — check the [[NBN Atlas]] and contact the Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records for local data
2. **Development affects water bodies** — rivers, streams, lakes, ponds, ditches, marshland, or their banks
3. **Indirect effects** — development that could affect nearby water habitats through noise, light pollution, or changes to water quality

The water proximity trigger is broad. In Gloucestershire, the Severn, Avon, Churn, Coln, Leadon, Windrush, and their tributaries mean that otter surveys will be relevant to a large proportion of development proposals across all six districts.

---

## Survey Methods

Survey work covers:

- **Presence/absence surveys** — searching for field signs (spraints, footprints, holts, slides, feeding remains)
- **Habitat evaluation** — assessing the quality and connectivity of riparian habitat

Unlike [[Bat Surveys]] (which have a detailed seasonal calendar) or [[GCN Surveys]] (which use eDNA and HSI scoring), otter survey methodology is less formally standardised in national guidance. The key requirement is that ecologists are qualified and experienced. Certain activities — such as entering or disturbing a holt — require a survey licence from Natural England.

There is no equivalent of the 4-season bat survey requirement or the eDNA/HSI framework for GCN. Practitioners may refer to Mammal Society or Otter Trust guidance for methodological standards.

---

## Assessing Effects on Otters

Development proposals must address:

- The likelihood of otter presence and use of the site
- The scale and proximity of development to otter habitat

**Potential harm sources include:**

| Harm Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Habitat loss | Removal of bankside vegetation, riparian scrub, or wetland |
| Habitat fragmentation | Roads, culverts, or structures blocking movement routes |
| Loss of holts | Destruction of resting or breeding places |
| Disturbance | Noise, vibration, or light during sensitive periods |
| Water quality | Pollution or sedimentation affecting fish prey |

---

## Mitigation Hierarchy

The standard EPS mitigation hierarchy applies: avoidance first, then mitigation, then compensation.

### Avoidance
- Avoid working near water bodies and known otter habitat
- Establish buffer zones along riverbanks
- Schedule construction work outside nighttime hours (otters are largely crepuscular and nocturnal)

> **Note**: Nighttime work restrictions for otters may conflict with requirements for other species on the same site — for example, [[Bats in Planning]] guidance focuses on avoiding disturbance to nocturnal bat activity. Where multiple EPS are present, mitigation schedules must be carefully coordinated.

### Mitigation
- Retain otter habitats in water and on banks during construction
- Install otter-proof fencing to exclude otters from dangerous working areas

### Compensation
- **Artificial holts** — constructed resting and breeding places to replace lost holts
- **Mammal ledges** — platforms installed on bridges and culverts to allow otters to pass without entering the water (important where road crossings are involved)
- **Viaducts and underpasses** — larger crossing structures at significant movement barriers
- **Habitat restoration** — improving or creating riparian habitat to offset losses

Mammal ledges and artificial holts are distinctive, checkable planning conditions. Communities and parish councils reviewing planning applications near watercourses should look for these measures in ecological reports and draft conditions.

---

## Licensing

Where a development cannot avoid harming otters or their habitats, a **mitigation licence** from Natural England is required. The standard [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] three-part test applies:

1. The development must be for an imperative reason of overriding public interest (IROPI) or other permitted purpose
2. There must be no satisfactory alternative
3. The favourable conservation status of the species must be maintained

**There is no District Level Licensing equivalent for otters.** Unlike great crested newts (where [[District Level Licensing]] offers a population-scale alternative), every otter case requires a site-by-site licence application to Natural England. This makes early survey and mitigation planning especially important.

---

## LPA Gatekeeping Duty

Before granting planning permission, a Local Planning Authority must:

- Be satisfied that Natural England is likely to grant a mitigation licence
- Ensure planning conditions align with the requirements of that licence
- Attach mitigation and compensation measures as enforceable planning conditions

This gatekeeping duty mirrors the obligation that applies to [[Bats in Planning]] and [[Great Crested Newts in Planning]]. An LPA that grants permission without addressing otter impacts where surveys indicate presence risks a legally flawed decision.

---

## Post-Development Monitoring

Approved management plans must:

- Maintain habitat suitability through active management
- Monitor for movement barriers introduced by the development
- Track human-related pressures (disturbance, water quality)
- Verify mitigation effectiveness through follow-up surveys and remedial action where needed

---

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire's river network makes otter survey triggers relevant across the county:

- **River Severn** — the county's main artery; otter presence well-documented
- **River Avon** — southern Gloucestershire
- **Rivers Churn, Coln, Leach, Windrush** — Cotswolds catchments
- **River Leadon** — Forest of Dean and western Gloucestershire
- **Smaller watercourses and drainage ditches** — widespread across the Vale of Gloucester and Severn Vale

The Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records holds local otter distribution data and should be consulted alongside the [[NBN Atlas]] at pre-application stage. Otter populations in England have recovered significantly since the 1970s, meaning presence near suitable habitat is increasingly likely.
