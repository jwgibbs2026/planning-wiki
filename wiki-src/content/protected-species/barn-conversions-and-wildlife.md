# Barn Conversions and Wildlife

The conversion of agricultural buildings to residential or other uses is one of the most common rural planning scenarios in Gloucestershire and frequently one of the most ecologically sensitive. Old farm buildings — barns, cattle sheds, granaries, and stables — provide nesting and roosting habitat for a wide range of protected species. Ecological surveys are almost always required.

## Why Agricultural Buildings Matter Ecologically

Traditional farm buildings, particularly pre-1960 stone, brick, or timber structures, offer:
- **Bat roosts**: roof spaces, gaps between tiles, wall cavities, exposed roof timbers; many barns support multiple bat species including nationally significant roosts
- **Barn owl nest sites**: open-plan barns with rodent access are classic barn owl habitat; Gloucestershire is a stronghold
- **Swift and swallow nests**: open-fronted barns and buildings with large gaps are used by swallows; swifts nest in cavities
- **Jackdaw and stock dove nest sites**: chimneys, roof spaces, and loft voids
- **Reptile habitat**: southern-facing rubble, corrugated iron sheets, rough grassland around buildings
- **Bats foraging habitat**: the surrounding pasture and hedgerow network is critical bat foraging habitat linked to roosts in buildings

Modern steel-framed agricultural buildings generally have lower ecological value, but even these may have bat roost potential in roof spaces.

## Survey Requirements

Before planning permission (or prior approval) is granted for conversion, the LPA will typically require:

### Bat Surveys
Required for virtually all agricultural building conversions. Bats are [[European Protected Species]] — the presence of a roost prevents commencement of works that would disturb, injure, or kill bats or damage/destroy a roost without a licence.

- **Initial inspection**: a dusk emergence and/or dawn re-entry survey; scoping survey to identify potential roost features
- **Full survey**: if potential features identified, a minimum of two dusk/dawn surveys in the active season (May–October); three surveys may be needed for complex buildings or rare species
- **Survey season**: May–October for activity surveys; hibernation roosts require winter surveys (November–March)
- See [[Bats in Planning]] and [[Bat Surveys]] for full methodology

### Barn Owl Surveys
- Presence/absence survey: site visit to assess evidence (pellets, feathers, whitewash); dawn/dusk watch
- Barn owls are [[Species of Principal Importance]] (S41); active nests protected under WCA 1981
- Mitigation: provision of a purpose-built barn owl nest box in or adjacent to the building; retained access holes in new build; ideally a separate owl tower or retained outbuilding
- See [[Barn Owls in Planning]]

### Swift and Swallow Surveys
- Dawn/dusk emergence watches during the nesting season (May–August)
- Swallows nest openly inside open-fronted barns — their nests are protected during the nesting season
- See [[Swifts in Planning]]

### Reptile Surveys
- Required where there is rough grassland, rubble, or bramble/scrub around the building footprint
- See [[Reptiles in Planning]]

## Prior Approval (Permitted Development)

Many agricultural building conversions use the **prior approval** route under Class Q of the General Permitted Development Order (GPDO) rather than full planning permission. Class Q allows conversion of agricultural buildings to dwellinghouses without full planning permission, subject to certain conditions.

**Ecology is still required under prior approval**: the LPA must assess the proposal for impacts on protected species, and prior approval applications must address ecological constraints. The prior approval route does not bypass the need for bat surveys, barn owl surveys, or EPS mitigation licences.

## EPS Licence Requirements

If bats are present and conversion works cannot avoid disturbing them:
1. A **Method Statement** (ecological mitigation plan) must be agreed
2. Planning permission or prior approval must be granted
3. An [[EPS Mitigation Licence]] must be obtained from Natural England **before works commence**
4. The three-part test must be satisfied: no satisfactory alternative; imperative reasons of overriding public interest; favourable conservation status maintained

Roost retention (keeping existing roost features within the conversion design), timing of works to avoid sensitive seasons, and provision of replacement roost features are the primary mitigation measures.

## Mitigation by Species

| Species | Key mitigation |
|---------|---------------|
| Bats | Timing works outside sensitive seasons; retain roof voids; integrated bat boxes/tubes; licence if roost affected |
| Barn owls | Nest box in building or adjacent structure; retained access holes |
| Swallows | Works outside nesting season (September–March); retain access if possible |
| Swifts | Swift bricks integrated into converted building; works outside May–August |
| Reptiles | Soft clearance of surrounding vegetation; translocation if necessary |

## Biodiversity Net Gain

Barn conversions are subject to mandatory Biodiversity Net Gain where they require full planning permission. The conversion of an agricultural building typically involves:
- Loss of building-associated habitat value (often low in the metric)
- Loss of surrounding field, scrub, or grassland habitat if the curtilage changes
- Gain from new landscaping, bat/bird boxes, and habitat management in the garden area

A [[Biodiversity Gain Plan]] must be submitted and approved before works commence.

## Gloucestershire Context

Agricultural building conversions are among the most frequently determined applications in rural Gloucestershire. The county has a high density of traditional stone and brick farm buildings, many of which support significant bat roosts (including greater and lesser horseshoe bats — very sensitive species), barn owl pairs, and swift colonies. The [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] has particularly strict design requirements for conversions alongside ecological requirements.
