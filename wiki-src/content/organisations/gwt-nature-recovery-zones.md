# GWT Nature Recovery Zones

The Nature Recovery Zones are the [[Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust]]'s way of helping wildlife beyond the boundaries of its own nature reserves. By working with neighbouring farmers and landowners, the Trust links up habitat into larger, better-connected "super reserves" — the "bigger, better, more, joined up" idea from the 2010 Lawton Report.

## The ten zones

Each zone is built around the habitats and species that make that part of the county special:

| Zone | What it's known for |
|------|---------------------|
| Cotswolds Escarpment | Limestone grassland and ancient woodland |
| Severn Hams | Floodplain wetlands and wading birds |
| Windrush Valley | Floodplain meadows and river valley |
| Slad Valley | Limestone grassland, orchids, white-clawed crayfish |
| Stroud Commons | Grasslands, woodlands, and bat habitat |
| The Golden Valley | Large blue butterflies and rich insect life |
| Central Forest | Pine marten and a varied habitat mosaic |
| Lower Wye and Tidenham Chase | Ancient woodland and heathland |
| Leadon Valley | Farmland, wildlife fragments, and wild daffodils |
| Lower Woods to Westonbirt | Ancient woodland and species-rich meadows |

## Why it matters in planning

The Trust uses these zones to focus its planning advice, and they line up with the spatial priorities in the [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy]]. Directing off-site [[Biodiversity Net Gain]] into a zone makes the gains join up rather than scatter, and the zones are a useful reference when Local Plans and neighbourhood plans set [[Green Infrastructure|green infrastructure]] priorities.
