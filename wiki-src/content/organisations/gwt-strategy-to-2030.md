# GWT Bringing Nature Back — Strategy to 2030

*Bringing Nature Back* is the [[Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust]]'s plan to stop and reverse wildlife decline in the county by 2030. It sets out three goals, each with concrete targets for managing land, creating habitat, involving people, and tackling climate change.

The starting point is stark: less than 1.5% of the Cotswolds' wildflower meadows survive (down from 40%), and around 70% of Gloucestershire's traditional orchards have been lost. The Trust currently looks after about 1,100 hectares and has some 28,000 members.

## The three goals

- **Stop and reverse wildlife decline.** Grow the land managed for nature to 1,700 hectares, work toward 30% of all the county's land supporting nature, add 300 hectares of urban green infrastructure, and develop ten [[GWT Nature Recovery Zones|Nature Recovery Zones]].
- **Get more people taking action.** Reach one person in four across the county, involve 25,000 schoolchildren, and support 15,000 people to take meaningful action for nature.
- **Make natural climate solutions normal.** Help create 2,000 hectares of woodland (Severn Treescapes), 300 hectares of new wetland (Wildbelt), and wildlife improvements across 5,000 hectares of farmland.

## The big programmes

- **Severn Treescapes** — millions of new trees linking the Forest of Dean and Wye Valley to the Wyre Forest.
- **Wildbelt** — new wetland along the River Severn that helps with natural flood management as well as wildlife.
- **[[GWT Nature Recovery Zones|Nature Recovery Zones]]** — ten areas across the county where habitat is expanded out from the Trust's reserves.
- **[[Building with Nature]]** — the standard the Trust promotes for nature-friendly development.
