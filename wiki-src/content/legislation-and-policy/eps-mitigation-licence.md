# EPS Mitigation Licence

An EPS Mitigation Licence (sometimes called a species licence or derogation licence) is a legal permission issued by Natural England that allows a developer to carry out activities that would otherwise be illegal under the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017 (2017 consolidation). It is required when development will unavoidably affect [[European Protected Species]] — most commonly bats, great crested newts, or otters — and cannot avoid disturbing, harming, or destroying their habitats after applying the mitigation hierarchy (avoidance → mitigation → compensation).

---

## When a Licence Is Required

The Habitats Regulations make it illegal to deliberately kill, injure, or disturb EPS, or to damage or destroy their breeding sites and resting places. These prohibitions apply even where planning permission has been granted. A licence is needed when a development will:

- Disturb EPS (e.g. disturbing bats roosting in a building during demolition)
- Damage or destroy a breeding site or resting place (e.g. destroying a bat roost, a great crested newt breeding pond, or an otter holt)
- Capture or handle EPS as part of translocation

The licence must be obtained **before works begin** — it is not retrospective.

---

## The Three-Part Test

Natural England can only issue an EPS licence if all three conditions are satisfied. This test derives from Article 16 of the EU Habitats Directive, retained in domestic law:

1. **No satisfactory alternative** — there must be no other reasonable way to achieve the development objective that would cause less harm to the species
2. **Overriding public interest** — the development must serve an imperative reason of overriding public interest (IROPI), or fall within one of the other specified purposes listed in the Regulations (e.g. public health and safety, research, education, or conservation)
3. **Favourable conservation status** — the licence must not adversely affect the favourable conservation status of the species at a population level

If any condition cannot be met, the licence cannot be issued — and planning permission should not be granted.

---

## Species Covered by EPS Mitigation Licence

EPS Mitigation Licences are available for all [[European Protected Species]], including:

- **Bats** (all species) — see [[Bats in Planning]]
- **Great crested newts** — see [[Great Crested Newts in Planning]]; [[District Level Licensing]] is an alternative population-scale route
- **Otters** — see [[Otters in Planning]]; no DLL equivalent exists
- **Dormice** and other EPS listed under the Habitats Regulations

---

## Species NOT Covered — Critical Distinctions

Not all protected species fall under the EPS licensing framework. Several commonly confused cases:

### Wild Birds — No Development Licence

**There is no EPS Mitigation Licence equivalent for wild birds.** Natural England cannot issue any licence permitting construction harm to wild birds. Bird licences exist only for purposes such as public health, air safety, scientific research, and crop protection — not to facilitate development. Avoidance is mandatory for birds: works affecting nesting birds must be timed to avoid the active nesting season, or nests must be confirmed absent before works begin. See [[Wild Birds in Planning]].

### Badgers — Separate Licensing Regime

Badgers are protected under the **Protection of Badgers Act 1992**, not the Habitats Regulations. The three-part EPS test does not apply. Badger licensing operates under a separate framework, and a separate badger licence is required for sett interference. See [[Badgers in Planning]].

### Water Voles — WCA 1981 Licensing

Water voles are protected under the **Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981**, not the Habitats Regulations. The three-part EPS test does **not** apply to water voles. Instead:

- A **standard mitigation licence** is required for projects that cannot avoid impacting water voles
- A simplified **Class Licence CL31** is available for projects affecting **less than 50 metres of bank** — this avoids the need for a full standard mitigation licence application and has no equivalent in the EPS framework

This distinction is important: water voles are **endangered** on England's mammal list and are a [[Species of Principal Importance]], yet they have a simpler licensing pathway than EPS for small-scale impacts. See [[Water Voles in Planning]].

## Relationship to Planning Permission

Planning permission and an EPS licence are **separate legal requirements** issued by different bodies. Planning permission is granted by the Local Planning Authority; the EPS licence is issued by Natural England. However, they are procedurally linked in an important way:

> **LPAs must be satisfied that a licence will be granted before approving development.**

This means LPAs cannot simply grant permission and leave licensing to be resolved later. They must assess whether the three-part test is likely to be met as part of their planning decision. Granting permission without this assessment creates legal risk for the LPA and may result in a development that cannot lawfully be built.

In practice, the typical sequence is:
- Ecological surveys must be completed before or during the planning application
- A mitigation strategy must be submitted with the application, sufficient for the LPA to assess licence likelihood
- Planning conditions must be worded to align with likely licence requirements
- Planning permission is granted before a licence application is submitted to Natural England
- The licence application follows after planning permission is granted

LPAs should consult Natural England where there is uncertainty. Planning conditions can be used to require licence approval before works commence, but best practice is for applicants to submit sufficient survey data and a mitigation strategy at application stage.

---

## What a Licence Covers

A mitigation licence will specify:
- The activities permitted (e.g. demolition of a roost, destruction of a pond, disturbance of an otter holt)
- The mitigation measures required (e.g. bat boxes, replacement ponds, otter holts)
- The timing of works (e.g. outside hibernation or breeding periods)
- Monitoring and reporting requirements

---

## Types of Licence

Natural England issues several types of licence relevant to EPS:

- **EPS mitigation licence** — the standard individual licence for specific development projects
- **Natural England bat mitigation class licence** — a streamlined route for lower-risk bat projects meeting defined criteria
- **Bat Earned Recognition (Bat ER)** — an accreditation scheme for surveyors and developers demonstrating competence; may enable faster licence processing
- **District Level Licensing (DLL)** — for great crested newts specifically, an alternative strategic approach (see below)

---

## Planning Conditions

Where a mitigation licence is anticipated, planning conditions typically require:

- Submission and approval of a detailed mitigation and compensation method statement
- Appointment of a named ecological clerk of works or supervising ecologist
- Pre-commencement surveys to confirm current species status
- Post-development monitoring and reporting

---

## Monitoring and Management

Licences typically include conditions requiring:
- Post-works monitoring to verify that mitigation has been implemented correctly
- Monitoring of species return rates to replacement or created habitats
- Management plans to ensure roosting sites, ponds, holts, or other habitat features remain functional and accessible over time

The duration of monitoring should be proportionate to the severity of the impact.

## Species-Specific Notes

### Bats
Bat mitigation licences are the most commonly issued EPS licences. Survey requirements follow the BCT Good Practice Guidelines. See [[Bats in Planning]].

### Great Crested Newts
GCN mitigation licences can be obtained via the standard site-by-site route or through [[District Level Licensing]] (DLL), which offers a faster, population-scale alternative in participating areas. See [[Great Crested Newts in Planning]].

### Otters
Otter mitigation licences follow the standard site-by-site route. **There is no District Level Licensing equivalent for otters.** Every otter case requires an individual licence application to Natural England. This makes early survey work and mitigation planning especially important for developments near watercourses. See [[Otters in Planning]].

---

## District Level Licensing — An Alternative for Great Crested Newts

For great crested newts specifically, [[District Level Licensing]] (DLL) offers an alternative to the traditional site-by-site mitigation licence. Under DLL, developers pay into a strategic habitat creation scheme rather than designing on-site mitigation, and a district-wide conservation strategy replaces individual site licences. No equivalent exists for bats, otters, water voles, or wild birds. See [[District Level Licensing]] for full details.

---

## Gloucestershire Context

Developers working in Gloucestershire on sites with bat roosts, great crested newt ponds, otter habitat, or dormouse habitat will commonly need to engage with the EPS licensing process. Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records can provide species data to inform survey scoping. Communities scrutinising planning applications should check whether the applicant has addressed licence requirements — if not, this is a material gap in the application.
