# Conservation Covenants

A conservation covenant is a voluntary, legally binding agreement between a landowner and a "responsible body" that places obligations on the land to deliver conservation outcomes in perpetuity (or for an agreed term). Conservation covenants were introduced by the Environment Act 2021 and are one of the two main legal mechanisms for securing off-site Biodiversity Net Gain.

## Why They Were Created

Before conservation covenants, securing long-term habitat management on private land was difficult. Section 106 agreements only bind land connected to a planning permission. Conservation covenants fill that gap — they can bind any land, for conservation purposes, independently of any planning application.

## How They Work

1. A landowner agrees to create, restore, or manage habitat on their land
2. They enter into a legally binding agreement with a **responsible body**
3. The covenant is registered and runs with the land — binding future owners too
4. The responsible body monitors compliance and can enforce the agreement

The covenant typically specifies:
- What habitat must be created or maintained
- Management standards (e.g. no herbicide use, cutting regimes)
- Monitoring and reporting requirements
- Duration (minimum 30 years for BNG purposes)

## Responsible Bodies

A responsible body must be approved by the Secretary of State. They include:
- Natural England
- National Trust
- Wildlife trusts (including potentially Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust)
- Other conservation NGOs approved for this role

## Conservation Covenants and BNG

Conservation covenants are one of two ways to legally secure off-site BNG habitats (the other is a Section 106 agreement). For BNG purposes:
- The covenant must last at least **30 years**
- The land must be registered on the **Biodiversity Gain Site Register** (a public register maintained by Natural England)
- Once registered, the habitat units from that land can be sold to developers needing off-site BNG

This creates a market: landowners create habitats, register them, and sell habitat units to developers. The covenant ensures the habitat is maintained for the required period regardless of who owns the land.

## Comparison with S106 for BNG

| Conservation Covenant | Section 106 |
|----------------------|-------------|
| Any land — no connection to planning | Only land connected to a development |
| Runs with the land permanently | Connected to the planning permission |
| Responsible body monitors and enforces | LPA monitors and enforces |
| Used for habitat banks and off-site BNG | Used for on-site obligations and off-site BNG |
| Registered on Biodiversity Gain Site Register | Recorded on planning register |

## Gloucestershire Context

Conservation covenants provide a mechanism for GWT's Nature Recovery Zone landowners to create certified habitat banks. Developers needing off-site BNG in Gloucestershire can buy units from these registered sites, directing investment toward LNRS priority areas. This connects the [[Biodiversity Metric]], the Local Nature Recovery Strategy, and private land management into a functioning local nature market.
