# Cotswolds National Landscape Management Plan 2025–2030

Every five years the [[Cotswolds National Landscape Board]] publishes a plan setting out how the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] — England's largest protected landscape — should be looked after. This is the plan covering 2025 to 2030, and it is a **material consideration** in planning decisions affecting the area and its setting: councils have to take it into account. It spans 787 square miles across 15 local authority areas, and [[Gloucestershire County Council]] alone covers nearly two-thirds of it.

## What the plan is trying to do

It is organised around three big challenges — the climate emergency, the decline of nature, and changes to people's health and society — and works toward them through a set of policies on conserving the landscape and helping people understand and enjoy it. The headline point for development is the [[Seek to Further Duty]]: since December 2023, public bodies must actively *seek to further* the conservation and enhancement of the landscape, not merely avoid harming it. (In practice, take-up has been slow — only a small share of 2024 planning decisions properly addressed the duty.)

## What it means for development

A few points stand out for anyone working on a scheme in the Cotswolds:

- **Biodiversity Net Gain is doubled.** Development here must deliver **20% [[Biodiversity Net Gain]]**, not the national 10% minimum, and the usual avoid–mitigate–compensate order applies.
- **Irreplaceable habitats are off-limits.** Ancient and veteran trees, ancient woodland, long-established unimproved grassland, and ancient hedgerows can't be recreated and are protected accordingly.
- **The bar for major development is very high.** The Cotswolds has the strongest landscape protection in the planning system, on a par with National Parks, so major schemes engage the tough tests in the [[NPPF]] and the [[Major Development in AONBs]] rules — refusal unless there are exceptional circumstances in the public interest.
- **Local character matters.** Proposals are expected to fit the landscape, use local limestone, protect dark skies and tranquillity, and keep a buffer along watercourses with sustainable drainage.
- **Affordable housing is the priority.** Where housing does go ahead, the emphasis is firmly on affordable homes, reflecting how stretched local affordability has become (house prices run at around eleven times incomes).

## How it connects to other plans

The duty behind the plan comes from the [[CROW Act 2000]], strengthened by the [[Levelling Up and Regeneration Act 2023]]. Progress is tracked through the [[Protected Landscapes Targets and Outcomes Framework]], the area's nature ambitions are carried in the [[Cotswolds Nature Recovery Plan]] and the county [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy]], and where conservation and public enjoyment pull against each other the [[Sandford Principle]] puts conservation first. Parish and town councils are encouraged to lean on the plan when preparing neighbourhood plans and village design statements.
