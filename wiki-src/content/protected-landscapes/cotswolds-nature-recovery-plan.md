# Cotswolds Nature Recovery Plan

The Cotswolds Nature Recovery Plan (CNRP) is a guidance document adopted by the [[Cotswolds National Landscape Board]] in **October 2021**. It provides a detailed assessment of the habitats and species of the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] and sets out a framework for nature recovery across the CNL.

---

## Status

The CNRP is adopted as CNL Board guidance. It is not a statutory document in its own right, but it informs the statutory CNL Management Plan and has been used to influence all six Local Nature Recovery Strategies coincident with the CNL.

---

## Highest priority habitats

The CNRP identifies three highest priority habitats for the CNL:
1. **Veteran trees** — irreplaceable; cannot be recreated
2. **Ancient woodland** — continuously wooded since 1600; irreplaceable
3. **Ancient unimproved limestone grassland** — continuously unimproved since 1945; irreplaceable

These habitats are also classified as irreplaceable under CNL Management Plan Policy CE8, meaning that compensation alone cannot justify their loss in planning decisions.

## Priority habitats and species (Appendix 8 of CNL Management Plan)

The CNL Management Plan Appendix 8 lists priority habitats and species adapted from the S41 NERC Act 2006 list, with CNL-specific additions:

**Priority habitats:**
- Lowland mixed deciduous woodland
- Lowland beech and yew woodland
- Wood pasture, parkland, and veteran trees mosaic
- Wildflower-rich grasslands (calcareous, meadows, neutral)
- Flushes, streams, and rivers
- Arable field margins
- Hedgerows
- Natural box scrub
- Batscapes (CNL-specific addition)

**Priority species:**
- Farmland birds: skylark, lapwing, corn bunting
- Pasqueflower
- Pearl-bordered fritillary
- Arable plants
- Juniper
- Cotswold pennycress (CNL-specific)
- Bats
- Hazel dormouse
- Water vole
- Brown hare
- Limestone grassland butterflies
- Marsh fritillary
- Violet click beetle
- White-clawed crayfish
- Native brown trout
- Bath asparagus (CNL-specific)
- Common box (CNL-specific)
- Rockrose pot beetle
- Rugged oil beetle
- Ancient woodland ground flora (helleborines, angular Solomon's seal)
- Ancient grassland flora (musk orchid, fly orchid)

---

## Wildflower grassland loss

One of the most striking findings in the CNRP context is the dramatic decline of wildflower-rich grassland: from **40% of the CNL area in the 1930s** to **less than 1.5% today**, primarily due to agricultural intensification. This represents one of the most severe habitat losses in the CNL.

---

## Relationship to LNRSs and ELM

The CNRP has been used to influence all six Local Nature Recovery Strategies coincident with the CNL. Environmental Land Management (ELM) schemes operating within the CNL are expected to include measures from the CNRP. Supporting LNRS delivery demonstrates compliance with the biodiversity duty under the Environment Act 2021.

---

## Gloucestershire context

The CNRP is particularly relevant to the Gloucestershire parts of the CNL, which include significant areas of Cotswolds District Council, Stroud District Council, and Tewkesbury Borough Council. The plan's priority habitats and species are directly relevant to ecological surveys, planning applications, and Neighbourhood Development Plans within these areas.
