# Green Bridges

A green bridge is a purpose-built structure that carries vegetation — and the wildlife that depends on it — over a road, railway, or other linear barrier. Green bridges are a habitat connectivity measure used to reconnect fragmented wildlife populations that would otherwise be isolated by infrastructure.

---

## Purpose

Linear infrastructure (roads, railways, canals, fences) can sever wildlife corridors, preventing animals from moving between habitat patches. For species that depend on continuous habitat networks — such as hazel dormice, badgers, and amphibians — this fragmentation can reduce genetic diversity, prevent recolonisation after local extinction, and accelerate population decline.

A green bridge addresses this by providing a vegetated crossing point that animals will use as a natural extension of their habitat. Unlike a simple wildlife underpass or culvert, a green bridge carries the habitat itself — shrubs, trees, and ground vegetation — making it suitable for species that will not use bare concrete tunnels.

---

## Relevance to Hazel Dormice

Green bridges are explicitly named in Natural England's standing advice on [[Hazel Dormice in Planning]] as a mitigation measure. Dormice are particularly dependent on continuous hedgerow and woodland networks for movement — they rarely cross open ground. Where a development (particularly a road or large building footprint) would sever a hedgerow or woodland connection, a green bridge can maintain or restore that connectivity.

Key design requirements for dormouse-friendly green bridges:
- Sufficient width to carry dense shrub vegetation (typically at least 50 metres wide for effective dormouse use, though narrower structures may have some value)
- Planted with dormouse food plants: hazel, honeysuckle, bramble, and other fruiting shrubs
- Connected at both ends to existing hedgerow or woodland
- Managed to maintain dense shrub structure over time

---

## Relevance to Other Species

Green bridges benefit a range of species beyond dormice:

- **[[Badgers in Planning]]**: Badgers use green bridges where sett territories are bisected by roads; they are more likely to use wide, vegetated structures than narrow underpasses
- **Amphibians**: Wide green bridges with suitable ground cover can facilitate amphibian movement, including great crested newts
- **Invertebrates**: Continuous vegetation supports pollinator movement along habitat corridors
- **Small mammals**: Wood mice, voles, and shrews benefit from connected vegetation networks

---

## Connection to Green Infrastructure and Planning Policy

Green bridges are a form of Green Infrastructure — they deliver multiple functions simultaneously: habitat connectivity, landscape character, and potentially amenity value. They are relevant to:

- **Biodiversity Net Gain**: Green bridges can contribute to the habitat connectivity component of BNG assessments
- **Local Nature Recovery Strategy**: Connectivity between habitat patches is a core LNRS objective; green bridges can be identified as strategic connectivity features
- **Neighbourhood Development Plans**: NDPs covering parishes bisected by major roads could include policies requiring green bridges or equivalent connectivity measures for new infrastructure
- **[[Dark Corridors]]**: Green bridges that are unlit contribute to dark corridor networks for light-sensitive species

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire's road network — including the M5, A417, A40, and numerous rural A and B roads — fragments woodland and hedgerow habitats across the county. The Forest of Dean, Cotswold escarpment, and Severn Vale all contain habitats where road severance is a known issue for dormice and other species.

### A417 Missing Link — Gloucestershire Case Study

The **A417 Missing Link** scheme (between Birdlip and Cowley on the Cotswold escarpment) is a nationally significant infrastructure project where green bridges and habitat connectivity were central to the environmental assessment and scheme design.

The scheme passes through:
- The [[Cotswolds National Landscape]]
- Habitats supporting [[Hazel Dormice in Planning|hazel dormice]] (hedgerow and woodland on the escarpment)
- [[Bats in Planning|Bat]] commuting corridors (lesser and greater horseshoe bat territory)
- [[Cotswold Beechwoods SAC]] catchment

The EIA required green bridges and dedicated wildlife crossing structures to maintain dormouse, bat, and wider wildlife connectivity across the new road. These structures are designed to carry woody vegetation linking existing hedgerows on both sides of the carriageway — a practical example of green bridges as EPS mitigation at infrastructure scale.

The A417 is a useful reference case for NDP writers and communities arguing for connectivity measures on any major road scheme through the Cotswold escarpment.
