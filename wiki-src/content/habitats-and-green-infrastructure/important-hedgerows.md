# Important Hedgerows

The Hedgerows Regulations 1997 protect hedgerows that meet the criteria to be classified as "important." These hedgerows cannot be removed without first notifying the Local Planning Authority, which can issue a "hedgerow retention notice" preventing removal for up to two years.

## What Makes a Hedgerow "Important"

A hedgerow is important under the Regulations if it meets **at least one** of the criteria across two categories:

### Category A — Ecological Criteria
A hedgerow qualifies if it:
- Contains **7 or more woody species** in a 30-metre section (counted using the Botanical Society of Britain and Ireland method)
- Contains **3 or more woody species** and also features other ecological elements (e.g. is a woodland edge, contains a notable species)
- Provides a habitat for **listed species** including:
  - Bats (any species)
  - Dormice
  - Great crested newts (adjacent habitat)
  - Schedule 1 birds nesting within it
  - Protected plants (Schedule 8 of WCA 1981)
  - Red Data Book plant species
- Contains a **species-rich ditch** along its base
- Has **veteran hedgerow trees**
- Is part of a field system over 30 years old

### Category B — Historical and Landscape Criteria
A hedgerow qualifies if it:
- Marks a **pre-1850 parish or township boundary**
- Incorporates an **archaeological feature** (old wall, bank, ditch)
- Borders a **historic road or drove road**
- Marks the boundary of a **pre-1600 estate or park**
- Contains an **archaeological or historical feature** within its width (old wall stub, mound)
- Is recorded in **pre-1850 documents** as a boundary

## The Removal Notification Process

Before removing a hedgerow on land used for agriculture, forestry, or the keeping of horses, the landowner must:
1. **Submit a Hedgerow Removal Notice** to the LPA — minimum 42 days before intended removal
2. The LPA assesses whether the hedgerow meets the "important" criteria
3. If important: the LPA issues a **Hedgerow Retention Notice** — removal is prohibited for up to 2 years
4. If not important: the LPA confirms removal can proceed, or the 42-day period expires without a notice being issued

Removing a hedgerow without notification or in breach of a retention notice is a **criminal offence**.

## Development and Hedgerows

Where development proposals would remove hedgerows, planning conditions routinely require:
- A **hedgerow survey** using the Hedgerow Survey Handbook methodology before determination
- Identification of "important" hedgerows and assessment of whether removal is necessary
- Replacement of removed hedgerows on a length-for-length or greater basis
- Where species are present (dormice, hedgehogs, nesting birds), additional ecological constraints apply

The [[Biodiversity Metric]] includes a **hedgerow units account** — hedgerow loss must be compensated for in the BNG calculation separately from habitat units.

## Gloucestershire Context

Hedgerows are a characteristic feature of the Gloucestershire landscape — particularly in:
- The Severn Vale (ancient field patterns, species-rich mixed hedges)
- The Cotswolds (drystone wall and hedgerow boundaries)
- The Forest of Dean area (woodland edge and farm hedgerows)

The [[Local Nature Recovery Strategy|Gloucestershire LNRS (2026)]] identifies hedgerow restoration and management as a priority action. GWT's Nature Recovery Zones include hedgerow connectivity as a key landscape-scale goal.

Species particularly associated with hedgerows in Gloucestershire:
- **[[Hazel Dormice in Planning|Hazel dormice]]**: primary habitat; hedgerow connectivity is critical for population viability
- **[[Bats in Planning|Bats]]**: commuting routes along hedgerow lines; removal interrupts [[Dark Corridors]]
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning|Nesting birds]]**: yellowhammer, linnet, bullfinch; nesting season protections apply
- **[[Badgers in Planning|Badgers]]**: foraging routes and territorial corridors
