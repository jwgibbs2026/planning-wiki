# SuDS Runoff Destination Hierarchy

The SuDS Runoff Destination Hierarchy is the legally mandated priority order for where surface water must be discharged in new developments in England. It is set out in **Standard 1** of the national standards for [[SuDS]], published as statutory guidance by MHCLG and Defra.

---

## The Hierarchy

Developers must discharge surface water via the **highest feasible priority** option. Moving to a lower-priority option requires demonstrating that all higher options have been exhausted:

| Priority | Destination |
|----------|-------------|
| 1 (highest) | Collection for non-potable reuse (e.g., toilet flushing, irrigation) |
| 2 | Infiltration to ground (e.g., soakaways, permeable paving) |
| 3 | Discharge to above-ground surface water bodies (e.g., rivers, streams, ponds) |
| 4 | Discharge to surface water sewers |
| 5 (lowest) | Discharge to combined sewers |

---

## The Cost Principle

**Higher cost alone is not a valid reason to use a lower-priority destination.** This is an explicit requirement in the national standards. Developers must demonstrate that higher-priority options are genuinely not feasible — not merely more expensive — before moving down the hierarchy.

This is a significant constraint on developer decision-making and gives Local Planning Authorities (acting as SuDS Approving Bodies) clear grounds to reject drainage designs that default to sewer discharge without adequate justification.

---

## Practical Implications

- **Infiltration** (priority 2) requires ground investigation to confirm soil permeability and groundwater depth. The base of infiltration features must remain at least 1 metre above maximum groundwater levels.
- **Contaminated land** does not automatically disqualify higher-priority options — practicable remediation must be considered first.
- **Previously developed (brownfield) sites** may agree a relaxation of up to 5× greenfield runoff rates with evidence, but this applies to flow rates (Standard 3), not to the destination hierarchy itself.
- **Pumping** is permitted only by exception where gravity drainage is impossible — it cannot be used to justify a lower-priority destination.

---

## Connection to Other Standards

The hierarchy in Standard 1 sets the destination; the other standards govern how water is managed once the destination is determined:

- **Standard 2** governs everyday rainfall interception before any discharge occurs
- **Standard 3** governs flow rates and volumes for extreme events
- **Standard 4** governs water quality treatment before discharge
- **Standard 6** governs biodiversity outcomes from the drainage features used
