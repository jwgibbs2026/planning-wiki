# Calcareous and Neutral Grassland

Species-rich grassland is among the most threatened habitats in England. Both calcareous (limestone) and neutral grassland types are **Priority Habitats** under the NERC Act 2006 S41 list and are key targets for the Gloucestershire LNRS and [[agri-environment-schemes|agri-environment schemes]]. Gloucestershire holds some of the most important remaining areas of both types in England.

## Types of Species-Rich Grassland

### Calcareous Grassland (Limestone / Chalk Grassland)
Calcareous grassland develops on shallow, lime-rich soils over limestone or chalk. It is characterised by short, flower-rich swards grazed by sheep or cattle. It is one of the most biodiverse habitats in Europe — a single square metre can contain over 40 plant species.

- **NVC communities**: CG1–CG14 (calcicolous grassland); most important in Gloucestershire is **CG2** (*Festuca ovina-Avenula pratensis*) and **CG5** (*Bromus erectus* grassland)
- **Indicator species**: common rock-rose, horseshoe vetch, kidney vetch, salad burnet, stemless thistle, purple milk-vetch, autumn gentian, orchids (bee, fly, pyramidal, fragrant, common spotted, greater butterfly)
- **Butterfly species**: Adonis blue, chalkhill blue, small blue, Duke of Burgundy, dark green fritillary — many dependent on specific food plants only found in calcareous grassland
- **Gloucestershire importance**: the Cotswolds escarpment holds one of the most extensive and best-quality areas of calcareous grassland in England; sites include [[Rodborough Common SAC]], [[Calcareous and Neutral Grassland|Painswick Beacon]], and numerous undesignated road verges and field edges

### Neutral Grassland
Neutral grassland develops on neutral pH soils, typically clay loams in lowland England. Traditional hay meadows managed by late cutting (allowing wildflowers to set seed) before aftermath grazing are the classic type.

- **NVC communities**: **MG5** (*Cynosurus cristatus-Centaurea nigra* grassland — lowland meadow) is the most important; also MG1 (old grassland of variable quality) and MG4 (*Alopecurus pratensis-Sanguisorba officinalis* — floodplain meadow)
- **Indicator species**: yellow rattle, common knapweed, ox-eye daisy, meadow buttercup, ragged-robin, pepper saxifrage, greater burnet (in floodplain meadows), green-winged orchid
- **Gloucestershire importance**: the Severn and Avon Vales once supported vast areas of traditionally managed floodplain meadow; over 97% has been lost nationally since the 1930s; remaining fragments in Gloucestershire include Crickley Hill, North Meadow Cricklade (Wiltshire border, NNR), and scattered LWS meadows

## Why These Habitats Are Relevant to Planning

### As a Planning Constraint
Species-rich grassland, once lost, cannot be recreated on a meaningful timescale — it is effectively irreplaceable, like [[Ancient Woodland in Planning|ancient woodland]]. Planning policy therefore treats it with a high degree of protection:

- **[[NPPF]] paragraph 180**: requires planning decisions to minimise impacts on and provide net gains for biodiversity including by protecting and restoring priority habitats
- **SSSI**: the most important sites are designated SSSIs; some are also SACs (e.g. Rodborough Common SAC)
- **Local Wildlife Sites**: many county-level grassland sites are LWS; LPAs must give significant weight to their protection
- **[[Biodiversity Metric]]**: species-rich grassland scores very high on the distinctiveness scale (often "High" or "Very High"); losing even small areas creates a very large BNG deficit that is hard to offset

### Survey Requirements
Where development is proposed on or adjacent to unimproved or semi-improved grassland, an ecological survey should include:
- **Phase 1 Habitat Survey**: initial classification of grassland type
- **National Vegetation Classification (NVC) Survey**: required to determine whether grassland qualifies as a priority habitat; carried out by a specialist botanist during the growing season (May–July optimal); involves quadrat sampling and comparison to NVC communities
- **Protected species surveys**: calcareous grassland supports chalkhill blue, Adonis blue, and other butterflies; horseshoe vetch supports rare butterflies that may be present

### Mitigation
Where grassland loss cannot be avoided:
- **Translocation of turf** ("turf-lifting") is technically possible but considered a last resort — results are poor and it does not justify destruction of the source site
- **Creation of new species-rich grassland** takes decades to develop from scratch; it can contribute to the [[Biodiversity Metric]] at low distinctiveness initially, improving over time
- [[agri-environment-schemes|Countryside Stewardship Higher Tier]] is the main mechanism for long-term management of surviving grassland

## Grassland and the LNRS

The Gloucestershire LNRS (2026) identifies species-rich grassland restoration as a priority, particularly:
- Expansion and connectivity of calcareous grassland on the Cotswolds escarpment
- Restoration of floodplain meadow in the Severn and Avon Vales
- Linking existing fragments through habitat corridors in the agricultural landscape
