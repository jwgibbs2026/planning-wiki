# SuDS and Biodiversity

Standard 6 of the national standards for [[SuDS]] requires that drainage design actively creates biodiversity value — not merely avoids harm. This makes SuDS one of the most practical on-site mechanisms for delivering Biodiversity Net Gain and supporting Local Nature Recovery Strategies in new development.

---

## What Standard 6 Requires

Standard 6 sets out four core biodiversity obligations for SuDS design:

1. **Create diverse, resilient local ecosystems** contributing to net biodiversity gains
2. **Support local habitat and species** through nature recovery strategies — designs must align with the relevant Local Nature Recovery Strategy
3. **Conduct biodiversity risk and opportunity assessments** — proportionate to site sensitivity; distinct from standard ecological surveys, these assess both the risks drainage design poses to existing biodiversity and the opportunities it creates
4. **Follow the mitigation hierarchy**: avoid harm first, then mitigate, then compensate — the same hierarchy applied across all biodiversity planning contexts

---

## Integration with Biodiversity Net Gain

SuDS habitats can contribute toward mandatory Biodiversity Net Gain calculations. This is an explicit integration point in the national standards, consistent with the Environment Act 2021 BNG mandate (effective February 2024).

In practice, this means:
- A well-designed SuDS pond, wetland, or swale with diverse native vegetation can be entered into the BNG metric as a habitat unit
- This reduces the need for off-site BNG credits or habitat bank purchases
- Within the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]], where 20% BNG is required, SuDS habitats are particularly valuable as on-site BNG delivery mechanisms

The biodiversity value of SuDS features depends on design quality — species-rich marginal planting, varied water depths, and connections to wider habitat networks all increase metric scores.

---

## Habitat Connectivity

Standard 6 requires SuDS designs to ensure **habitat connectivity** — drainage features should not fragment existing wildlife corridors and should ideally enhance them. This connects SuDS design to:

- [[Dark Corridors]]: SuDS ponds, swales, and bioretention areas with dense vegetation can serve as dark, wildlife-friendly corridors for bats, amphibians, and other species
- Habitats and Ecosystems: SuDS wetland habitats can support priority species including great crested newts, water voles, and wetland birds
- Green Infrastructure: SuDS management trains are a primary GI delivery mechanism in new development

---

## Biodiversity Risk and Opportunity Assessment

The biodiversity risk and opportunity assessment required by Standard 6 is a proportionate, site-specific exercise. It is distinct from standard ecological surveys (which assess existing species and habitats) in that it specifically evaluates:

- **Risks**: could drainage features harm existing habitats or species? (e.g., altered hydrology affecting adjacent wetland, contaminated runoff reaching a watercourse)
- **Opportunities**: what biodiversity gains could drainage features deliver? (e.g., new pond habitat for GCN, wetland margins for water voles, diverse swale vegetation for pollinators)

The level of assessment should be proportionate to site sensitivity — a development adjacent to a [[SSSI]] requires more rigorous assessment than one on an urban brownfield site.

## Practical Examples

| SuDS Feature | Biodiversity Opportunity |
|---|---|
| Detention pond with varied margins | Great crested newt habitat; wetland birds; water voles |
| Swale with native wildflower margins | Pollinators; small mammals; foraging habitat for bats |
| Green roof | Nesting birds; invertebrates; urban biodiversity |
| Bioretention area with native planting | Invertebrates; foraging habitat; habitat connectivity |
| Permeable paving with grass joints | Ground-nesting invertebrates; minor connectivity |

## Connections for NDP Authors

Neighbourhood Development Plan authors can use Standard 6 to require biodiversity outcomes from drainage design in planning applications within their area. Specifically:
- Policies can require biodiversity risk and opportunity assessments for all drainage designs
- Policies can require SuDS features to be designed to contribute to BNG targets
- Policies can require alignment with the local LNRS priorities
- Standards 5 and 6 together give communities legitimate grounds to expect drainage that enhances the local environment, not merely manages water
