# Tree Preservation Orders

A Tree Preservation Order (TPO) is an order made by a Local Planning Authority to protect specific trees, groups of trees, or woodlands where it is in the public interest to do so. Carrying out works to a TPO-protected tree without consent is a criminal offence.

## What a TPO Protects

A TPO can cover:
- Individual trees
- Groups of trees
- Areas of trees (typically woodland)
- Woodlands (where the woodland as a whole is protected)

The order identifies the tree(s) precisely by location and species. Trees in Conservation Areas receive equivalent automatic protection without a formal TPO.

## What Requires Consent

For any TPO tree, you must apply to the LPA for consent before:
- **Cutting down** (felling) the tree
- **Topping or lopping** — removing branches or the crown
- **Uprooting**
- **Wilful damage or destruction**

Permitted development rights allow some minor works (e.g. removing dead branches) without consent.

## Who Can Make a TPO

Only the Local Planning Authority can make a TPO. They may do so at any time — proactively to protect valued trees, or reactively in response to a planning application that threatens trees. Natural England and the [[Forestry Commission]] do not make TPOs.

## TPOs and Planning Applications

Where a planning application involves works to or near TPO trees, the LPA must consider:
- Whether the proposed works would harm the tree's health, form, or longevity
- Whether the tree can be retained and protected during construction
- Arboricultural impact assessment (following BS 5837: Trees in relation to design, demolition and construction)

An **Arboricultural Impact Assessment (AIA)** and **Tree Protection Plan** are standard requirements for applications affecting trees.

## Conservation Area Trees

Trees in Conservation Areas that are not covered by a TPO still have protection. Anyone wishing to carry out works must give the LPA **six weeks' written notice**, allowing time to make a TPO if the tree warrants it.

## Ancient and Veteran Trees

[[Ancient and Veteran Trees]] have their own, stronger layer of protection through the NPPF and Natural England/Forestry Commission standing advice, regardless of whether a TPO exists. A TPO adds a further layer of statutory protection, but the planning policy protection for ancient trees applies independently.

## Penalties

Carrying out works to a TPO tree without consent, or failing to comply with conditions on a consent, can result in:
- An unlimited fine on conviction
- A requirement to replace the tree

## Gloucestershire Context

All six Gloucestershire LPAs maintain registers of TPO trees. Significant trees — particularly ancient oaks, veteran field trees, and mature street trees — are commonly protected. TPO registers can be searched through each council's online planning portal or by contacting the council's arboriculture or tree officer.
