# SuDS — Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems

Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SuDS) are drainage approaches that mimic natural water management — handling surface water runoff at or near its source, close to the surface, rather than routing it underground through traditional pipe systems. In England, SuDS are **mandatory** for all new developments requiring planning permission (excluding trunk roads and retrofits). This is not optional best practice; it is a statutory requirement enforced through the planning system.

The national standards are set out in statutory guidance published by MHCLG and Defra. Local Planning Authorities act as SuDS Approving Bodies and must assess compliance. [[Lead Local Flood Authorities]] provide flood risk and drainage information that developers must engage with.

---

## Why SuDS Matter

Traditional drainage routes surface water rapidly into sewers and watercourses, increasing flood risk, degrading water quality, and eliminating the ecological and social value of water in the landscape. SuDS slow, store, clean, and use water in ways that deliver multiple co-benefits: flood management, water quality improvement, biodiversity habitat, amenity, and climate resilience. These co-benefits are not optional add-ons — they are separate numbered standards with their own requirements.

## The Seven National Standards

### Standard 1: Runoff Destination Hierarchy

Surface water must discharge via the highest feasible priority:

1. Collection for non-potable reuse *(highest priority)*
2. Infiltration to ground
3. Discharge to above-ground surface water bodies
4. Discharge to surface water sewers
5. Discharge to combined sewers *(lowest priority)*

**Higher cost alone is not a valid reason to use a lower-priority destination.** Developers must demonstrate all higher options have been exhausted before moving down the hierarchy. This is a significant constraint on developer decision-making.

### Standard 2: Everyday Rainfall Management (Interception)

Systems must manage at least the first 5 mm of rainfall without runoff to surface waters or piped systems. Seasonal targets:

- **80% interception**: May–October (summer)
- **50% interception**: November–April (winter)

Acceptable methods include green roofs, rainwater harvesting, permeable surfaces, swales, bioretention areas, and detention basins.

### Standard 3: Extreme Rainfall and Flooding

- Protect on-site development from drainage system flooding
- For **50% AEP** (1-in-2 year) events: limit discharge to greenfield rates or 3 litres/second/hectare (whichever is greater)
- For **1% AEP** (1-in-100 year) events: controlled discharge matching greenfield conditions
- Climate change must be designed for using the [[Environment Agency]]'s current Upper End Allowance factors — this is required, not optional

**Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP)** is the statistical measure of flood event frequency: 50% AEP = a flood likely in any given year; 1% AEP = a 1-in-100-year flood.

**Greenfield runoff rate** is the natural pre-development discharge rate used as the design benchmark. The minimum floor is 3 litres/second/hectare.

### Standard 4: Water Quality

- Robust water quality risk assessments required
- Management trains must address diffuse particulates and chemicals
- Pre-treatment required for infiltration systems
- High-risk land uses (e.g., petrol stations, industrial sites) may need proprietary treatment systems or discharge to foul sewers

### Standard 5: Amenity

- Designs must contribute to placemaking and environmental enhancement
- Vegetation must be included to combat urban heat island effects
- Support for walking, cycling, recreation, and informal play where feasible
- Educational signage explaining flood functions and environmental benefits is encouraged

### Standard 6: Biodiversity

SuDS must create diverse, resilient local ecosystems contributing to net biodiversity gains. Key requirements:

- **Biodiversity risk and opportunity assessments** required, proportionate to site sensitivity
- Follow the **mitigation hierarchy**: avoid harm first, then mitigate, then compensate
- **SuDS habitats can contribute toward mandatory Biodiversity Net Gain calculations** — this is an explicit integration point between drainage infrastructure and biodiversity policy
- Designs must support Local Nature Recovery Strategies and habitat connectivity
- Species protection measures must be integrated

This standard means that well-designed SuDS ponds, swales, and bioretention areas can count toward the mandatory 10% BNG requirement (or 20% within the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]]).

### Standard 7: Construction, Operation, Maintenance, and Structural Integrity

- Management and maintenance plans must support all other standards throughout the development's lifetime
- Designers must assess failure scenarios and risks
- Structural components must have a minimum design life matching the development lifetime
- Maintenance access required for all personnel, vehicles, and equipment
- Monitoring provisions must be agreed with approving bodies for larger developments

## Design Principles

### Natural Water Management

SuDS should mimic natural drainage — managing runoff at or near source, as close to the surface as practicable. This avoids traditional underground pipe conveyance and delivers multiple environmental and social co-benefits.

### Early and Integrated Design

Surface water management must be considered from the earliest site appraisal and integrated with:
- Existing topography, hydrology, and watercourses
- Road and building layouts
- Biodiversity and Green Infrastructure delivery
- Climate resilience planning

Leaving SuDS design until late in the planning process is a common mistake that leads to poor outcomes and compliance failures.

### SuDS Management Train

The most effective approach is a **series of drainage features** operating near the runoff source, working together to control flow rates and volumes, provide water quality benefits, encourage biodiversity, and integrate into landscape design. Features might include: source control (green roofs, permeable paving), conveyance (swales, filter strips), and storage/treatment (detention basins, constructed wetlands, ponds).

## Regulatory Framework

**Planning**: Applicants must demonstrate compliance with national standards from conceptual planning stages. LPAs act as SuDS Approving Bodies. Early engagement with LPAs helps identify local strategies and requirements.

**Adoption and Maintenance**: Appropriate bodies must adopt and maintain surface water systems serving multiple properties. Since **April 2020**, sewerage companies can adopt SuDS under Ofwat's Code for Adoption Agreements.

**Building Regulations**: Approved Document H, Section H3 applies within property curtilages.

**Environmental Permits**: Required under Environmental Permitting Regulations for discharges containing pollutants. Clean rainwater from roofs and small hardstandings may be exempt if treated.

**Lead Local Flood Authorities**: Developers must engage [[Lead Local Flood Authorities]] for flood risk and drainage information, including Local Flood Risk Management Strategies and Strategic Flood Risk Assessments.

---

## Key Technical Notes

- **Pumping**: Permitted only by exception where gravity drainage is impossible
- **Infiltration depths**: Base of infiltration features must remain 1 metre above maximum groundwater levels
- **Previously developed (brownfield) sites**: A relaxation of up to 5× greenfield runoff rates may be agreed with evidence — but this is an exception, not a default
- **Contaminated land**: Higher-priority destinations should not be discounted where practicable remediation is possible

## Scope

- Applies to all new developments requiring planning permission in England
- **Excludes**: trunk roads and retrofit projects
- Applies to England only

## Connections to Other Planning Topics

**Biodiversity Net Gain**: SuDS habitats (ponds, wetlands, swales with diverse vegetation) can contribute toward the mandatory 10% BNG requirement. This makes SuDS one of the most practical on-site BNG delivery mechanisms available to developers.

**Green Infrastructure**: SuDS management trains are one of the most concrete GI delivery mechanisms in new development. Swales, ponds, and bioretention areas serve as multi-functional green spaces.

**[[Dark Corridors]]**: SuDS features — particularly ponds, swales, and bioretention areas with dense vegetation — can serve as dark, wildlife-friendly corridors for bats, amphibians, and other species.

**Building with Nature**: GWT's Building with Nature standards overlap significantly with SuDS amenity and biodiversity standards. Both frameworks require integrated, multi-functional design from the outset.

**Local Nature Recovery Strategy**: Standard 6 explicitly requires SuDS to support local nature recovery strategies — giving LNRSs a direct hook into drainage design for new development.

**Neighbourhood Development Plan**: NDP authors can reference Standards 5 and 6 to require amenity and biodiversity outcomes from drainage design in planning applications within their area. These standards give communities legitimate grounds to expect more than functional drainage.

## Gloucestershire Context

- **[[Gloucestershire County Council]]** is the Lead Local Flood Authority for Gloucestershire. Developers must engage with the county council for flood risk and drainage information, including the Local Flood Risk Management Strategy and Strategic Flood Risk Assessments.
- Gloucestershire has significant flood risk areas, particularly in the Severn Vale and around Gloucester, Tewkesbury, and the lower Avon catchment. SuDS design in these areas must use the EA's Upper End Allowance climate change factors.
- The [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] requires 20% BNG for new developments. SuDS habitats contributing to BNG calculations are therefore particularly valuable in CNL planning applications.
- Stroud District Council has produced Recreation Mitigation Strategies for Rodborough Common and Cotswold Beechwoods — SuDS water quality standards are relevant to development near these sensitive sites.
- Whether individual Gloucestershire LPAs have published local SuDS strategies or supplementary planning documents is not confirmed; developers should engage early with the relevant LPA and [[Gloucestershire County Council]] as LLFA.
