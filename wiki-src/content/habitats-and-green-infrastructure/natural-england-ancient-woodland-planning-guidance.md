# Natural England — Ancient Woodland Planning Guidance

When a development could affect ancient woodland or old trees, [[Natural England]] and the [[Forestry Commission]] publish joint standing advice that the local planning authority must take into account. This page explains it plainly; see [[Ancient Woodland in Planning]] and [[Ancient and Veteran Trees]] for the fuller picture.

## The idea at the heart of it: irreplaceability

Ancient woodland, ancient trees, and veteran trees have built up over centuries and **cannot be recreated**. You can't move them and you can't replace them, so no amount of compensation, on its own, makes their loss acceptable. That single point drives everything else.

## A high bar for permission

Because these habitats are irreplaceable, permission for development that would harm them should be **refused unless there are wholly exceptional reasons** *and* a suitable compensation strategy is in place. It is deliberately a hard test to meet.

## Keeping a safe distance

The advice sets out buffers to protect what's there:

- at least **15 metres** from the edge of ancient woodland; and
- for an individual tree, **15 times the trunk's diameter, or 5 metres beyond the canopy** — whichever is greater.

## Both direct and indirect harm count

It isn't only about chopping things down. Cutting woodland off from other habitat, pollution, disturbance, and changes to how nearby land is managed all matter just as much, and all have to be assessed. The evidence needs to include a proper BS 5837 tree survey, not just a desk study.

## Gloucestershire relevance

This is very much a Gloucestershire issue. The **Forest of Dean** is one of England's largest ancient woodlands, the **Cotswold escarpment** holds important fragments, and ancient and veteran trees are scattered through hedgerows, parkland, and old orchards across all six districts.
