# Green Infrastructure

Green Infrastructure (GI) is a network of multi-functional green and blue spaces and features — both urban and rural — that delivers benefits for people, nature, and climate simultaneously.

## What Counts as Green Infrastructure?

GI includes a wide range of features:
- Parks, playing fields, and public open spaces
- Woodlands, hedgerows, and street trees
- Wildflower meadows, grasslands, and nature reserves
- River corridors, ponds, and wetlands (sometimes called "blue infrastructure")
- Green roofs and living walls
- Allotments and community gardens
- Sustainable urban drainage ([[SuDS]]) features — rain gardens, permeable paving, swales
- Footpaths, cycle routes, and green corridors
- Local Green Spaces protected through planning

The defining feature of GI is that it is **multi-functional** — a single feature can provide multiple benefits at once.

## Benefits of Green Infrastructure

| Benefit | Examples |
|---------|---------|
| Nature recovery | Habitat creation and connectivity for the Nature Recovery Network |
| Climate adaptation | Shade, cooling, flood reduction through [[SuDS]] |
| Physical health | Active travel, accessible outdoor space |
| Mental health | Proximity to nature, reduced urban stress |
| Food production | Allotments, orchards, community gardens |
| Air quality | Trees filtering particulates |
| Economic | Increased property values, tourism, reduced flood damage costs |

## GI in Planning Policy

The [[NPPF]] requires Local Plans to plan for a network of multi-functional GI. Local Plans across Gloucestershire include GI policies, and NDPs can add local detail.

Biodiversity Net Gain delivery often takes the form of GI creation or enhancement.

## Standards and Frameworks

Two key frameworks guide high-quality GI delivery:
- Building with Nature — 12 standards developed by Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust and the University of the West of England
- Natural England GI Framework — 15 principles for planning, creating, and managing GI
