# Habitats and Ecosystems

Habitats are the physical environments in which species live. In planning, the quality, extent, and connectivity of habitats are central to biodiversity assessment, Biodiversity Net Gain calculations, and the protection of [[European Protected Species]] and [[Species of Principal Importance]]. Habitat type is often the primary trigger for ecological survey requirements — the presence of suitable habitat can require survey even in the absence of confirmed species records.

---

## Habitat Types and Survey Triggers

### Ancient Woodland

Ancient woodland (continuously wooded since 1600 AD) is the most legally protected habitat category in the English planning system. It is classified as **irreplaceable** — it cannot be recreated, and compensation alone cannot justify approval of development that would harm it. Ancient woodland is found across the Forest of Dean, Cotswolds, and Severn Vale in Gloucestershire.

Survey trigger: Any development within or adjacent to ancient woodland. See [[Ancient Woodland in Planning]] for full detail including buffer zone requirements.

### Woodland (Non-Ancient)

Broadleaved and mixed woodland supports a wide range of protected species. Survey triggers include:

- **[[Hazel Dormice in Planning]]**: Woodland is a primary survey trigger habitat; dormice favour structurally diverse woodland with a shrub layer
- **[[Bats in Planning]]**: Woodland provides roost sites (in trees) and foraging/commuting habitat
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Woodland supports nesting birds; clearance during March–August requires particular care
- **[[Badgers in Planning]]**: Woodland is prime badger sett habitat

### Hedgerows

Hedgerows are critical linear habitat features connecting woodland patches and supporting movement of many species. They are protected under the Hedgerows Regulations 1997 where they meet criteria for importance. Survey triggers include:

- **[[Hazel Dormice in Planning]]**: Hedgerows are a primary survey trigger; dormice depend on continuous hedgerow networks for movement
- **[[Bats in Planning]]**: Hedgerows are key commuting routes; removal can sever dark corridors
- **[[Water Voles in Planning]]**: Hedgerows adjacent to watercourses can support water voles
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Hedgerows support nesting birds
- **[[Badgers in Planning]]**: Badgers use hedgerow bases for movement

### Scrub

Transitional scrub habitat — particularly with hazel, honeysuckle, bramble, and other fruiting shrubs — is important for:

- **[[Hazel Dormice in Planning]]**: Scrub is a primary survey trigger habitat; dormice forage extensively in scrub
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Scrub supports nesting birds including warblers and finches
- **[[Badgers in Planning]]**: Scrub can conceal sett entrances

### Riparian Habitats (Rivers, Streams, Wetlands)

Riparian habitats — the banks, margins, floodplains, and vegetation associated with rivers, streams, ditches, lakes, and other water bodies — are among the most ecologically important and legally sensitive habitats in Gloucestershire. They support multiple protected species and can trigger survey requirements for several species simultaneously. Survey triggers include:

- **[[Otters in Planning]]**: Any development within 250–500 metres of a river, stream, lake, or marsh requires otter survey
- **[[Water Voles in Planning]]**: Slow-flowing or still water with soft earth banks and dense bankside vegetation triggers water vole survey
- **[[Bats in Planning]]**: Watercourses are key bat foraging and commuting routes; riparian trees may contain roost features
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Riparian habitats support kingfishers, grey herons, reed warblers, and other species

Riparian habitats also support badgers where woodland or scrub adjoins watercourses. See [[Badgers in Planning]].

#### Water vole habitat indicators

Water voles have specific habitat requirements within the broader riparian zone:

- Soft earth banks alongside watercourses — suitable for burrow construction
- Slow-flowing or still water (rivers, streams, ditches, ponds, canals)
- Dense bankside vegetation (rushes, sedges, grasses) providing food and cover
- Predator escape areas — vegetation structure allowing rapid retreat from mink and herons

#### Key watercourses in Gloucestershire

- **River Severn** — the county's main river; extensive floodplain and riparian corridor; otters, water voles, bats
- **River Avon** — southern Gloucestershire
- **Rivers Churn, Coln, Leach, Windrush** — Cotswolds catchments; chalk-fed, high water quality
- **River Leadon** — Forest of Dean and western Gloucestershire
- **Severn Vale drainage network** — extensive ditch and rhyne system

The connectivity of these watercourses makes them important not only for individual species but as Green Infrastructure corridors linking habitats across the county.

### Grassland

Unimproved and semi-improved grassland — including lowland meadows and calcareous grassland — supports invertebrates, small mammals, reptiles, and the birds that feed on them. Lowland meadows have severely declined nationally and are important in the Severn Vale and Cotswolds. Calcareous grassland on the Cotswolds escarpment supports rare plants and invertebrates. Survey triggers include:

- **[[Bats in Planning]]**: Grassland supports foraging bats, particularly pipistrelles
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Grassland supports ground-nesting birds and farmland birds
- **[[Great Crested Newts in Planning]]**: Rough grassland adjacent to ponds can be GCN terrestrial habitat

### Ponds and Wetlands

Standing water bodies are critical for amphibians and support a wide range of other species. The Cotswold Water Park is a significant wetland complex. Survey triggers include:

- **[[Great Crested Newts in Planning]]**: Any pond within 500 metres of a development site triggers GCN survey assessment; ponds within 250 metres require eDNA or population survey
- **[[Bats in Planning]]**: Ponds are key bat foraging sites
- **[[Otters in Planning]]**: Lakes and marshland are otter survey triggers
- **[[Water Voles in Planning]]**: Still or slow-moving water bodies with suitable bankside habitat

### Buildings and Structures

Older buildings, barns, bridges, and other structures can support:

- **[[Bats in Planning]]**: Roosts in roof spaces, behind cladding, in gaps in masonry
- **[[Wild Birds in Planning]]**: Nesting birds including swallows, house martins, and barn owls
- **[[Barn Owls in Planning]]**: Old farm buildings are a primary survey trigger

## Habitat Connectivity

Habitat fragmentation — the disconnection of wildlife populations through development — is a key harm type across multiple species. Maintaining and enhancing habitat connectivity is a core principle of the mitigation hierarchy for:

- **Water voles** — linear riparian territories are particularly vulnerable to fragmentation
- **Otters** — depend on continuous riparian corridors
- **Bats** — require dark, unlit commuting corridors
- **Dormice** — depend on continuous hedgerow and woodland networks for movement
- **Badgers** — territorial connectivity across landscapes

The Local Nature Recovery Strategy for Gloucestershire (when published) will identify priority areas for habitat connectivity enhancement, providing a spatial framework for planning decisions.

## Habitats and BNG

Biodiversity Net Gain requires a 10% measurable improvement in biodiversity value. The BNG Metric assigns habitat condition scores that reflect the quality of existing habitats on a development site. High-quality habitats (e.g. ancient woodland, species-rich grassland) score highly and are harder to offset — reinforcing the principle that irreplaceable habitats should be avoided entirely.

## Gloucestershire Priority Habitats

Gloucestershire contains significant areas of nationally important habitat:

- **Forest of Dean** — one of England's largest ancient woodland complexes; also supports dormice, bats, otters, and breeding birds
- **Cotswold escarpment** — ancient woodland fragments, calcareous grassland, and hedgerow networks; important for dormice, bats, and reptiles
- **Severn Vale** — extensive riparian and wetland habitats along the Severn and its tributaries; key for otters, water voles, and wading birds; also supports great crested newts in pond-rich areas
- **Cotswold Water Park** — extensive wetland and open water; important for wintering and breeding birds, great crested newts, and otters
- **Hedgerow networks** — particularly in the Cotswolds and Forest of Dean fringe; critical for dormice and bat commuting

The Gloucestershire Wildlife Trust and Gloucestershire Centre for Environmental Records (GCER) are the primary sources of local habitat and species data.
