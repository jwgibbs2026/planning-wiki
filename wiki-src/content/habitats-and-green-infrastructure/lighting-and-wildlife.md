# Lighting and Wildlife

Artificial light at night (ALAN) is a significant and often underestimated threat to wildlife. It disrupts the behaviour, navigation, feeding, and reproduction of numerous species. In planning, lighting impacts must be considered for any development that introduces or intensifies artificial lighting near ecologically sensitive features.

## Why Lighting Matters for Wildlife

Most ecologically sensitive species in Gloucestershire are negatively affected by artificial lighting:

| Species | Impact of artificial lighting |
|---------|------------------------------|
| **Bats** | Disrupts commuting routes along [[Dark Corridors]]; slower species (lesser horseshoe, greater horseshoe) avoid lit areas entirely; fast species may exploit lit areas to prey on moths but displaces commuting activity |
| **Invertebrates** | Moths and other nocturnal insects are disoriented and killed by light traps; widespread population-level effect |
| **Otters** | Avoid lit watercourses; lighting of bridges and culverts can sever otter movement routes |
| **Dormice** | Light disrupts emergence from hibernation and foraging activity |
| **Nesting birds** | [[Wild Birds in Planning]] near streetlights begin singing earlier, disrupting breeding behaviour; waders and wildfowl disturbed at night |
| **Badgers** | Avoid lit areas near setts; lighting near sett entrances is a mitigation requirement |
| **Amphibians** | Disrupts breeding migration; GCN and common toads affected by lit roads and paths |

## Planning Policy

### NPPF
The [[NPPF]] (paragraph 191) requires planning decisions to limit the impact of light pollution on "local amenity, intrinsically dark landscapes and nature." LPAs must consider lighting impacts as a material planning consideration.

### Dark Sky Policy
Several of Gloucestershire's LPAs and the [[Cotswolds National Landscape]] have specific dark sky or lighting policies. The Cotswolds National Landscape has a particularly strong commitment to dark skies as part of its special character — artificial lighting is a sensitive issue for any development within or adjacent to its boundary.

### Bat-Specific Requirements
Natural England's bat standing advice (and the [[Bats in Planning]] guidance) is explicit: lighting of bat commuting routes, roost access points, or foraging habitat requires careful mitigation. Common conditions include:
- No lighting of bat roost access points
- Directional, shielded lighting to prevent light spill onto [[Dark Corridors]]
- Low colour temperature (warm white, <2700K) to reduce invertebrate attraction
- Motion-sensor activation rather than continuous lighting

## The Mitigation Hierarchy for Lighting

1. **Avoid**: locate lit areas away from ecologically sensitive features; retain dark corridors unlit
2. **Reduce**: minimise lux levels; direct light downward; use shields and hoods; time-limit lighting
3. **Compensate**: where some lighting is unavoidable, enhance connectivity elsewhere

## Institute of Lighting Professionals (ILP) Guidance

The ILP's *Guidance Note 08/23: Bats and Artificial Lighting in the UK* is the professional standard for assessing and mitigating lighting impacts on bats. Ecologists and lighting designers use this in combination to produce **Lighting Impact Assessments** and **Lighting Design Strategies** for planning applications.

## Gloucestershire Context

Gloucestershire has significant areas of intrinsically dark landscape — the Forest of Dean, the Cotswolds escarpment, and the Severn Vale are all important for dark-sky dependent species. Key concerns:
- **Lesser and greater horseshoe bats**: both present in Gloucestershire; highly sensitive to lighting; any development near known roosts requires specialist lighting assessment
- **River corridors**: the Severn, Wye, Avon, and tributaries are important otter corridors — bridge lighting is a recurring planning issue
- **Cotswolds NL**: dark sky protection is part of the statutory management plan for the landscape

When responding to planning applications near ecologically sensitive areas, artificial lighting proposals should always be checked and challenged if inadequate.
